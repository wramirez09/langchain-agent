MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Epidural steroid injections (ESI) — including transforaminal (TFESI), interlaminar (ILESI), and caudal (CESI) approaches — deliver corticosteroid (with or without local anesthetic) into the epidural space to reduce inflammation, relieve radicular pain, and restore function. These procedures are intended for short-to-intermediate-term pain relief in patients with radicular or radiculopathic symptoms and are not a substitute for definitive surgical management when surgery is otherwise indicated.

For any ESI approach to be considered medically necessary, ALL of the following general criteria must be met:

-   Radicular pain or radiculopathy with a dermatomal or myotomal pattern consistent with the targeted spinal level, OR neurogenic claudication from spinal stenosis with symptoms correlating to the region to be injected.
-   Advanced imaging (MRI preferred; CT myelogram acceptable when MRI is contraindicated) demonstrating pathology — such as disc herniation, foraminal stenosis, central canal stenosis, or spondylotic foraminal narrowing — that correlates anatomically with the patient's reported symptoms and clinical examination findings.
-   Pain causing functional disability, documented with objective findings on physical examination (e.g., positive straight-leg raise, dermatomal sensory deficit, diminished reflexes, motor weakness) and a reported average pain level of 6 or greater on a 0–10 numeric rating scale, OR documented interference with activities of daily living (ADLs) or occupational function.
-   Failure of at least 4–6 weeks of appropriate active conservative care, which must include at minimum two of the following unless contraindicated and documented:
    -   Supervised physical therapy or a documented structured home exercise program (HEP) with provider instruction.
    -   Oral analgesic or anti-inflammatory therapy at appropriate doses (NSAIDs, acetaminophen, or a short course of oral corticosteroids).
    -   Activity modification and self-management education.
    -   Exception: conservative care requirement may be shortened or waived when the patient presents with rapidly progressive neurologic deficit (new or worsening motor weakness, bowel/bladder dysfunction) or severe functionally disabling pain; the clinical rationale must be documented in the medical record.
-   Image guidance (fluoroscopy or CT) required for all transforaminal injections and strongly recommended (considered medically necessary by most payers) for all caudal and interlaminar injections; blind (landmark-guided) epidural injections without image guidance are generally not covered.
-   No contraindications present (see LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS).

INDICATIONS

Initial Injection

An initial ESI is indicated when ALL general medical necessity criteria above are met and the procedure is consistent with the patient's overall plan of care. Specific indication categories include:

-   Lumbar or cervical radiculopathy due to disc herniation (HNP) or foraminal stenosis:
    -   MRI or CT myelogram showing disc herniation or foraminal narrowing at a level and side consistent with the dermatomal pattern of pain, paresthesias, or weakness.
    -   Clinical examination corroborating nerve root involvement (positive tension signs, dermatomal sensory loss, reflex change, or motor deficit).
    -   Symptoms present for at least 2–4 weeks with insufficient improvement despite conservative measures; may be expedited for severe or rapidly worsening deficits.
-   Neurogenic claudication due to spinal stenosis:
    -   MRI or CT myelogram showing central or lateral recess stenosis correlating with bilateral or unilateral lower-extremity symptoms that are positionally exacerbated (worse with standing/walking, improved with flexion).
    -   Documented functional limitation (e.g., reduced walking distance, difficulty with stairs or prolonged standing).
    -   Failure of conservative care as above; caudal or interlaminar approaches are commonly used for central stenosis.
-   Thoracic radiculopathy:
    -   MRI or CT demonstrating thoracic disc herniation, foraminal stenosis, or other compressive pathology at a level consistent with the patient's dermatomal distribution.
    -   Interlaminar approach with fluoroscopic guidance required; thoracic transforaminal injections carry elevated risk and require documented specialist-level consideration.
-   Post-laminectomy or failed back surgery syndrome with ongoing radicular component:
    -   Prior operative reports and current imaging documenting a structural correlate (e.g., epidural fibrosis, recurrent or residual stenosis, adjacent-level disease) consistent with the symptomatic distribution.

Repeat Injection

Repeat ESI after a prior injection in the same spinal region is considered medically necessary only when ALL of the following are met:

-   The prior injection in the same region produced clinically meaningful benefit, defined as:
    -   At least 50% reduction in pain intensity (based on NRS/VAS) lasting a minimum of 4 weeks, OR
    -   Documented meaningful functional improvement (return to activity, improved ADLs, reduced analgesic use, improved physical therapy participation) persisting for at least 4 weeks.
-   Symptoms have recurred or partially recurred after the documented period of improvement; the patient has not returned to pre-injection baseline pain levels without any benefit.
-   General medical necessity criteria (imaging correlation, functional disability, absence of contraindications) continue to be met at the time of the repeat request.
-   The request is consistent with the applicable frequency limitations:
    -   Lumbar or caudal region: no more than 3 injections per 6-month period (per spinal region); some payers limit to 3–4 per 12 months.
    -   Cervical or thoracic region: no more than 3 injections per 6-month period (per spinal region).
    -   A minimum interval of 2 weeks between injections in the same region is generally required; some payers require a minimum of 4 weeks.
-   If no clinically meaningful benefit was achieved after 2 consecutive injections in the same region, additional injections in that region are generally not supported unless there has been a change in diagnosis, new imaging, or documented change in clinical circumstances.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

All prior authorization requests for ESI must include the following; incomplete submissions will result in delays or non-certification.

Imaging

-   MRI or CT myelogram report (preferably within the past 12 months for elective requests; older imaging is acceptable if clinical status is unchanged and documented as such) demonstrating pathology at the level(s) to be injected.
-   Imaging must show a structural abnormality (disc herniation, foraminal stenosis, central stenosis, spondylosis) that correlates with the patient's laterality and dermatomal distribution.
-   Plain radiographs alone are not sufficient to establish medical necessity for ESI.
-   If updated imaging is not available for a repeat request, the treating provider must document that the patient's clinical status is consistent with the previously confirmed structural diagnosis and that no new indications for imaging exist.

Conservative Care

-   Documentation of the type, frequency, duration, and provider of conservative care rendered (physical therapy records, HEP instruction notes, medication trials with dosing).
-   If conservative care was abbreviated or waived due to clinical urgency (progressive motor deficit, severe functional impairment), explicit documentation of the clinical rationale is required.
-   Duration of conservative treatment (minimum 4–6 weeks) and the patient's response or lack of sufficient response must be clearly stated.

Clinical Examination and Symptom Documentation

-   Recent history and physical examination (within 60 days of request) documenting:
    -   Pain location, quality, radiation pattern, and numeric pain rating (NRS 0–10).
    -   Relevant neurologic findings (motor strength, sensation, reflexes, tension signs such as straight-leg raise or Spurling test).
    -   Functional impact on ADLs, work, or ambulation.
-   Any prior diagnostic workup relevant to the request (EMG/NCS results if performed).

Prior Injection Response (for Repeat Requests)

-   Procedure note from the prior injection including date, approach, level(s) injected, agent and dose administered, and use of image guidance.
-   Post-injection follow-up note documenting the degree and duration of pain relief (percentage improvement, duration in weeks) and any functional gains or adverse effects.
-   If the prior injection produced less than 50% relief or relief lasting fewer than 4 weeks, the request for an additional injection must include clinical justification (e.g., partial but meaningful functional benefit, change in technique or level planned).

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Axial low back pain or neck pain without a radicular or radiculopathic component: ESI is not indicated for purely axial or mechanical pain without clinical and imaging evidence of nerve root irritation or compressive pathology. Facet-mediated or myofascial pain should be addressed with appropriate alternative interventions.
-   Absence of correlating imaging pathology: ESI is not covered when imaging does not demonstrate a structural abnormality consistent with the patient's symptoms, or when imaging findings exist at a level that does not correlate with the clinical presentation.
-   Injections without image guidance: Transforaminal ESI without fluoroscopic or CT guidance is considered not medically necessary. Interlaminar and caudal injections performed without image guidance are generally not covered by commercial payers; documentation of image guidance confirmation of needle placement is expected in the procedure report.
-   Exceeding frequency limits: Injections that exceed the applicable per-region, per-period limits (typically 3 per spinal region per 6-month period) are not covered absent extraordinary clinical circumstances documented in writing.
-   No documented response to prior injections: If two injections in the same spinal region have produced no clinically meaningful benefit (less than 50% pain relief, no functional improvement, duration of benefit less than 4 weeks), further injections in that region are not covered unless there is new imaging, a change in diagnosis, or a documented change in clinical management plan.
-   Failure to complete or adequately trial conservative care without documented contraindication or urgent clinical indication.
-   Active systemic infection or local infection at the proposed injection site.
-   Severe uncorrected coagulopathy or anticoagulation that cannot be safely managed perioperatively per proceduralist assessment.
-   Known allergy to the injectable agent (corticosteroid or local anesthetic) without documented plan to use an alternative agent.
-   Severe spinal stenosis with intraspinal obstruction that precludes safe epidural needle placement.
-   Injections performed for the sole purpose of surgical or anesthetic preparation without a therapeutic indication (unless separately indicated as a diagnostic block under a distinct clinical rationale).
-   Bilateral simultaneous transforaminal injections at the same level on the same date of service require explicit documentation of bilateral radiculopathy and are subject to payer-specific policies; injections in multiple distinct spinal regions on the same date of service require documented medical rationale.
-   Cumulative corticosteroid dose considerations: payers may apply limits on total annual corticosteroid dose (e.g., equivalent of no more than 3 mg/kg triamcinolone or equivalent per year across all injection types); documentation of agents and doses across all injection visits should be maintained.
