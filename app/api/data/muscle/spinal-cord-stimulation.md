MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is an implantable neuromodulation therapy in which one or more epidural leads deliver low-level electrical current to the dorsal columns of the spinal cord, modulating pain signal transmission. Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) stimulation is a variant that targets the DRG directly and may offer focal coverage for difficult-to-treat areas such as the foot, groin, or knee. Both are considered for patients with chronic, intractable neuropathic pain who have failed conventional medical and interventional management.

SCS therapy is a two-stage process. A temporary percutaneous trial is required before permanent implantation to confirm adequate pain relief and functional benefit for that individual patient. The trial period typically lasts 5 to 10 days, conducted under close clinical supervision.

SCS (trial and permanent implant) is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following criteria are met:

-   Diagnosis of chronic intractable neuropathic pain of at least 6 months' duration attributable to one of the covered indications listed below.
-   Pain causing significant functional disability, defined as meaningful interference with activities of daily living (ADLs), occupational function, or quality of life, AND average pain intensity of 5 or greater on a 0–10 numeric rating scale despite active treatment.
-   Failure of adequate conservative and medical therapy, including ALL of the following unless medically contraindicated or clinically inappropriate:
    -   Trial of structured physical therapy or a supervised home exercise program of at least 6 weeks.
    -   Adequate trial of at least two appropriate pharmacologic agents for neuropathic pain, such as anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin), tricyclic antidepressants or serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (duloxetine), or opioid analgesics at therapeutic doses with documented inadequate response, intolerable side effects, or medical contraindication to continued use.
    -   Consideration of other appropriate interventional therapies (for example, epidural steroid injections, nerve blocks, or radiofrequency ablation) where clinically applicable, with documented inadequate or unsustained response.
-   Psychological evaluation clearance from a licensed mental health professional (psychologist, psychiatrist, or licensed clinical social worker with pain psychology training) documenting:
    -   Absence of untreated major psychiatric disorder (for example, untreated major depressive disorder, psychosis, active suicidal ideation) that would preclude safe use of or adherence to the therapy.
    -   Absence of active, untreated substance use disorder (SUD). If SUD is present, the member must be engaged in and compliant with a recognized treatment program and the treating SUD clinician must provide clearance.
    -   Absence of significant somatization, active malingering, or secondary gain concerns that would predict poor outcomes.
    -   Cognitive and behavioral capacity to operate the patient programmer and follow post-implant instructions.
-   Absence of absolute contraindications (see LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS).
-   Procedure to be performed by a qualified specialist (pain management physician, neurosurgeon, or physiatrist) with documented training and ongoing experience in implantable neurostimulation.

INDICATIONS

SCS Trial (Percutaneous Temporary Lead Placement)

The SCS trial is indicated to evaluate predicted response prior to permanent implantation. A trial is required before permanent implantation unless a documented clinical reason makes a trial unsafe or unfeasible. The following diagnoses are covered indications for an SCS trial when the medical necessity criteria above are met:

-   Failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS) / Persistent spinal pain syndrome type 2 (PSPS-T2): Chronic radicular or axial neuropathic pain persisting or recurring after one or more lumbar or cervical spinal surgeries, with pain attributable to post-surgical neuropathic etiology rather than new structural pathology amenable to additional surgery.
-   Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) type I or type II: Chronic neuropathic pain meeting Budapest Criteria, refractory to conventional medical management.
-   Painful diabetic neuropathy (PDN): Chronic, symmetric or asymmetric distal painful neuropathy attributable to diabetes mellitus, refractory to guideline-directed pharmacologic therapy.
-   Refractory radiculopathy: Chronic neuropathic pain in a dermatomal distribution from nerve root injury or compression, where further surgical intervention is not indicated or has been declined, and adequate conservative and interventional therapy has failed.
-   Chronic intractable neuropathic pain from other etiologies: Including post-herpetic neuralgia, lumbosacral arachnoiditis, peripheral vascular disease (ischemic limb pain), and refractory angina (where no revascularization option exists), subject to payer-specific coverage policies.

The trial is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following apply:

-   All medical necessity criteria above are satisfied.
-   Pre-operative psychological evaluation has been completed and clearance documented within 12 months of the planned trial.
-   Advanced spinal imaging (MRI or CT myelogram of the relevant spinal segment) has been reviewed to confirm that epidural lead placement is anatomically feasible and to identify any contraindications (for example, severe spinal stenosis at the implant level, epidural fibrosis precluding lead placement).
-   Informed consent documents that the member understands the staged nature of the therapy, the criteria for proceeding to permanent implantation, and the risks of the procedure.

Permanent SCS Implantation (Generator and Lead Implant)

Permanent implantation of the neurostimulator system (implantable pulse generator and permanent lead or leads) is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following criteria are met:

-   A successful SCS trial has been completed, defined by ALL of the following:
    -   Trial duration of at least 5 days (typically 5 to 10 days) with the device active and in use.
    -   At least 50% reduction in pain intensity from baseline on a validated pain scale (NRS or VAS), OR documented clinically meaningful functional improvement (for example, improved ambulation, ability to return to work or daily activities, reduction in opioid consumption) if pain reduction alone is insufficient to demonstrate benefit.
    -   The member and treating clinician confirm that the improvement is sufficient to justify permanent implantation.
-   All pre-trial medical necessity criteria continue to be met.
-   No new contraindications have emerged between the trial and the scheduled permanent implant.
-   For rechargeable IPG selection: the member demonstrates physical and cognitive ability to manage charging requirements.

DRG Stimulation (Dorsal Root Ganglion Stimulation)

DRG stimulation (CPT 63650 with DRG-targeted leads) may be considered as an alternative or adjunct to conventional SCS for focal neuropathic pain in distributions that are difficult to cover with standard SCS, including foot, ankle, knee, groin, or chest wall pain attributable to a covered indication above. All SCS medical necessity criteria and the trial requirement apply equally to DRG stimulation. DRG stimulation requires payer-specific coverage confirmation, as some plans follow CMS local coverage determinations that explicitly include or exclude DRG stimulation.

Revision, Replacement, and Explantation

-   Lead revision or replacement (CPT 63663, 63664): Medically necessary when there is documented lead migration, fracture, or failure causing loss of adequate stimulation in a patient with an established, functioning SCS system who has been receiving therapeutic benefit.
-   Generator replacement (CPT 63685): Medically necessary for end-of-battery life or device malfunction in a patient receiving documented ongoing therapeutic benefit.
-   Explantation (CPT 63661, 63662, 63688): Medically necessary for infection, device malfunction not amenable to revision, loss of benefit, intolerable stimulation side effects, or patient request.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

All requests for SCS trial or permanent implant require submission of the following documentation:

-   Comprehensive pain management evaluation notes from the requesting specialist documenting: diagnosis, pain duration (minimum 6 months), pain intensity ratings, functional impairment assessment, and treatment history.
-   Conservative and medical therapy failure documentation:
    -   Physical therapy or supervised home exercise program: dates of service, treating provider, and summary of response.
    -   Pharmacologic trial records: names of medications trialed, doses, duration, and reason for discontinuation (inadequate efficacy, side effects, or contraindication) for at least two appropriate neuropathic pain agents.
    -   Prior interventional procedures: operative or procedure notes, dates, and documented response for any applicable procedures (for example, epidural steroid injections, nerve blocks, radiofrequency ablation).
-   Psychological evaluation report from a licensed mental health professional, completed within 12 months of the trial request, explicitly addressing:
    -   DSM-5 psychiatric diagnoses present and their treatment status.
    -   Substance use disorder screening and status; if active SUD is present, documentation of treatment engagement and clearance from the treating SUD clinician.
    -   Behavioral and cognitive suitability for neurostimulation therapy.
    -   Explicit clearance or deferral recommendation with clinical rationale.
-   Advanced spinal imaging report (MRI preferred; CT myelogram if MRI is contraindicated): reviewed to confirm feasibility of epidural lead placement and absence of anatomical contraindications at the targeted spinal level, obtained within 24 months of the request.
-   For permanent implant requests: trial summary documenting trial duration, stimulation parameters used, pre- and post-trial pain scores, and the treating clinician's assessment of trial success meeting the 50% pain relief or functional improvement threshold.
-   Current medication list, including all pain medications and any anticoagulants or antiplatelets requiring perioperative management.
-   Operative note or procedure report for any prior spinal surgery (for FBSS/PSPS-T2 diagnoses).

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Permanent implantation without a successful trial: Permanent SCS implantation is not covered without documentation of a successful temporary trial meeting the criteria above, except in rare cases where a trial is medically contraindicated and documented accordingly.
-   Untreated major psychiatric comorbidity: SCS is not covered when an untreated major psychiatric disorder (untreated major depressive disorder, active psychosis, active suicidal ideation, untreated borderline personality disorder with severe impulsivity) is present that has not been addressed to the satisfaction of the psychological evaluator.
-   Active, untreated substance use disorder: SCS is not covered when active SUD is present and the member is not enrolled in and compliant with a recognized treatment program. Stable, treated SUD with documented clearance does not constitute an absolute exclusion.
-   Pain primarily of nociceptive or mechanical origin: SCS is generally not covered for axial mechanical back pain without neuropathic features (for example, uncomplicated lumbar facetogenic pain, unspecified low back pain without radiculopathy or nerve injury) in the absence of a specific covered neuropathic indication.
-   Pain amenable to further surgical correction: SCS is not indicated when a structural lesion is present (for example, recurrent disc herniation with radiculopathy, spinal instability) that is amenable to and appropriate for additional surgical intervention.
-   Active spinal or systemic infection: Implantation is contraindicated in the presence of active infection at or near the implant site, or untreated systemic bacteremia or sepsis.
-   Coagulopathy or anticoagulation not managed perioperatively: Uncorrected coagulopathy that cannot be safely reversed for the procedure.
-   Implanted cardiac devices: Prior to implantation, compatibility of SCS with existing cardiac pacemakers or implantable cardioverter-defibrillators must be confirmed with the cardiac device manufacturer; this is not an absolute exclusion but requires pre-procedural cardiac device evaluation and programming adjustments.
-   Pregnancy: Implantation should generally be deferred during pregnancy unless risks of untreated pain clearly outweigh procedural risk.
-   Inadequate psychological evaluation: Coverage will be denied when the required psychological evaluation has not been performed, is incomplete, explicitly defers clearance, or was performed by a provider without appropriate licensure or pain psychology training.
-   Serial or repeated SCS trials in the absence of a new clinical indication: A second SCS trial is generally not covered within 12 months of a failed trial unless a new or substantially changed clinical circumstance is documented.
-   Exclusively central (brain-mediated) pain syndromes: SCS targeting the spinal cord is not covered for thalamic pain syndrome, central post-stroke pain, or other central sensitization syndromes where the primary driver is supraspinal and spinal cord stimulation has no established evidence base.
-   Investigational waveform modalities: Novel waveform configurations (for example, closed-loop adaptive stimulation, ultra-high frequency stimulation above 10 kHz) may be subject to payer-specific investigational status determinations; coverage follows the applicable clinical policy.
