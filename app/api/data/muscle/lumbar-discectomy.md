MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Lumbar discectomy (open or microdiscectomy technique) is considered medically necessary for the surgical decompression of a lumbar nerve root when the member presents with symptomatic lumbar disc herniation and meets the criteria outlined below. The procedure is indicated only when imaging findings are concordant with the clinical level and side of radiculopathy confirmed by neurologic examination, and when appropriate non-operative care has been attempted or is contraindicated by clinical urgency.

All of the following standard criteria must be met for elective lumbar discectomy:

-   Lumbar radiculopathy with dermatomal pain, paresthesia, or neurologic deficit corresponding to the distribution of the compressed nerve root at the affected level (for example, posterior thigh and calf for L5 or S1, anterior thigh for L3 or L4).
-   Advanced imaging (MRI preferred; CT myelography acceptable when MRI is contraindicated) demonstrating disc herniation (protrusion, extrusion, or sequestration) at the level and side concordant with the clinical syndrome.
-   Objective neurologic findings on physical examination consistent with nerve root compromise at the affected level, such as one or more of the following:
    -   Positive straight-leg raise test (SLR, typically positive at 60 degrees or less for L4–S1 pathology) or positive femoral stretch test (L2–L4 pathology)
    -   Dermatomal sensory deficit (numbness, hypoesthesia) corresponding to the affected root
    -   Motor weakness in a myotomal distribution corresponding to the affected root (for example, extensor hallucis longus weakness for L5; gastrocnemius/soleus weakness for S1; quadriceps weakness for L3 or L4)
    -   Diminished or absent deep tendon reflexes at the knee (L3–L4) or ankle (S1)
-   Failure of at least 6 weeks of clinically supervised, guideline-consistent non-operative treatment unless an urgent indication (see below) applies. Non-operative care must include a multimodal approach consisting of:
    -   Active physical therapy or a structured home exercise program targeting lumbar spine mobility, core stabilization, and neural mobilization
    -   At least one additional modality such as:
        -   Oral anti-inflammatory or analgesic medication (NSAIDs, oral corticosteroids, neuropathic agents)
        -   Epidural steroid injection or transforaminal nerve root block at the affected level
        -   Activity modification, chiropractic manipulation, or structured self-directed rehabilitation
-   Persistent or worsening radicular symptoms despite non-operative care, with functional impairment that is consistent with the imaging and clinical findings and that is expected to improve with surgical decompression.

URGENT INDICATIONS (conservative care trial not required)

Lumbar discectomy is considered medically necessary without the requirement for a 6-week conservative care trial when any of the following acute or urgent conditions is present and confirmed by clinical examination and imaging:

-   Progressive motor deficit attributable to nerve root compression from disc herniation, including but not limited to foot drop (L4–L5 disc, L5 root) or quadriceps weakness (L3–L4 disc), with documented worsening on serial examination or acute onset of significant functional impairment.
-   Cauda equina syndrome, defined as neural compression at the cauda equina producing two or more of the following: bilateral lower-extremity motor or sensory deficits, saddle (perianal) anesthesia or hypoesthesia, bowel or bladder dysfunction (urinary retention, overflow incontinence, or fecal incontinence) attributable to neurogenic compromise. Cauda equina syndrome requires urgent surgical evaluation and is generally treated emergently.
-   Severe, intractable radicular pain that is refractory to or not appropriate for conservative management in the acute setting, with objective neurologic findings and concordant imaging, when delay of surgery is not clinically reasonable.

INDICATIONS

Lumbar discectomy is indicated for the treatment of symptomatic lumbar disc herniation causing nerve root compression. The specific procedure (open discectomy, microdiscectomy, minimally invasive or endoscopic technique) is determined by surgeon preference, anatomy, and extent of herniation.

Appropriate candidates include members with:

-   Single-level lumbar disc herniation with radiculopathy that has failed adequate conservative treatment as described above, with concordant imaging and neurologic examination findings.
-   Recurrent disc herniation at a previously operated level (re-herniation), provided the clinical syndrome and imaging are concordant and the member has failed or is not a candidate for repeat conservative management, and there is adequate residual disc material for decompression.
-   Far-lateral (extraforaminal) disc herniation producing nerve root compression concordant with clinical dermatomal symptoms and exam findings, after failure of conservative care or in the setting of urgent indications.
-   Multi-level disc herniation when a dominant symptomatic level(s) can be identified based on clinical correlation, and surgical decompression is expected to address the primary source of radicular disability.

CPT code 63035 (each additional interspace) is indicated as an add-on to 63030 when more than one interspace is treated at the same operative session based on clinical and imaging evidence.

CPT code 63042 applies to re-exploration or revision discectomy at a previously operated lumbar interspace.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation must be present in the medical record to support medical necessity for lumbar discectomy:

-   History and physical examination:
    -   Detailed description of radicular symptom onset, duration, laterality, and character (pain, paresthesia, weakness), including dermatomal distribution
    -   Symptom severity with functional impact on activities of daily living
    -   Neurologic examination documenting motor strength by myotome, sensory testing by dermatome, deep tendon reflexes, and provocative testing (SLR, femoral stretch test, crossed SLR)
    -   Documentation of bowel, bladder, and sexual function status (to screen for cauda equina signs)

-   Advanced imaging:
    -   MRI lumbar spine (or CT myelogram if MRI is contraindicated) demonstrating disc herniation type (protrusion, extrusion, or sequestration), affected level(s), affected side, and degree of nerve root or thecal sac compression
    -   Explicit documentation by the ordering or treating clinician of imaging-clinical concordance, confirming the herniation level and side match the dermatomal and neurologic findings
    -   Imaging should generally be obtained within 12 months of surgery unless there is documented acute change; older imaging may require updated study to confirm continued relevance

-   Conservative care documentation (for elective cases):
    -   Duration, type, and frequency of physical therapy or structured exercise
    -   Medication history (type, duration, and response to NSAIDs, oral steroids, neuropathic agents)
    -   Documentation of epidural steroid injection or nerve block if performed, including response and date
    -   Any additional conservative modalities attempted and clinical response or lack thereof
    -   Clear documentation that 6 or more weeks of non-operative treatment has been completed without adequate improvement, or explanation of why conservative care was not completed (for urgent cases)

-   Surgeon evaluation:
    -   Treating surgeon's clinical assessment confirming surgical candidacy, procedure planned, and expected benefit
    -   Documentation that the risks, benefits, and alternatives to surgery have been discussed with the patient
    -   If urgent indication applies (progressive motor deficit or cauda equina), explicit documentation of the acute or worsening neurologic finding and timing

-   Operative plan:
    -   Planned CPT procedure code(s) and interspace(s) to be treated
    -   Planned operative approach (open, microscopic, minimally invasive, or endoscopic)

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

Lumbar discectomy is not considered medically necessary in the following circumstances:

-   Axial (mechanical) low back pain without objective radiculopathy — discectomy is not indicated for low back pain alone (without nerve root compression signs or symptoms) even in the presence of disc degeneration or disc herniation on imaging. Discectomy addresses nerve root compression, not discogenic pain.
-   Non-concordant imaging — a disc herniation identified on imaging that does not correspond anatomically (by level and side) to the patient's dermatomal symptoms, neurologic deficits, and physical examination findings does not justify surgery. Incidental disc herniations are common and should not drive surgical decision-making without clinical correlation.
-   Failure to complete an adequate conservative care trial — absent an urgent indication, surgery should not be authorized before at least 6 weeks of guideline-consistent non-operative management. This includes cases where the member declined conservative treatment without a documented clinical reason precluding it.
-   Discectomy for lumbar spinal stenosis without disc herniation — central canal or lateral recess stenosis due to ligamentum flavum hypertrophy, facet arthropathy, or spondylotic osteophytes without a soft disc component is generally treated with laminectomy or decompression rather than discectomy; authorization should reflect the primary procedure.
-   Prophylactic or pre-emptive discectomy — removal of a lumbar disc without current symptomatic radiculopathy or objective neurologic deficit is not medically necessary.
-   Discectomy combined with fusion at the same level — routine addition of fusion to a single-level discectomy is not supported for uncomplicated disc herniation with radiculopathy. Fusion at the same level requires additional independent medical necessity criteria (for example, instability, spondylolisthesis, or prior decompressive surgery with demonstrated instability).
-   Isolated imaging findings — MRI findings of disc herniation or nerve root contact in the absence of clinical radiculopathy with objective neurologic findings do not alone support surgical authorization.
-   Psychosocial contraindications — unaddressed psychosocial risk factors, secondary gain, or non-physiologic modifiers of pain that have not been evaluated should be documented and addressed prior to surgical authorization to optimize outcomes.
-   Medical contraindications to elective surgery — active spinal infection, severe coagulopathy, or other absolute medical contraindications to surgery that have not been managed.
