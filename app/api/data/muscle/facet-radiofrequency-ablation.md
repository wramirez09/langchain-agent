MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Facet joint radiofrequency ablation (RFA), also called medial branch radiofrequency neurotomy or paravertebral facet joint denervation, uses thermal energy delivered via an electrode placed along the medial branch nerves to interrupt nociceptive transmission from zygapophyseal (facet) joints. It is indicated for chronic axial spinal pain of confirmed facet joint origin that has not responded adequately to conservative care, after two separate positive diagnostic medial branch blocks have established facet-mediated pain as the predominant pain generator.

Facet joint RFA is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following criteria are met:

-   Chronic axial (non-radicular) spinal pain — cervical, thoracic, or lumbar — of at least 3 months' duration
-   Pain causing functional disability or an average pain level of 6 or greater on a 0–10 numeric rating scale attributable to the requested spinal region
-   Lack of evidence that the primary pain source is sacroiliac joint pain, discogenic pain, disc herniation, or radiculopathy
-   Failure of active conservative management for a minimum of 6 weeks prior to the initial diagnostic block series, including at least two of the following:
    -   Structured physical therapy or physiatric rehabilitation program emphasizing active modalities
    -   Home exercise program (HEP) with documented prescription by a physician, physical therapist, or chiropractor
    -   Analgesic pharmacotherapy trial (NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, or other appropriate agents) at therapeutic doses unless contraindicated
    -   Chiropractic manipulation, acupuncture, or other supervised non-pharmacologic care
-   Two separate diagnostic medial branch nerve blocks (dual block protocol) performed at the same segmental level, on different dates, each producing at least 80% concordant relief of the patient's index pain, with duration of relief consistent with the agent used (short-acting versus long-acting local anesthetic)
-   Image guidance (fluoroscopy or CT) used for all diagnostic blocks and for the RFA procedure itself
-   No absolute contraindications (active systemic or local infection, coagulopathy not corrected, allergy to procedural agents, or unstable medical condition precluding the procedure)

INDICATIONS

Initial RFA

All criteria in the Medical Necessity section above must be met. The procedure is authorized per spinal region (cervical, thoracic, or lumbar); multi-region treatment on the same date of service is generally not medically necessary unless documented clinical reasons make separate scheduling medically contraindicated (for example, required anticoagulation interruption that creates undue risk if done on two separate dates).

Repeat RFA

Repeat facet joint RFA at a previously treated level is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following are met:

-   The prior RFA at the same level produced a positive therapeutic response, defined as at least 50% reduction in pain or clinically significant documented functional improvement
-   The duration of relief from the prior RFA was at least 6 months
-   The index pain has recurred, consistent with nerve regeneration rather than a new or alternative pain generator
-   No new structural, infectious, neoplastic, or inflammatory etiology has emerged to explain the recurrent pain
-   Repeat diagnostic medial branch blocks are not routinely required before a repeat RFA when the original blocks were positive and the patient had a confirmed favorable RFA response; however, repeat blocks may be required by individual payer policy or when the clinical presentation has changed materially since the prior procedure
-   Frequency limit: repeat RFA at a given spinal level is typically covered no more than once per 6 months, and not more than twice in a 12-month period per level, absent extraordinary clinical circumstances with documented justification

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

Diagnostic Block Results

-   Documentation of two separate medial branch nerve block procedures at the targeted segmental level, performed on different dates
-   Pre- and post-block pain scores (numeric rating scale or visual analog scale) recorded at the time of each block visit
-   Confirmation that each block produced at least 80% concordant relief of the patient's characteristic axial pain
-   Duration of relief documented and consistent with the pharmacologic agent injected
-   Notation that relief was concordant (i.e., reproduced the patient's typical pain pattern, not just generalized or non-anatomic relief)
-   Specification of spinal region and segmental levels blocked (e.g., right L3–L4 and L4–L5 medial branches for treatment of the L4–L5 and L5–S1 facet joints)

Prior RFA Response (for repeat procedures)

-   Clinical note or procedural record from the prior RFA documenting the level(s) treated and date of service
-   Provider documentation of at least 50% pain reduction or significant functional improvement following the prior RFA
-   Onset and duration of relief from the prior RFA, confirming at least 6 months of sustained benefit before recurrence
-   Description of the current recurrence pattern and correlation with expected nerve regeneration timeline

Conservative Care

-   Progress notes, physical therapy records, or chiropractic records documenting at least 6 weeks of active conservative management
-   Documented pharmacotherapy trials with agents used, doses, and response or reason for discontinuation
-   Home exercise program documentation, including the prescription and evidence of patient engagement
-   Functional assessment at baseline and at the time of PA request (e.g., Oswestry Disability Index, Neck Disability Index, or equivalent validated tool)

General Clinical Documentation

-   History and physical examination with psychosocial and functional assessment
-   Characterization of pain as predominantly axial and non-radicular
-   Advanced imaging (MRI or CT of the relevant spinal region) within the preceding 3 years, or sooner if clinical change warrants, with findings correlated to clinical presentation
-   Attestation by the ordering clinician that image guidance (fluoroscopy or CT) will be used for the RFA procedure
-   Documentation that absolute contraindications have been excluded

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   RFA without prior positive dual diagnostic medial branch blocks is not covered; a single positive block is insufficient to establish facet-mediated pain as the primary diagnosis
-   RFA for primarily radicular pain, myelopathy, or neurogenic claudication attributable to disc herniation, spinal stenosis, or nerve root compression is not covered under this indication; appropriate alternative procedures should be considered
-   RFA of spinal levels not confirmed by positive concordant diagnostic blocks is not covered
-   Intraarticular facet joint injection or steroid injection alone does not qualify as a diagnostic medial branch block and cannot substitute for a medial branch nerve block in the prerequisite block series
-   RFA performed without fluoroscopic or CT image guidance is not covered
-   Repeat RFA sooner than 6 months from the prior RFA at the same level is not covered unless there is documentation of an exceptional clinical circumstance
-   Repeat RFA when the prior procedure did not produce at least 50% relief for at least 6 months is not covered; alternative pain generators should be evaluated
-   RFA as an isolated intervention without concurrent engagement in a multimodal pain management program, including home exercise and self-management strategies, may be subject to denial
-   Bilateral RFA and multi-level RFA on the same date of service in multiple spinal regions (e.g., cervical and lumbar on the same day) are generally not covered without documented medical necessity for same-day multi-region treatment
-   RFA for acute pain, fracture, neoplasm, infection, or inflammatory spondyloarthropathy as the primary pain etiology is not covered under this guideline
-   Pulsed radiofrequency (PRF) applied to the dorsal root ganglion or spinal nerve is not equivalent to thermal medial branch RFA and is subject to separate coverage criteria; PRF for facet-mediated pain is generally considered investigational
