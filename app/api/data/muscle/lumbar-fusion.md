MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Lumbar spinal fusion (arthrodesis) is a surgical procedure in which two or more adjacent lumbar vertebrae are permanently joined to eliminate motion at a painful or unstable segment. It is performed after decompressive procedures such as discectomy or laminectomy when structural instability, deformity, or recurrent pathology is present, or as a primary procedure when instability is the dominant indication. Instrumentation (pedicle screws, interbody cages) and bone grafting materials are used at the surgeon's discretion and must be limited to FDA-approved indications.

Lumbar fusion is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following general conditions are satisfied, together with the pathology-specific criteria in the INDICATIONS section:

-   The member is skeletally mature.
-   Advanced imaging (MRI or CT, within 12 months or sooner if clinical change occurred) demonstrates a structural abnormality at one or more lumbar levels that correlates anatomically with the patient's reported symptoms and objective physical examination findings.
-   The clinical presentation includes at least one of the following:
    -   Neurogenic claudication or radiculopathy attributable to the confirmed structural pathology.
    -   Objective neurologic deficit (motor weakness, sensory loss, or abnormal deep-tendon reflexes) corresponding to the affected level.
    -   Documented spinal instability, deformity, or deformity progression as defined under INDICATIONS.
-   For all non-emergent, degenerative indications: at least 6 continuous months of structured, multimodal, non-operative treatment has been completed without adequate clinical improvement, including:
    -   A supervised active physical therapy program (minimum 6–8 weeks of structured spine-specific rehabilitation targeting core stabilization, flexibility, and functional restoration).
    -   At least one additional conservative measure, such as:
        -   Anti-inflammatory or analgesic medication (NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, or oral corticosteroids, as clinically appropriate).
        -   Epidural steroid injection, selective nerve root block, or medial branch block.
        -   Activity modification, chiropractic manipulation, massage therapy, or acupuncture.
    -   Exceptions to the 6-month conservative care requirement apply to emergent or urgent indications (cauda equina syndrome, progressive motor deficit, fracture with instability, tumor, or infection; see INDICATIONS).
-   Psychosocial risk factors that may preclude benefit from surgery have been assessed and addressed. Non-physiologic contributors to pain (e.g., peripheral neuropathy, piriformis syndrome, sacroiliac dysfunction, somatoform disorder) have been ruled out or are not the primary driver of symptoms.
-   The proposed procedure is appropriate for the number of levels and surgical approach given the confirmed pathology.

INDICATIONS

Lumbar fusion may be considered medically necessary for the following pathology-specific indications:

1. Degenerative Spondylolisthesis with Instability and Neural Compression

    Fusion (with or without concomitant decompression) is indicated when all of the following apply:

    -   MRI or CT confirms spondylolisthesis (any Meyerding grade) at one or more lumbar levels with associated neural compression (central canal, lateral recess, or foraminal stenosis) correlating with symptoms.
    -   Dynamic flexion-extension lumbar radiographs demonstrate instability (translation of 4 mm or greater, or angular motion of 10 degrees or greater at the affected segment), OR grade II or higher spondylolisthesis is confirmed.
    -   Clinical presentation includes neurogenic claudication, radiculopathy, or objective neurologic deficit corresponding to the affected level.
    -   At least 6 months of conservative care has failed, as defined under MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA (unless progressive neurologic deficit is present).

2. Isthmic (Lytic) Spondylolisthesis with Neural Compression or Instability

    Fusion is indicated when all of the following apply:

    -   MRI or CT demonstrates isthmic spondylolisthesis (pars interarticularis defect) with foraminal or canal stenosis correlating with radiculopathy or neurogenic claudication.
    -   Grade II or higher listhesis, or symptomatic grade I listhesis with documented instability on dynamic films (translation 4 mm or more, or angulation 10 degrees or more).
    -   At least 6 months of conservative care has failed (unless progressive neurologic deficit).

3. Symptomatic Lumbar Stenosis with Instability or Iatrogenic Instability

    Fusion following decompression is indicated when:

    -   MRI or CT confirms central, lateral recess, or foraminal stenosis producing neurogenic claudication or radiculopathy.
    -   One or more of the following instability criteria are met:
        -   Pre-existing spondylolisthesis or dynamic instability at the level(s) requiring decompression.
        -   Planned decompression requires bilateral facetectomy or removal of more than 50% of one facet joint, creating iatrogenic instability.
        -   Adjacent-level instability at a segment requiring decompression.
    -   At least 6 months of conservative care, including at least one epidural steroid injection or selective nerve root block, has been completed without adequate relief (unless progressive neurologic deficit).

4. Degenerative Lumbar Scoliosis or Adult Spinal Deformity

    Fusion is indicated when:

    -   Standing full-length scoliosis radiographs confirm degenerative lumbar scoliosis (Cobb angle 20 degrees or greater in the coronal plane) or significant sagittal imbalance (SVA greater than 5 cm, pelvic tilt greater than 20 degrees, or lumbar lordosis-pelvic incidence mismatch greater than 10 degrees).
    -   Symptoms include neurogenic claudication, radiculopathy, or progressive axial deformity pain with documented functional impairment (limitation of ADLs or ambulation).
    -   MRI or CT confirms structural neural compression correlating with symptoms at the operative levels.
    -   At least 6 months of conservative care has failed.
    -   All deformity surgery requests are reviewed on a case-by-case basis given complexity.

5. Recurrent Lumbar Disc Herniation with Radiculopathy

    Fusion at the time of repeat discectomy (or as a stand-alone procedure) is indicated when:

    -   MRI confirms recurrent disc herniation at a previously operated level with significant disc height loss, segmental instability, or facet arthropathy at the same level.
    -   The patient has had prior discectomy at the same level and presents with recurrent or persistent radiculopathy or neurologic deficit correlating with the reherniation.
    -   Significant segmental instability is present on dynamic radiographs (translation 4 mm or more, or angular motion 10 degrees or more) OR MRI demonstrates Modic type I or II endplate changes with disc height loss of 50% or more at the operative level.
    -   Conservative care of at least 6 weeks has been attempted for the current recurrence episode (shorter trials acceptable if neurologic deficit is progressing).

6. Pseudarthrosis (Failed Prior Fusion)

    Revision fusion is indicated when:

    -   CT of the lumbar spine (thin-cut, without contrast) confirms nonunion at a previously attempted fusion level (absence of continuous trabecular bridging bone, hardware failure, or evidence of motion at the fusion site).
    -   The member has persistent or recurrent pain or neurologic symptoms attributable to the pseudarthrosis.
    -   At least 6 months have elapsed since the index fusion surgery (or sooner if hardware failure with instability is confirmed).
    -   Modifiable factors contributing to pseudarthrosis (nicotine use, poorly controlled diabetes, osteoporosis, metabolic bone disease) have been addressed or are being actively managed prior to revision.

7. Spinal Fracture with Instability

    Fusion is indicated on an urgent or emergent basis (conservative care trial not required) when:

    -   CT or MRI confirms a traumatic or pathologic lumbar fracture with:
        -   Neurologic compromise (motor deficit, cauda equina syndrome), OR
        -   Structural instability (e.g., burst fracture with posterior column failure, fracture-dislocation, retropulsed fragment causing significant canal compromise), OR
        -   Progressive deformity or pain refractory to bracing with confirmed instability.
    -   Surgical stabilization is expected to provide neurologic protection, deformity correction, or pain relief superior to non-operative management.

8. Spinal Tumor

    Fusion is indicated (conservative care trial not required) when:

    -   CT or MRI confirms a primary or metastatic spinal tumor at a lumbar level causing instability, deformity, or neurologic compromise.
    -   Fusion is performed in conjunction with tumor resection or stabilization to restore spinal stability and preserve or recover neurologic function.
    -   The patient's overall oncologic status and life expectancy are consistent with the expected benefit of surgical stabilization.

9. Spinal Infection (Osteomyelitis, Discitis, Epidural Abscess)

    Fusion is indicated (conservative care trial not required) when:

    -   MRI confirms vertebral osteomyelitis, septic discitis, or epidural abscess at a lumbar level with associated bony destruction, instability, or neurologic compromise.
    -   Surgical debridement, drainage, or decompression is required, and fusion is necessary to restore stability after resection of infected tissue.
    -   Appropriate infectious disease consultation and antimicrobial therapy are in place.

10. Isolated Chronic Low Back Pain (Discogenic Pain) without Instability or Deformity

    Fusion for isolated axial low back pain attributed to degenerative disc disease without structural instability, deformity, or neural compression is considered medically necessary only when ALL of the following apply:

    -   MRI demonstrates concordant findings at one level (Modic type I or II endplate changes, disc height loss) corresponding to the patient's pain pattern; multi-level degenerative changes alone are insufficient.
    -   At least 6 months of comprehensive active conservative care has been completed, including a minimum of 8 weeks of structured physical therapy and a formal cognitive-behavioral rehabilitation or functional restoration program.
    -   Provocation discography (with CT post-discography, performed at a facility with appropriate protocol) demonstrates concordant pain reproduction at the affected level with a negative control level, and the study is technically adequate.
    -   The member has been evaluated by an independent spine specialist or pain management physician confirming the surgical indication.
    -   Psychosocial screening (e.g., Distress and Risk Assessment Method, Örebro Musculoskeletal Pain Questionnaire, or equivalent) has been performed; high-risk psychosocial profiles require multidisciplinary evaluation prior to approval.
    -   This indication is reviewed on a case-by-case basis; authorization is not automatic and evidence supports variable outcomes in this population.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation is required for all lumbar fusion prior authorization requests:

-   Office visit notes (within 90 days) including complete spine-focused history and physical examination with notation of:
    -   Pain location, character, severity (numeric pain scale), and functional impairment (impact on ADLs, ambulation distance, work capacity).
    -   Neurologic examination findings: motor strength by myotome, sensory testing, deep-tendon reflexes, straight-leg raise result.
    -   Duration of symptoms and treatment history.

-   Advanced imaging:
    -   MRI of the lumbar spine (without contrast, unless infection or tumor is suspected; within 12 months or sooner if symptoms changed) with report identifying the affected level(s), type and degree of neural compression, disc pathology, and any deformity.
    -   CT lumbar spine when MRI is contraindicated, for bony detail (fracture characterization, foraminal stenosis evaluation, pseudarthrosis assessment), or for pre-operative planning.
    -   CT myelography when MRI and CT are inadequate to characterize neural compression or in the setting of prior instrumentation obscuring MRI.

-   Dynamic flexion-extension lumbar radiographs (standing, within 12 months) when instability is a primary indication, demonstrating interspinous motion, listhesis translation, and angular change.

-   Full-length standing scoliosis radiographs (36-inch cassette including pelvis) when adult spinal deformity is an indication, with Cobb angle measurement, sagittal vertical axis, and pelvic parameters.

-   Conservative care documentation (for all non-emergent cases):
    -   Physical therapy records with dates of attendance, number of sessions, treatment modalities used, and documented functional outcomes.
    -   Records of all other conservative measures attempted, with dates, dosages (for medications), and clinical response.
    -   Injection records, if applicable: type of injection, level(s) treated, date(s), and degree and duration of relief.
    -   Total duration of conservative care must be clearly calculable from the submitted records.

-   Surgical plan:
    -   Operative levels and approach (posterior, anterior, lateral, or combined).
    -   Instrumentation strategy (interbody device type, pedicle screw construct, number of levels).
    -   Bone grafting plan (autograft, allograft, bone graft substitute, BMP use with documentation of FDA-approved indication if applicable).
    -   Surgeon's attestation that the procedure is the least extensive intervention expected to achieve the surgical goals.

-   For revision fusion requests: prior operative reports, all available post-operative imaging including thin-cut CT confirming pseudarthrosis or hardware failure, and documentation of factors addressed prior to revision (smoking cessation, glucose control, osteoporosis treatment).

-   For deformity cases: documentation of functional assessment tool (e.g., Oswestry Disability Index score of 40 or higher, or equivalent), standing radiographs with measurements, and case-by-case narrative of deformity progression or symptoms.

-   For isolated discogenic low back pain: provocation discography report with CT post-discogram images, independent specialist note, and psychosocial screening results.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Lumbar fusion is not covered for isolated axial low back pain attributed to degenerative disc disease without imaging-confirmed instability, deformity, or neural compression, in the absence of the additional requirements specified above for discogenic pain.

-   Lumbar fusion is not covered when the required minimum of 6 months of documented, multimodal, structured conservative care has not been completed, except for emergent indications (cauda equina syndrome, progressive motor deficit, fracture with instability, tumor, or active infection).

-   Fusion is not covered solely on the basis of radiographic degenerative changes that are asymptomatic or do not correlate with the patient's clinical presentation.

-   Multi-level fusion (3 or more levels) for degenerative disease without deformity, instability, or progressive neurologic involvement requires additional clinical justification and is subject to enhanced review.

-   Fusion is not covered as an alternative to decompression alone when decompression is the appropriate surgical treatment and instability is absent (e.g., isolated lumbar disc herniation or stenosis without listhesis, instability, or destabilizing decompression).

-   Fusion combined with disc arthroplasty at the same level is not covered.

-   Repeat or revision fusion requests require documentation that modifiable risk factors for pseudarthrosis (active tobacco use, uncontrolled diabetes with HbA1c above 7.5%, untreated osteoporosis) have been addressed prior to surgery.

-   Use of recombinant human bone morphogenetic protein (rhBMP-2, Infuse) in lumbar fusion is covered only for FDA-approved indications (single-level ALIF, L2–S1, in skeletally mature patients with degenerative disc disease); off-label use is not routinely covered.

-   Lumbar fusion is not covered in the setting of active untreated psychiatric illness, active substance use disorder without concurrent treatment, or other psychosocial contraindications that have not been evaluated and addressed through appropriate multidisciplinary care.

-   Medical contraindications that significantly increase operative risk and have not been optimized (uncontrolled cardiopulmonary disease, severe anemia, active systemic infection, malnutrition) may preclude coverage until medical status is stabilized.
