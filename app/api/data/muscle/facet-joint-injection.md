MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Facet joint injections (intraarticular) and medial branch nerve blocks (MBB) are procedures targeting the zygapophyseal (facet) joints or the medial branch nerves that innervate them. They serve either a diagnostic purpose — to confirm suspected facet-mediated axial spinal pain — or a therapeutic purpose when short-term pain relief with corticosteroid is warranted. Image guidance (fluoroscopy or CT) is required for all facet joint and medial branch block procedures.

Facet joint injections or medial branch blocks are considered medically necessary when ALL of the following criteria are met:

-   Chronic axial (non-radicular) spinal pain of suspected facet joint origin lasting at least 3 months, or subacute pain of at least 6 weeks duration with documented failure to respond to conservative care
-   Average pain level of 6 or greater on a 0–10 numeric rating scale attributable to the requested spinal region, OR documented functional disability that significantly impairs activities of daily living or ability to work
-   Failure to achieve adequate relief after at least 6 weeks of active conservative management directed at the affected spinal region, including at minimum two of the following: structured physical therapy or a documented active home exercise program, NSAIDs or other appropriate analgesics (unless contraindicated), activity modification, and/or chiropractic or manual therapy
-   Pain is primarily axial and non-radicular in character; there is no evidence that the primary pain generator is a disc herniation, radiculitis, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, or discogenic pain
-   History and physical examination findings consistent with facet-mediated pain, such as axial pain that may refer to the buttocks, groin, or shoulder girdle in typical zygapophyseal referral patterns, pain with spinal extension or rotation, and tenderness to paraspinal palpation overlying the facet joints
-   Absence of absolute contraindications (see Limitations and Exclusions)
-   Procedure is to be performed under fluoroscopic or CT image guidance

For diagnostic medial branch blocks performed as a prerequisite to radiofrequency ablation (RFA), the additional standard is that two separate confirmatory diagnostic MBBs demonstrating at least 80% concordant pain relief (or at minimum 70% relief with documented improvement in function) are required before proceeding to facet denervation. The two diagnostic blocks must be performed with different local anesthetic agents (comparative blocks) unless a single-block protocol is accepted under payer-specific criteria.


INDICATIONS

Facet joint injections and medial branch blocks may be indicated for the following clinical presentations when the medical necessity criteria above are satisfied:

-   Chronic axial cervical pain (cervicogenic axial pain, spondylosis of the cervical spine) consistent with upper, mid, or lower cervical zygapophyseal joint origin, with or without non-radicular referred pain to the occiput, shoulder, or upper extremity that does not follow a dermatomal pattern
-   Chronic axial thoracic pain consistent with thoracic facet joint origin, typically midline or paramedian with extension-related aggravation
-   Chronic axial lumbar pain (axial low back pain, lumbar spondylosis, lumbar facet arthropathy) consistent with lumbar or lumbosacral zygapophyseal joint origin, with or without non-radicular referred pain to the buttocks, hip, or proximal posterior thigh that does not follow a dermatomal pattern
-   Facet synovial cyst confirmed on imaging as a cause of axial or referred pain — intraarticular facet injection may be performed in conjunction with an epidural steroid injection on the same date of service when a synovial cyst is confirmed on imaging and causes both axial and neural compressive pain
-   Pre-RFA diagnostic confirmation: two sequential diagnostic medial branch blocks in the cervical, thoracic, or lumbar region, each demonstrating concordant relief, performed as a mandatory prerequisite before paravertebral facet joint denervation (radiofrequency neurolysis) is authorized
-   Patients whose pain has responded to prior facet or MBB procedures with documented improvement in function or pain scores, when the relief obtained from the prior injection has dissipated and the duration of prior relief was clinically meaningful (generally at least 3 months for therapeutic injections)


REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

All requests for facet joint injections or medial branch blocks must include the following:

-   Recent pertinent office visit notes (typically within 90 days) documenting the nature and duration of axial spinal pain, functional limitations, and clinical findings consistent with facet-mediated pain
-   Detailed pain history specifying: location and character of pain (axial, non-radicular), duration, aggravating and relieving factors (e.g., extension, rotation, prolonged sitting or standing), and numeric pain rating scale score (0–10) for the affected spinal region
-   Physical examination findings, including spinal range of motion assessment, paraspinal tenderness localized to the facet joints, neurological examination confirming absence of myelopathy or significant radiculopathy as the primary pain driver, and any relevant provocative maneuvers
-   Documentation of conservative care failure: dates, duration, provider, and type of treatments attempted (physical therapy with attendance records or documented structured home exercise program; medication trials with agents, doses, and duration; other modalities); minimum 6 weeks of active conservative management is required unless clinically contraindicated
-   Relevant advanced imaging (MRI or CT of the affected spinal region, preferably within 12 months unless clinical stability is documented) confirming degenerative changes of the facet joints, spondylosis, or other findings consistent with facet arthropathy; imaging is required to exclude other primary pain generators (disc herniation, tumor, fracture, infection, significant canal stenosis)
-   For repeat therapeutic injections: documentation of the date, spinal level(s), and response (percent pain relief and functional improvement) achieved with the prior injection, confirming at least 3 months of meaningful relief for therapeutic procedures
-   For diagnostic MBBs preceding RFA: documentation from both prior diagnostic blocks including date of each block, spinal levels and specific nerves blocked, local anesthetic used, percent pain relief achieved (must be at least 70–80% concordant relief per block), duration of relief, and functional changes observed; this documentation is required at the time of RFA authorization request
-   Physician attestation that the procedure will be performed under fluoroscopic or CT image guidance with contrast confirmation of needle placement where applicable
-   Relevant specialist notes if care has been directed by a pain management specialist, physiatrist, neurologist, or orthopedic or neurosurgical consultant


LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Not medically necessary for primary radicular pain (dermatomal leg pain or arm pain following a nerve root distribution), acute disc herniation with radiculopathy, or neurogenic claudication as the dominant pain syndrome; the appropriate procedure for those indications is a selective nerve root block or epidural steroid injection
-   Not covered without fluoroscopic or CT image guidance; landmark-guided or blind facet injections are not considered medically necessary for commercial payers
-   Not medically necessary for acute spinal pain of less than 6 weeks duration (except in select circumstances such as acute-on-chronic exacerbation with documented baseline chronic axial pain)
-   Frequency limits — therapeutic facet joint injections and medial branch blocks:
    -   Cervical and thoracic region: generally no more than 3 injections per spinal region per year; repeat injections require documentation of prior meaningful relief (at least 3 months duration) and recurrence of pain
    -   Lumbar region: generally no more than 3–4 injections per spinal region per year; same recurrence and prior relief documentation required
    -   Bilateral injections at the same spinal level may be performed in the same session when bilateral facet-mediated pain is documented
    -   Injections in multiple spinal regions (e.g., cervical and lumbar) on the same date of service are generally not medically necessary; exceptions may be considered on a case-by-case basis when holding anticoagulation therapy on separate dates would create undue clinical risk, with documentation
-   Different types of spinal injections in the same spinal region (e.g., facet injection plus epidural steroid injection at the same cervical, thoracic, or lumbar level) should not be performed on the same date of service, except facet injection combined with epidural steroid injection for a synovial cyst confirmed on imaging
-   Not medically necessary when performed solely for the purpose of surgical planning without an intent to provide diagnostic or therapeutic benefit to the patient
-   Absolute contraindications: active systemic infection, active spinal infection or osteomyelitis, skin or soft tissue infection at the planned needle entry site, coagulopathy or therapeutic anticoagulation that cannot be safely held, allergy to contrast media or local anesthetics or corticosteroids that cannot be managed, and patient inability to cooperate with the procedure
-   Not appropriate as a standalone treatment for discogenic pain, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, or spinal instability where those are identified as the primary pain generators
-   Diagnostic MBBs performed as a prerequisite to RFA require two separate positive blocks (comparative blocks with different anesthetic agents) under most commercial payer policies; a single positive block is generally insufficient to authorize radiofrequency neurolysis
-   Intraarticular facet injections and medial branch blocks are distinct procedures; intraarticular injection places the agent inside the joint capsule while MBB targets the medial branch nerve proximal to the joint; authorization requests must specify which technique is planned, as CPT coding and coverage criteria may differ
