MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Total shoulder arthroplasty (TSA) — both anatomic and reverse — is considered medically necessary when the clinical and radiographic criteria below are met. There is no medical necessity to perform shoulder arthroplasty in individuals with severe radiological disease and no symptoms. Anatomic TSA resurfaces the humeral head and native glenoid using a polyethylene glenoid component, preserving normal glenohumeral biomechanics, and requires an intact and functional rotator cuff. Reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) reverses the ball-and-socket geometry, transferring primary elevation force to the deltoid, and is indicated when the rotator cuff is absent, irreparable, or in clinical situations where anatomic reconstruction is not feasible.

Total shoulder arthroplasty (anatomic or reverse) is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following general criteria are met, plus the procedure-specific indications described below:

-   Persistent pain and documented functional impairment of the affected shoulder that significantly limits activities of daily living, work, or sleep.
-   Failure of at least 3 months of appropriate non-operative conservative management, unless the severity of the presentation or the specific indication makes conservative care inappropriate or contraindicated (for example, acute complex proximal humerus fracture, severe glenohumeral destruction with bone loss, or progressive pseudoparalysis).
-   Radiographic evidence of structural glenohumeral disease on standard radiographs (true AP Grashey view, axillary lateral, and scapular Y view) corroborating the clinical indication.
-   No absolute contraindications to surgery are present.
-   Pre-operative medical clearance confirming the patient is an acceptable surgical candidate.
-   No cortisone injection into the glenohumeral joint within 12 weeks of the planned surgery.
-   No prior arthroscopic surgery of the shoulder within 12 weeks of the planned surgery.

INDICATIONS FOR TOTAL SHOULDER ARTHROPLASTY

Anatomic Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (TSA)

Anatomic TSA is considered medically necessary for glenohumeral osteoarthritis with an intact and functional rotator cuff when ALL of the following apply:

-   Diagnosis of glenohumeral osteoarthritis confirmed by radiographic findings of two or more of the following on standard shoulder radiographs:
    -   Joint space narrowing (moderate to severe, defined as less than 2 mm remaining joint space on AP Grashey view)
    -   Subchondral sclerosis or cyst formation of the humeral head or glenoid
    -   Osteophyte formation (humeral head osteophytes, inferior humeral osteophyte, or glenoid rim osteophytes)
    -   Humeral head flattening or deformity
-   Rotator cuff is intact or has only minor partial-thickness tears that do not compromise cuff function, confirmed by MRI or clinical examination with full active forward elevation.
-   Adequate glenoid bone stock sufficient to accept a glenoid component, as assessed on radiograph or CT.
-   Documented failure of at least 3 months of conservative management including physical therapy and at least one of the following:
    -   Oral anti-inflammatory or analgesic medication (NSAID, acetaminophen, or equivalent)
    -   Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (if not contraindicated and not within 12 weeks pre-operatively)
    -   Activity modification
-   Clinically significant functional impairment documented on physical examination (for example, restricted active range of motion, pain with passive motion, or impaired activities of daily living).

Anatomic TSA is also considered medically necessary for the following additional indications when the above general criteria are met:

-   Avascular necrosis (osteonecrosis) of the humeral head with involvement of the glenoid articular surface or advanced humeral head collapse (Ficat-Arlet Stage III or IV equivalent), with an intact rotator cuff.
-   Rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory arthropathy of the glenohumeral joint with glenoid involvement, with a functional rotator cuff, after failure of appropriate disease-modifying medical management.
-   Post-traumatic glenohumeral arthritis with an intact rotator cuff and adequate glenoid bone stock.

Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (rTSA)

Reverse TSA is considered medically necessary when the general criteria above are met and ONE OR MORE of the following specific indications are documented:

-   Rotator Cuff Tear Arthropathy (Hamada Classification Grade 2 or higher, or Seebauer Classification 1A/1B/2A/2B):
    -   Massive or irreparable rotator cuff tear (involving supraspinatus and infraspinatus, or including subscapularis) with associated glenohumeral arthritis (joint space narrowing, humeral head superior migration to within 7 mm of the acromion, or acetabulization of the acromion).
    -   Documented loss of active forward elevation to less than 90 degrees due to rotator cuff insufficiency (pseudoparalysis), with or without advanced glenohumeral arthritis.

-   Irreparable Rotator Cuff Tear with Pseudoparalysis (without advanced glenohumeral arthritis):
    -   Massive, irreparable rotator cuff tear (full-thickness tear involving at minimum supraspinatus and infraspinatus) confirmed on MRI, with significant muscle atrophy and/or fatty infiltration (Goutallier Grade 3 or 4) precluding repair.
    -   Active forward elevation less than 90 degrees (pseudoparalysis) with demonstrable deltoid function and intact axillary nerve.
    -   Failure of non-operative treatment (physical therapy for deltoid strengthening, activity modification) for at least 3 months unless pseudoparalysis is severe and progressive.

-   Complex Proximal Humerus Fracture (acute or subacute):
    -   Three- or four-part proximal humerus fracture in patients typically age 65 or older, or any age when open reduction and internal fixation is not technically feasible due to bone quality, fracture pattern, or comminution.
    -   Fracture-dislocation of the proximal humerus with high risk of avascular necrosis.
    -   Head-splitting proximal humerus fracture.
    -   Fracture with significant tuberosity comminution making anatomic ORIF reconstruction unreliable.
    -   Reverse TSA is preferred over hemiarthroplasty in older patients with complex proximal humerus fracture when there is concurrent rotator cuff deficiency or when tuberosity healing is uncertain.

-   Failed Prior Shoulder Arthroplasty (Conversion or Revision to Reverse TSA):
    -   Failed hemiarthroplasty with progressive glenohumeral arthritis, instability, or rotator cuff insufficiency, meeting indications for conversion to total or reverse TSA.
    -   Failed anatomic TSA with glenoid component failure, rotator cuff deficiency, or instability requiring revision to reverse TSA.
    -   Failed prior reverse TSA requiring revision to another reverse TSA configuration.

-   Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis with Rotator Cuff Deficiency:
    -   Severe glenohumeral osteoarthritis with concurrent massive, irreparable rotator cuff tear where the cuff tear precludes use of anatomic TSA.

-   Inflammatory Arthropathy with Rotator Cuff Deficiency:
    -   Rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory arthropathy with glenohumeral destruction and concurrent rotator cuff insufficiency precluding anatomic TSA.

Hemiarthroplasty

Hemiarthroplasty (humeral head replacement without glenoid resurfacing) may be considered medically necessary when:

-   The patient meets all criteria for a total shoulder arthroplasty but has avascular necrosis or osteonecrosis of the humeral head without advanced glenoid disease (preserved glenoid cartilage on imaging or arthroscopy).
-   Acute complex proximal humerus fracture (three- or four-part, head-splitting, or fracture-dislocation) where reverse TSA is not preferred based on patient age, bone quality, or surgeon assessment and tuberosity healing is expected.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation is required for prior authorization of total shoulder arthroplasty:

-   Clinical history and physical examination findings:
    -   Duration and severity of shoulder pain, including impact on sleep, activities of daily living, and work.
    -   Active and passive range of motion measurements (forward elevation, abduction, external rotation, internal rotation).
    -   Strength testing of the rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis) and deltoid.
    -   Documentation of shoulder-specific functional limitations using a validated outcome measure (for example, ASES shoulder score, PENN shoulder score, Simple Shoulder Test, or DASH score).
    -   Notation of axillary nerve and deltoid function (required for reverse TSA).

-   Imaging:
    -   Standard shoulder radiographic series: true AP (Grashey view), axillary lateral, and scapular Y (outlet) view, taken within the prior 12 months, demonstrating the relevant glenohumeral pathology.
    -   For rotator cuff assessment: MRI of the shoulder (or CT arthrogram if MRI is contraindicated) within the prior 12 months documenting rotator cuff tear size, tendon retraction, muscle atrophy, and Goutallier fatty infiltration grade when rotator cuff status is clinically uncertain or when reverse TSA is planned.
    -   For complex proximal humerus fracture: CT scan of the proximal humerus to characterize fracture pattern and tuberosity comminution.
    -   For revision arthroplasty: radiographs of the shoulder demonstrating hardware status, periprosthetic bone loss, or component failure; inflammatory laboratory markers (ESR, CRP) to evaluate for periprosthetic infection unless radiographic evidence of hardware failure or instability is clear.

-   Conservative treatment documentation:
    -   Records of physical therapy participation (dates, modalities, frequency, duration, and outcomes), or documentation of why physical therapy was not appropriate or was deferred.
    -   Medication trials including NSAIDs or analgesics, duration of use, and response.
    -   Documentation of any intra-articular corticosteroid injections including date, response, and duration of relief.
    -   Documentation of activity modification attempts.

-   Surgical planning documentation:
    -   Clearly stated surgical plan identifying the specific procedure (anatomic TSA, reverse TSA, or hemiarthroplasty) and the clinical rationale for the choice.
    -   For reverse TSA: documentation of rotator cuff status (MRI findings), deltoid and axillary nerve integrity, and indication category.
    -   Pre-operative medical evaluation confirming surgical fitness.
    -   Confirmation that no cortisone injection was administered into the glenohumeral joint within 12 weeks prior to the planned surgery date.
    -   Confirmation that no arthroscopic shoulder surgery was performed within 12 weeks prior to the planned surgery date.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Not indicated for glenohumeral osteoarthritis that is radiographically mild (Samilson-Prieto Grade 1, or minimal joint space narrowing without subchondral changes) without documented failure of at least 3 months of conservative management.
-   Anatomic TSA is not appropriate when the rotator cuff is massively torn and irreparable (Goutallier Grade 3 or 4 fatty infiltration of supraspinatus and infraspinatus); reverse TSA is the appropriate procedure in that setting.
-   Not indicated for isolated subacromial impingement, rotator cuff tendinopathy, or partial-thickness rotator cuff tear without glenohumeral arthritis or structural humeral head changes.
-   Not indicated for adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) without underlying glenohumeral arthritis or structural pathology warranting arthroplasty.
-   Not indicated as primary treatment for full-thickness rotator cuff tear with preserved glenohumeral joint space and no pseudoparalysis; rotator cuff repair or other shoulder procedures are appropriate first.
-   Any cortisone injection into the glenohumeral joint within 12 weeks of planned surgery is a contraindication to proceeding.
-   Any prior arthroscopic surgery of the shoulder within 12 weeks of planned surgery is a contraindication to proceeding.
-   Active or recent local or systemic infection is a contraindication.
-   Non-functioning deltoid or confirmed axillary nerve palsy is a contraindication for reverse TSA (deltoid activation is required for the biomechanical function of reverse TSA).
-   Severe osteoporosis with inadequate bone stock to support implant fixation (as evidenced by radiographic osteopenia, DXA T-score less than -3.0, or osteomalacia) is a relative contraindication requiring individualized assessment.
-   Acromial fracture or severely deficient acromion (from prior subacromial decompression or stress fracture) is a contraindication for reverse TSA due to risk of acromial stress fracture.
-   Neurogenic pain syndrome, complex regional pain syndrome, or primary psychological contributors to pain without structural pathology are contraindications.
-   Insufficient glenoid bone stock to accommodate a glenoid component (for anatomic TSA) requires advanced imaging assessment; if glenoid bone loss is severe, reverse TSA, bone grafting, or alternative procedures may be required.
-   Relative contraindication: BMI greater than 40 kg/m² — requires documented discussion of surgical risk; weight loss attempts are not required as a prerequisite but should be encouraged when feasible.
-   Repeat or serial arthroplasty requests require documentation of the specific failure mode, infection workup (ESR, CRP, joint aspiration when indicated), and a clearly defined surgical plan.
