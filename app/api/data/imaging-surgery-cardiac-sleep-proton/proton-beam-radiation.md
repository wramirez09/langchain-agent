\### General Criteria

\- Care/services are expected to be provided by a licensed clinician.

\- If a specific clinical indication is not directly addressed, medical
necessity is determined using widely accepted standard-of-care criteria
supported by evidence-based or peer-reviewed sources.

\### Evidence-Based Criteria

\#### Highly Prevalent Cancer Types

\- Defined as cancers with 60,000 or more cases per year in the USA.

\- Examples listed:

\- Breast

\- Prostate

\- Lung

\- Melanoma of the skin

\- Colon

\- Adult lymphoma

\- Bladder

\- Kidney

\- Head and neck

\- Uterus

\- Pancreatic

\- For these cancers, validation of proton/neutron beam treatment
requires:

\- A published phase 3 randomized controlled trial

\- Comparison against a standard radiation treatment

\- A significant, superior, and clinically meaningful benefit for that
specific cancer type and modality

\#### Less Prevalent Cancer Types

\- Defined as cancers with fewer than 60,000 cases per year in the USA.

\- Proton beam therapy may be supported when:

\- Evidence comes from peer-reviewed medical literature

\- The literature has undergone critical review for scientific accuracy,
validity, and reliability

\- In-house publications from entities tied to manufacturing, selling,
or distributing proton equipment are excluded

\- Evidence review includes:

\- Whether patient and cancer characteristics are adequately represented

\- Whether the administered regimen is adequately represented

\- Whether outcomes show a significant, superior, and clinically
meaningful benefit, consisting of:

\- A survival benefit, or

\- A decrease in chronic/long-term toxicity

\- Not merely a decrease in acute toxicity

\- Statistical significance of p \< 0.05

\- Whether the study design is appropriate to the research question

\- Non-randomized trials with a significant number of subjects may also
support approval

\### Proton Beam Therapy Criteria

\#### Adult Indications

\- Liver (hepatocellular carcinoma) and intrahepatic bile duct cancers

\- Paranasal sinus, nasopharynx, maxillary sinus, ethmoid sinus, and
cavernous sinus cancers

\- Oropharynx cancer, stage III/IV

\- Chordomas and chondrosarcomas of the spine and base of skull

\- Meningioma

\- Arteriovenous malformations

\- Acoustic neuroma

\- Pituitary adenoma

\- Intraocular (uveal) melanoma

\- Other brain or spinal tumors when:

\- They are adjacent to critical structures such as:

\- Optic nerve

\- Optic chiasm

\- Brain stem

\- Spinal cord

\- And they cannot be sufficiently spared using IMRT or SRS

\#### Pediatric Indications

\- Approved for all pediatric patients age 18 years or younger

\- Patients older than 18 years with the same histology as common
pediatric cancers:

\- May be evaluated as adolescent/young adult cases

\- May be approved case by case

\- Pediatric cases should be managed by radiation oncologists who:

\- Have access to clinical research trials

\- Have considerable experience treating pediatric patients

\- Multidisciplinary consultation should be considered, including a
radiation oncologist, to reduce late radiation effects

\- The treating specialist may determine the appropriate dose and number
of fractions

\#### Re-Irradiation

\- Re-irradiation is additional radiation to the same anatomic area that
already received prior radiation

\- It does not apply when the new treatment area is completely separate
from the prior treatment area

\- Approved for all patients who:

\- Previously received radiation to an anatomic location

\- Now require another course of radiation to that same area

\- Dose and fractionation:

\- Depend on prior treatment history

\- Are left to the discretion of the treating physician

\- Should use peer-reviewed literature when possible

\### Neutron Beam Therapy Criteria

\- Considered medically necessary for salivary gland cancers that are:

\- Unresectable, or

\- Recurrent

\## Required Documentation

\- Appropriate supporting documentation

\- Recent pertinent office visit notes

\- Laboratory data

\- Results of any special testing

\- All prior relevant imaging results, if applicable

\- The reason alternative imaging cannot be performed, if applicable

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\- Proton beam therapy is excluded when the medical evidence for a
cancer type consists only of:

\- An abstract

\- A meeting abstract

\- A published case study

\- Proton beam therapy is excluded when:

\- There is insufficient medical evidence to establish medical necessity

\- The patient is enrolled in a clinical trial and enrollment is the
only basis offered for coverage

\- Proton beam therapy is excluded when the only published studies are:

\- Physics studies

\- Dosimetry studies

\- Neutron beam therapy is excluded for tumors not listed in the neutron
beam indications section

\## Additional Coverage Notes

\- A related reimbursement note indicates proton beam therapy is a
covered benefit for individuals younger than 21 years.

\- For individuals age 21 years and older, proton beam therapy is a
covered benefit with conditions for the following primary cancers:

\- Esophageal

\- Head/neck

\- Skull-based

\- Hepatocellular carcinoma

\- Brain/spinal

\- Ocular

\- Other primary cancers when all other treatment options are
contraindicated after review by a multidisciplinary tumor board

\- All other conditions are identified as non-covered in that
reimbursement note.
