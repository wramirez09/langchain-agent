1.  MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

A. General requirements for inherited-condition genetic testing

-   Genetic testing is medically necessary when all of the following are
    > met:

    -   The individual is suspected of having a known genetic condition
        > based on clinical presentation, or is presymptomatic but at
        > significant risk based on family history.

        > The disorder being evaluated has clearly defined
        > genes/pathogenic variants and the test has high sensitivity
        > and specificity for clinical decision-making.

        > The testing has established analytical and clinical validity
        > and is performed in an appropriately accredited/certified
        > laboratory.

        > Alternate biochemical or other clinical tests are unavailable,
        > indeterminate, or less effective.

        > The disease has significant morbidity and/or mortality.

        > Knowledge of the pathogenic variant is expected to directly
        > affect management, including diagnostic, predictive,
        > surveillance, therapeutic, or reproductive decisions.

    > Family history may include first-, second-, or third-degree
    > relatives depending on inheritance pattern.

    > Confirmatory testing is medically necessary if the above criteria
    > are met and either:

    -   A pathogenic/likely pathogenic germline variant was found
        > through an FDA-approved direct-to-consumer test, or

        > A pathogenic/likely pathogenic germline variant was found
        > through IRB-approved clinical research.

    > Testing may be performed only once per lifetime for a given
    > condition.

B. Multigene panel testing

-   May be considered only when all general and condition-specific
    > criteria are met and all of the following apply:

    -   The panel is as focused as reasonably possible.

        > Each included gene has evidence of disease association.

        > Pathogenic variants in each gene could affect clinical
        > management.

        > More probable genes should be tested before panel testing when
        > clinically appropriate.

C. Cardiac conditions

-   General rule:

    -   Cardiac genetic testing is medically necessary only when the
        > general inherited-condition criteria are also met.

1.  Hereditary arrhythmia syndromes

-   Medically necessary when any of the following are present:

    -   Symptomatic individual with supporting clinical and ECG features
        > for long QT syndrome, CPVT, or Brugada syndrome.

        > Presymptomatic individual with characteristic ECG features
        > suggesting inherited arrhythmia syndrome and a first-degree
        > relative with:

        -   sudden cardiac death

            > unexplained syncope

            > unexplained cardiac arrest

        > Known familial pathogenic variant for long QT syndrome, CPVT,
        > or Brugada syndrome in a first- or second-degree relative.

    > Also required:

    -   Testing must be focused on variants relevant to the suspected
        > diagnosis and known familial genetics.

2\. Hereditary cardiomyopathy syndromes

-   Medically necessary when all of the following are met:

    -   First-degree relative with supporting clinical features of HCM,
        > ARVC/D, or DCM.

        > Alternate acquired etiology has been excluded.

        > Testing is focused on relevant pathogenic variants for the
        > suspected diagnosis and family genetics.

    > Or:

    -   Symptomatic individual under age 18 with no known family
        > history, no clinically identified syndrome, and acquired
        > causes excluded.

3\. Hereditary aortopathies

-   Targeted testing may be medically necessary when any of the
    > following apply:

    -   Personal history of thoracic aortic disease before age 60 and
        > acquired causes excluded.

        > Personal history of thoracic aortic disease at any age with
        > aneurysm and/or dissection/rupture of other arteries.

        > Physical findings suggest a syndromic connective tissue
        > disorder with known genetic aortic risk but diagnosis remains
        > unclear.

        > Asymptomatic individual with first- or second-degree relatives
        > unavailable for testing who had thoracic aortic disease,
        > unexplained sudden cardiac death, or aneurysm/dissection in
        > other arteries.

    > Testing for a known familial aortopathy variant is medically
    > necessary when both apply:

    -   A first- or second-degree blood relative has a pathogenic
        > variant associated with HTAD.

        > Testing is targeted to the known familial variant.

4\. Post-mortem testing after sudden cardiac death

-   Medically necessary for an asymptomatic individual when all apply:

    -   The decedent was a first- or second-degree relative.

        > Sudden cardiac death occurred at or before age 50.

        > Cause of death remains unexplained despite history, autopsy,
        > toxicology, and cardiac pathology.

5\. Amyloidosis

-   TTR sequencing is medically necessary when transthyretin amyloidosis
    > has already been confirmed and testing is needed to distinguish
    > hereditary from wild-type disease.

D. Primary mitochondrial disease

-   Medically necessary when the individual has an unexplained
    > progressive multisystem disorder, usually involving the CNS and/or
    > neuromuscular system, with findings such as:

    -   Brain MRI pathology associated with mitochondrial disease

        > Organic acid pattern suggestive of mitochondrial disease

        > Evidence of mitochondrial dysfunction in tissue

    > Testing order after criteria are met:

    -   Common mtDNA variant testing or specific nuclear gene testing if
        > a specific mitochondrial disorder is suspected

        > Whole mtDNA sequencing and large-deletion analysis if
        > presentation is nonspecific or prior testing is
        > negative/uninformative

        > Targeted nuclear gene panel under 25 genes if whole mtDNA
        > testing does not yield a diagnosis

E. Neurological conditions

-   Genetic testing for inherited neurologic conditions may be medically
    > necessary when the general requirements or multigene panel
    > criteria are met.

    > Testing for screening/diagnosis of the following common categories
    > is not medically necessary:

    -   Alzheimer's dementia

        > Frontotemporal dementias

        > Motor neuron diseases such as ALS

    > Exception:

    -   Single-gene SOD1 testing is medically necessary when both apply:

        -   Adult with clinical diagnosis of ALS

            > Candidate for tofersen per FDA labeling

F. Retinal disorders

-   Testing for pathogenic variants associated with inherited retinal
    > disorders may be medically necessary when general or multigene
    > panel criteria are met.

    > Testing for a known familial retinal variant is medically
    > necessary when both apply:

    -   First- or second-degree relative has a pathogenic/likely
        > pathogenic retinal-condition variant.

        > Testing is targeted to that known familial variant.

G. Thrombophilia testing

-   Testing for common pathogenic variants associated with Factor V
    > Leiden and/or prothrombin G20210A is medically necessary to inform
    > anticoagulation decisions when any of the following are met:

    -   Unprovoked or weakly provoked VTE at or before age 50

        > Recurrent VTE

        > VTE plus either:

        -   two or more family members with VTE, or

            > one first-degree relative with VTE at or before age 40

        > VTE involving cerebral or splanchnic veins

        > Individual contemplating pregnancy with a first-degree
        > relative with VTE and confirmed hereditary thrombophilia

        > Unprovoked VTE and stopping anticoagulation is being
        > considered, and a positive thrombophilia result would change
        > that decision

H. Preimplantation genetic testing

-   PGT is medically necessary when embryos are at increased risk of a
    > recognized inherited condition and all of the following apply:

    -   The condition and variants would cause significant morbidity
        > and/or mortality.

        > The condition is due to either:

        -   a single-gene abnormality (PGT-M), or

            > structural chromosomal rearrangement in a gamete provider
            > (PGT-SR)

        > One of the following gamete-provider criteria is met:

        -   both providers carry the same autosomal recessive condition

            > one provider is a known carrier of an autosomal recessive
            > disorder and the couple previously had an affected child

            > at least one provider carries an autosomal dominant or
            > sex-linked condition

            > one provider has at least 25% risk of carrying an
            > autosomal dominant or X-linked condition and requests
            > nondisclosure testing

            > at least one provider carries a balanced structural
            > chromosome abnormality

            > one provider is an anonymous reproductive donor with
            > unknown status while the other provider is a known carrier

    > PGT-A is medically necessary only when there is a clear heritable
    > indication, including:

    -   X-linked recessive conditions

        > Sex-limited conditions

I. Biomarker testing for rejection in solid organ transplantation

-   AlloMap gene-expression profiling is medically necessary for
    > low-risk adolescent/adult cardiac transplant patients when all
    > apply:

    -   Age at least 15 years

        > At least 6 months post-transplant

        > Clinically stable with no signs/symptoms of congestive heart
        > failure

        > No signs/symptoms of graft rejection and no acute rejection
        > treatment needed

        > Testing frequency does not exceed:

        -   every 3 months from month 6 to month 24

            > every 6 months from month 24 to month 60

            > no testing beyond 60 months post-transplant

3\. REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

General documentation expected to support a request

-   Complete history and physical examination

    > Relevant laboratory studies

    > Relevant diagnostic testing

    > Response to prior therapeutic intervention when applicable

    > Documentation that accurately reflects the clinical situation at
    > the time of request

    > Documentation that sufficiently reflects the ordering clinician's
    > intent

Genetic counseling elements expected before testing

-   Family and medical history interpretation for risk assessment

    > Education regarding inheritance, testing, management, prevention,
    > and resources

    > Counseling to support informed decision-making and adaptation

    > Counseling regarding psychological aspects of testing

    > Discussion of:

    -   testing limitations

        > possibility that a negative result does not eliminate
        > hereditary risk

        > possibility of variants of uncertain significance

        > possibility of future variant reclassification

    > Post-test counseling for any diagnostic genetic test result

Condition-specific documentation examples specifically identified in the
policy

-   Cardiac testing:

    -   Supporting ECG features for arrhythmia syndromes

        > Family history of sudden cardiac death, syncope, cardiac
        > arrest

        > Evidence that acquired cardiomyopathy or acquired cardiac
        > disease has been excluded

        > Autopsy/toxicology/pathology details for post-mortem testing

        > Confirmed diagnosis of transthyretin amyloidosis for TTR
        > testing

    > Mitochondrial disease:

    -   Brain MRI findings

        > Organic acid studies

        > Tissue evidence of mitochondrial dysfunction

    > Neurologic testing examples from evaluation table:

    -   Brain MRI

        > EMG

        > CK testing

        > Muscle biopsy

        > Family history

        > Evaluation to rule out alternate causes where applicable

    > Retinal disorders:

    -   Evidence of inherited retinal phenotype and, when applicable,
        > documentation of known familial pathogenic variant in a first-
        > or second-degree relative

    > Thrombophilia:

    -   VTE history, age at event, provoking status, recurrence history

        > Family history of VTE

        > Pregnancy planning context when relevant

        > Evidence that positive testing would alter anticoagulation
        > decision-making

    > PGT:

    -   Known familial pathogenic/likely pathogenic variant or
        > structural rearrangement

        > Carrier status of gamete providers

        > Prior affected offspring when relevant

        > Clear heritable indication for PGT-A if requested

    > Cardiac transplant biomarker testing:

    -   Age

        > Time since transplant

        > Clinical stability

        > Absence of CHF symptoms

        > Absence of graft rejection symptoms/acute rejection treatment

        > Testing interval/frequency documentation

4\. LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

General limitations

-   Testing may be performed only once per lifetime for a given
    > condition.

    > Multigene panels must be focused; broader panels are not
    > automatically supported.

    > Genetic tests not specifically mentioned in the guideline are
    > considered not medically necessary.

    > Repeated testing of the same indication/location is limited and
    > may require additional review or peer-to-peer discussion.

    > Multiple simultaneous diagnostic/therapeutic requests may require
    > peer-to-peer review.

Specific exclusions / not medically necessary items

-   MTHFR-gene variant testing for hereditary thrombophilia risk
    > assessment.

    > Screening/diagnostic genetic testing for:

    -   Alzheimer's dementia

        > Frontotemporal dementias

        > Motor neuron diseases such as ALS

        > except for the specific SOD1/tofersen exception noted above

    > PGT is not medically necessary for:

    -   PGT-A without heritable risk, including advanced maternal age,
        > prior trisomy pregnancy, recurrent pregnancy loss, recurrent
        > implantation failure

        > testing solely to determine carrier status for an autosomal
        > recessive condition

        > somatic genetic changes

        > multifactorial conditions

        > polygenic risk scores/disorders

        > variants of uncertain significance

        > gender selection without sex-linked or sex-limited risk

        > nonmedical traits such as physical characteristics

    > Donor-derived cell-free DNA testing for diagnosis/monitoring of
    > cardiac transplant rejection is not medically necessary.

    > Genetic testing, including donor-derived cell-free DNA, gene
    > expression profiling, or microRNA testing, for kidney or other
    > non-cardiac transplant rejection monitoring is not medically
    > necessary.

    > Retrieved code section also labels certain specific codes as not
    > medically necessary, including 81324-81326, 81440, 81441, 81448,
    > 81558, 0055U, 0079U, 0087U, 0088U, 0118U, 0217U, and 0268U-0276U.

BOTTOM LINE

-   The policy supports genetic testing only when it is clinically
    > focused, supported by phenotype/family history, analytically
    > valid, likely to change management, and documented well.

    > The strongest condition-specific coverage areas in the retrieved
    > material are cardiac syndromes, mitochondrial disease, retinal
    > disorders, thrombophilia, certain PGT scenarios, and limited
    > low-risk cardiac transplant monitoring with AlloMap.

    > Major exclusions include broad unsupported testing, MTHFR
    > thrombophilia testing, routine neurologic screening for common
    > complex disorders, non-heritable PGT-A indications, and most
    > transplant-rejection biomarker testing outside narrow cardiac
    > AlloMap criteria.
