MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

General clinical criteria that support appropriateness

-   Before any intervention, the clinician should confirm the diagnosis
    > or establish the pretest likelihood through a complete evaluation.

    > Complete evaluation includes:

    -   History and physical examination

        > Review of relevant laboratory studies, when applicable

        > Review of diagnostic testing, when applicable

        > Review of response to prior therapeutic intervention, when
        > applicable

    > The expected benefit of the intervention should outweigh potential
    > harms.

    > Current treatment guidelines, literature, and standards of care
    > should support the intervention as offering the greatest net
    > benefit among alternatives.

    > There should be a reasonable likelihood that the intervention will
    > change management and/or improve outcomes.

Wearable cardioverter defibrillator is medically necessary when ANY of
the following apply

-   Existing implanted cardioverter defibrillator must be explanted
    > because of infection or malfunction, and BOTH are true:

    -   The medical necessity criteria supporting the original
        > implantation are still present.

        > The device cannot be replaced before hospital or facility
        > discharge.

    > Criteria are met for initial implantable cardioverter
    > defibrillator implantation, but implantation must be delayed
    > because of:

    -   Systemic infection, or

        > Another medical condition such as:

        -   Lack of vascular access

            > Temporary critical need for anticoagulation

            > Temporary critical need for antiplatelet therapy

    > The individual has a cardiomyopathy likely to recover, such as:

    -   Takotsubo cardiomyopathy

        > Myocarditis-related cardiomyopathy

        > Post-partum cardiomyopathy and has already had hemodynamic
        > instability due to tachyarrhythmia.

    > Criteria are met for initial implantable cardioverter
    > defibrillator implantation in an individual who is on a waiting
    > list for heart transplantation and will be managed as an
    > outpatient.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

Scope limitations

-   The guideline addresses wearable cardioverter defibrillators only.

    > It does not address:

    -   Implantable cardioverter defibrillators

        > Portable non-wearable automatic defibrillators

Clinical limitations noted in the document

-   The document notes that evidence of benefit is lacking for certain
    > groups whose arrhythmic risk may decrease over time, including:

    -   Left ventricular dysfunction in the early period after
        > myocardial infarction

        > Newly diagnosed non-ischemic cardiomyopathy

        > Peripartum cardiomyopathy

        > Myocarditis

    > Patient compliance with wearing the device is important for
    > effectiveness, and noncompliance has been reported.

Utilization and review limitations

-   Multiple diagnostic or therapeutic interventions ordered at the same
    > time may require additional review or peer-to-peer discussion.

    > Repeat diagnostic testing using the same or similar technology may
    > be subject to additional review or peer-to-peer discussion,
    > especially when there are:

    -   Technical issues at the same facility

        > Requests to repeat testing at a different facility because of
        > provider preference or quality concerns

        > Persistent symptoms without clinical change, treatment, or
        > intervention since the prior study

        > Multiple providers ordering repeat testing of the same area
        > over a short period

    > Repeat therapeutic intervention before the expected duration of
    > benefit has elapsed is not appropriate unless it can be confirmed
    > that the prior intervention was never administered.

    > Nonspecific or unlisted procedure codes may require additional
    > documentation and review.

BOTTOM LINE

-   Coverage support in this document is focused on temporary use of a
    > wearable cardioverter defibrillator when implantable defibrillator
    > therapy is indicated but cannot be provided immediately, or when a
    > previously implanted device must be removed and replacement is
    > delayed.

    > The strongest approval scenarios are:

    -   Explant due to infection or malfunction with delayed replacement

        > Delayed initial implant despite meeting implant criteria

        > Recoverable cardiomyopathy with prior hemodynamic instability
        > from tachyarrhythmia

        > Outpatient heart transplant waitlist patient who meets initial
        > implant criteria
