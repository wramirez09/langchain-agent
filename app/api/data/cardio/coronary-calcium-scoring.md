MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Coronary artery calcium scoring is performed by either EBCT or non-contrast cardiac CT. CAC testing provides a quantitative assessment of coronary artery calcium content in Agatston units, as an adjunct to the estimation of global risk for coronary or cardiovascular events over the next 10 years. A CAC Score greater than 0 is a highly specific feature of coronary atherosclerosis.

Coronary artery calcium testing is considered medically necessary only to help with management decisions for hypercholesterolemia when all of the following apply:

-   No known atheromatous vascular disease
-   Not diabetic
-   Age 40 to 75
-   LDL 70 to 190 mg/dL
-   10-year ASCVD risk in the borderline-to-intermediate range (about 5% to 20%), reported as one of two distinct bands:
    -   Borderline risk: about 5% to less than 7.5% (most useful with risk enhancers and uncertainty about starting statin therapy)
    -   Intermediate risk: about 7.5% to 20% (to help decide on starting or intensifying statin therapy)
-   None of the following risk-enhancing factors:
    -   Family history of premature ASCVD
    -   LDL 160 mg/dL or higher
    -   Triglycerides above 175 mg/dL
    -   Metabolic syndrome
    -   Chronic kidney disease
    -   Chronic inflammatory condition
    -   Menopause before age 40
    -   History of preeclampsia
    -   High-risk race/ethnicity such as South Asian ancestry
    -   Elevated hs-CRP
    -   Elevated lipoprotein(a)
    -   ApoB above 130 mg/dL
    -   Ankle-brachial index below 0.9

INDICATIONS FOR CORONARY ARTERY CALCIUM (CAC) TESTING

Patients can be considered for CAC testing when there is well-documented evidence of one of the following, and when the result is expected to change management:

-   Asymptomatic adult, without known CAD, when the CAC result will change management.
-   Borderline or intermediate 10-year ASCVD risk where CAC will guide statin therapy:
    -   Borderline risk (about 5% to less than 7.5%) with risk enhancers and uncertainty about starting statin therapy.
    -   Intermediate risk (about 7.5% to 20%) to help decide on starting or intensifying statin therapy.
-   Selected cases with uncertainty despite clinical assessment, such as:
    -   Diabetes age 40 to 75 with uncertainty about statin intensity.
    -   Strong family history of premature ASCVD.
    -   Primary hypercholesterolemia or persistent risk factors where CAC will clarify the need for or intensity of therapy.

The result is expected to impact treatment (for example, defer statin if CAC = 0 in appropriate patients; initiate or intensify statin if CAC is elevated).

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

-   Clinical rationale describing how the CAC result will change management.
-   10-year ASCVD risk calculation (pooled cohort equation) and the data used (age, sex, lipid panel, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes).
-   Risk-enhancing factors (for example, family history of premature ASCVD, LDL level, chronic kidney disease, inflammatory conditions).
-   Confirmation that the member is asymptomatic and has no known CAD or prior coronary revascularization.
-   Prior and current lipid values and therapies considered or discussed.
-   Ordering clinician's intent (for example, to initiate, intensify, or defer statin, or to consider aspirin based on CAC).

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Not for individuals with known CAD, prior myocardial infarction, prior PCI/CABG, or established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. A diagnosis coded as I25.x (known/chronic ischemic heart disease) is not a supporting indication for CAC scoring and should not be submitted as the rationale; if the encounter is for risk stratification, use a screening/risk code (for example Z13.6) instead.
-   Not for evaluation of active chest pain or symptomatic suspected CAD; use appropriate diagnostic testing instead.
-   Not for routine screening in low-risk individuals when results will not change management.
-   Not for serial monitoring of therapy; repeat CAC is generally not covered, and if allowed, typically not more often than every 5 years.
-   Not for preoperative clearance or unrelated risk stratification without clear impact on management.
-   CAC scoring does not directly evaluate coronary stenosis severity and is not used for acute chest pain or suspected acute coronary syndrome.
