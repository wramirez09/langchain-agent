MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Hemodialysis vascular access procedures encompass surgical creation of a permanent arteriovenous (AV) connection for chronic hemodialysis and subsequent interventional or surgical procedures necessary to maintain or restore access function. All criteria are grounded in KDOQI (Kidney Disease Outcomes Quality Initiative) Clinical Practice Guidelines for Vascular Access and mainstream evidence-based practice.

Access creation is medically necessary when a patient has end-stage renal disease (ESRD) or advanced chronic kidney disease (CKD stage 4 or 5) and requires hemodialysis as the modality of renal replacement therapy, provided no existing functioning access is available or the existing access is irreparable. Autogenous AV fistula is the preferred access type; prosthetic AV graft is appropriate only when anatomy or other clinical factors preclude a fistula.

Access maintenance — including diagnostic fistulogram, percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA), stent placement, mechanical or pharmacologic thrombectomy, and open surgical revision — is medically necessary when clinical, hemodynamic, or imaging criteria indicate access dysfunction that will result in inadequate dialysis delivery or imminent access loss if not treated.

INDICATIONS — ACCESS CREATION

Autogenous Arteriovenous Fistula (CPT 36818, 36819, 36820, 36821)

An autogenous AV fistula is indicated when ALL of the following apply:

-   Patient has ESRD (CKD stage 5, GFR less than 15 mL/min/1.73 m²) or CKD stage 4 (GFR 15 to 29 mL/min/1.73 m²) with anticipated need for hemodialysis within 6 to 12 months, OR has CKD stage 3b or higher with rapidly progressive kidney disease and imminent need for renal replacement therapy
-   Hemodialysis has been selected as the modality of renal replacement therapy by the patient and treating nephrologist (transplant not yet available, peritoneal dialysis not feasible or declined, or temporary catheter use is being minimized per best practice)
-   Preoperative venous mapping (duplex ultrasound or equivalent) has been performed demonstrating suitable venous anatomy, specifically:
    -   Cephalic or basilic vein diameter of at least 2.0 mm (some guidelines cite 2.5 mm minimum for the wrist fistula) in the non-dominant arm preferred; dominant arm acceptable when contralateral anatomy is inadequate
    -   Arterial inflow vessel (radial or brachial artery) without significant occlusive disease and with adequate diameter for anastomosis
-   No existing functioning permanent vascular access (AV fistula, AV graft) is present on the ipsilateral or contralateral extremity that can adequately deliver the prescribed dialysis dose
-   Surgical consultation documents the anatomic and clinical feasibility of fistula creation at the planned site

Preferred access hierarchy per KDOQI (in descending order):

1.  Wrist (radiocephalic) AV fistula — primary autogenous fistula at the radial artery–cephalic vein anastomosis; preferred when vein caliber and artery permit
2.  Elbow (brachiocephalic) AV fistula — brachial artery to cephalic vein; indicated when wrist anatomy is inadequate
3.  Brachiobasilic AV fistula with basilic vein transposition — indicated when cephalic vein is absent, inadequate, or previously exhausted; requires basilic vein transposition or superficialization (CPT 36819)
4.  Other autogenous configurations using forearm or upper arm veins as anatomically feasible

Prosthetic Arteriovenous Graft (CPT 36825, 36830)

A prosthetic AV graft is indicated when ALL of the following apply:

-   Patient has ESRD or advanced CKD as described above with need for hemodialysis
-   Autogenous AV fistula is not feasible because one or more of the following apply:
    -   Venous mapping demonstrates no suitable autogenous vein in either upper extremity (veins smaller than 2.0 mm, non-compressible, or previously thrombosed throughout the forearm and upper arm)
    -   Patient has had multiple failed autogenous fistulas and remaining venous anatomy is exhausted
    -   Patient's life expectancy or vascular anatomy makes fistula creation clinically inappropriate per documented multidisciplinary assessment
    -   Urgent dialysis initiation is required and time to fistula maturation (typically 4 to 6 months) is not clinically acceptable
-   No functioning permanent access is available
-   Lower extremity graft placement is documented as necessary when upper extremity sites are exhausted

INDICATIONS — ACCESS MAINTENANCE AND INTERVENTION

Diagnostic Fistulogram (CPT 36901)

A diagnostic fistulogram (contrast angiography of the dialysis access circuit including inflow artery, access conduit, and venous outflow to the central veins) is indicated when ANY of the following clinical or hemodynamic criteria are present:

Physical examination findings:

-   Decreased or absent thrill or bruit over the access
-   Increased pulsatility of the access (suggesting outflow obstruction)
-   Ipsilateral arm, hand, or breast swelling (suggesting central venous stenosis or occlusion)
-   Development of new superficial collateral venous channels consistent with venous outflow stenosis or central venous obstruction
-   Distal ischemia or steal syndrome (pain, paresthesias, pallor, or tissue loss in the access-limb hand)
-   Ischemic monomelic neuropathy in the access extremity
-   Symptomatic aneurysm or pseudoaneurysm of the access conduit

Hemodynamic and dialysis-related criteria:

-   Unexplained decrease in delivered Kt/V or urea reduction ratio (URR) below target on two consecutive treatments without other explanation
-   Static or dynamic venous pressures persistently elevated above acceptable thresholds on two or more consecutive dialysis sessions (dynamic venous pressure greater than 150 mmHg at a blood pump speed of 200 mL/min, or static venous pressure ratio greater than 0.5)
-   Measured access blood flow (Qa) less than 600 mL/min for AV fistula or less than 800 mL/min for AV graft, OR a decrease in Qa of more than 25% from a prior baseline measurement over a monitoring period
-   Difficulty with needle placement or inability to achieve prescribed blood flow rates on two or more consecutive treatments
-   Failure of an autogenous AV fistula to mature within 4 to 6 weeks after surgical creation (failure-to-mature), defined as inability to achieve a flow rate of at least 600 mL/min, a luminal diameter of at least 6 mm, and a depth less than 6 mm suitable for cannulation

Access thrombosis:

-   Thrombosed AV fistula or AV graft with loss of thrill and bruit, confirmed clinically or by duplex ultrasound, where salvage is clinically appropriate and technically feasible

Therapeutic Interventions on the AV Access Circuit

Percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA) of dialysis circuit (CPT 36902) and/or stent placement (CPT 36903) are indicated when diagnostic fistulogram or prior imaging demonstrates ANY of the following in the setting of clinical or hemodynamic dysfunction as described above:

-   Stenosis of 50% or greater diameter reduction in the access vein, anastomosis, or venous outflow, accompanied by at least one hemodynamic or clinical indicator of dysfunction (elevated venous pressures, reduced access flow, decreased Kt/V, abnormal physical exam)
-   Central venous stenosis (subclavian, innominate, or superior vena cava) of 50% or greater with ipsilateral symptoms (arm edema, collateral formation, reduced access function)
-   Anastomotic stenosis causing measurable reduction in access flow or dialysis adequacy
-   Post-thrombectomy stenosis identified as a contributing anatomic lesion

Thrombectomy (CPT 36904, 36905, 36906) is indicated when:

-   Complete access thrombosis has occurred and the access is deemed salvageable based on the anatomic configuration, prior access longevity, and absence of infection
-   No active local or systemic infection contraindicates the procedure
-   Adequate residual venous outflow exists to support restored access function

Surgical revision or open thrombectomy (CPT 36831, 36832, 36833) is indicated when:

-   Percutaneous intervention has failed or is technically not feasible
-   Anatomic lesion (juxta-anastomotic stenosis, graft kinking, vein transposition is needed) is best corrected surgically
-   Access salvage requires interposition graft segment replacement or anastomotic revision

Stent placement in the outflow vein or central vein (CPT 36903, 36908) is indicated when:

-   Stenosis recurs within 3 months of technically successful PTA (elastic recoil or rapid restenosis pattern)
-   Flow-limiting dissection occurs as a complication of PTA
-   Symptomatic central venous stenosis of the subclavian, innominate, or superior vena cava is present with documented failure of PTA alone

Embolization or ligation for steal syndrome or access aneurysm (CPT 36909) is indicated when:

-   Hemodynamically significant steal syndrome causes rest pain, ischemic ulceration, or gangrene in the access limb and conservative or surgical banding has been considered
-   Large or symptomatic access aneurysm or pseudoaneurysm poses risk of rupture, skin breakdown, or infection
-   DRIL (distal revascularization-interval ligation) procedure is planned for high-flow steal not amenable to banding

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

For access creation:

-   Nephrology or treating physician note confirming ESRD or CKD stage with current eGFR or creatinine, dialysis modality selection, and estimated timeline to dialysis initiation
-   Preoperative duplex vein mapping report documenting vein diameters and compressibility, artery patency and diameter, and identification of the planned access site
-   Surgical consultation note documenting clinical rationale for access type (autogenous fistula versus prosthetic graft), planned configuration, and reason if autogenous fistula is not selected
-   Documentation of absence of a functioning existing permanent access, or rationale for new access creation when a prior access is present but non-functional
-   For prosthetic graft: explicit documentation that autogenous fistula is not feasible with supporting anatomic findings from venous mapping

For access maintenance and intervention:

-   Clinical notes from dialysis unit or treating provider documenting at least two of the following: elevated venous pressures, reduced access flow measurements, decreased dialysis adequacy (Kt/V or URR), difficulty with needle placement, or abnormal physical examination findings
-   Measurement of access blood flow (Qa) by dilution technique or ultrasound if available, with comparison to prior baseline
-   Static or dynamic venous pressure logs from the dialysis unit demonstrating the threshold abnormalities described under indications
-   For failure-to-mature: operative note from fistula creation with date of surgery, plus follow-up documentation at 4 to 6 weeks confirming inadequate development
-   For thrombosis: clinical confirmation of access occlusion (absence of thrill/bruit; duplex ultrasound if performed)
-   For central venous stenosis: prior imaging or clinical documentation of ipsilateral central venous catheter history, which is a major risk factor for central venous stenosis
-   Ordering provider attestation that the intervention is intended to restore or preserve a functioning permanent hemodialysis access
-   For stent placement: documentation of prior PTA at the same lesion site with restenosis within 3 months, or of procedural complication requiring stent

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Routine surveillance fistulograms (contrast angiography) performed solely on the basis of a monitoring schedule, without any supporting clinical, physical examination, or hemodynamic findings of dysfunction, are not covered. Surveillance programs based on access flow monitoring or venous pressure monitoring are supported, but an intervention trigger must be documented before proceeding to fistulogram.
-   PTA or stent placement performed on a stenosis of less than 50% diameter reduction without accompanying hemodynamic or clinical evidence of dysfunction is generally not covered.
-   Stent placement as a first-line intervention without a prior attempt at PTA is not covered except in cases of flow-limiting dissection or when PTA is technically not feasible and documented as such.
-   Creation of a new access when a functioning permanent access is present and capable of delivering adequate dialysis is not covered unless there is documented clinical rationale (planned surgical procedure requiring access site, access infection requiring removal, access retirement for medical reasons).
-   Simultaneous bilateral upper extremity AV access creation is generally not covered without compelling documented justification.
-   Access creation or maintenance for peritoneal dialysis patients or for purposes other than hemodialysis is not covered under this indication.
-   Tunneled central venous catheter (CVC) placement is addressed under a separate guideline and is distinct from the permanent AV access creation covered here; CVC placement as a bridge to permanent access is not covered under these CPT codes.
-   Interventions on a permanently abandoned or infected access that is not planned for salvage are excluded.
-   Repeat thrombectomy procedures for repeatedly thrombosing access without documented evaluation and treatment of the underlying anatomic etiology (stenosis) are subject to additional review.
-   PTA or stent placement performed prophylactically on a hemodynamically insignificant lesion identified incidentally on imaging without any clinical or physiologic dysfunction is not covered.
