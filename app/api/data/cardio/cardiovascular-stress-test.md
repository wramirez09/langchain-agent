MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

A cardiovascular stress test (exercise treadmill ECG test or pharmacologic stress ECG without imaging) is a non-invasive diagnostic study used to evaluate for myocardial ischemia, arrhythmia provoked by exertion, functional capacity, and hemodynamic response to exercise. Coverage is based on Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC) published by the American College of Cardiology (ACC) and American Heart Association (AHA). All clinical criteria below are substantiated by evidence-based literature; where published AUC scores are available they are noted.

A cardiovascular stress test without imaging is considered medically necessary when one or more of the following indications are met, adequate documentation is provided, and there are no applicable exclusions. The indication must reflect a specific clinical question that the test result is reasonably expected to answer and that will affect patient management.

INDICATIONS FOR CARDIOVASCULAR STRESS TEST

Symptoms Suggestive of Myocardial Ischemia — Suspected Coronary Artery Disease

-   Chest pain or anginal equivalent (exertional dyspnea, jaw pain, left arm pain, pressure, or tightness) with intermediate pretest probability of obstructive CAD in a patient who:
    -   Has an interpretable resting ECG (no LBBB, Wolff-Parkinson-White pattern, electronically paced rhythm, ST depression greater than 1 mm at rest, or LVH with repolarization changes), and
    -   Is able to exercise to an adequate workload (target heart rate achievable; functional capacity sufficient to perform treadmill or cycle ergometry protocol) (AUC Score 8)
-   Asymptomatic patient with intermediate-to-high clinical risk who is scheduled to begin an unsupervised vigorous exercise program, when the resting ECG is interpretable and the patient can exercise (AUC Score 7)
-   When a clear non-cardiac explanation for symptoms has been established and documented, exercise stress testing for suspected ischemia is not required

Known or Established Coronary Artery Disease — New or Worsening Symptoms

-   New onset or worsening exertional chest pain or anginal equivalent in a patient with known CAD (prior MI, prior PCI or CABG, or angiographically documented stenosis) when:
    -   Resting ECG is interpretable, and
    -   The patient is able to exercise
-   Change in symptom pattern (frequency, duration, or severity) suggesting destabilization of previously stable ischemic heart disease, when further evaluation by stress testing will guide change in management

Risk Stratification After Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)

-   Predischarge or early post-discharge (typically 3 to 10 days after hospital presentation) risk stratification following low- to intermediate-risk ACS (unstable angina or NSTEMI) in patients who:
    -   Were managed with an initial conservative (non-invasive) strategy,
    -   Have not undergone coronary angiography,
    -   Are clinically stable without rest pain, hemodynamic compromise, or signs of heart failure, and
    -   Have an interpretable ECG and adequate functional capacity to exercise
-   Following STEMI or high-risk ACS managed conservatively, when the patient is stable and coronary anatomy has not been fully evaluated by angiography

Preoperative Cardiac Evaluation Before Elevated-Risk Surgery

-   Preoperative evaluation is indicated when all of the following are present:
    -   The planned surgical procedure is an elevated-risk non-cardiac surgery (major vascular, thoracic, abdominal, or orthopedic surgery associated with a greater than 1% risk of perioperative major adverse cardiac event)
    -   The patient has at least one active cardiac condition or clinical risk factor (known CAD, history of congestive heart failure, cerebrovascular disease, diabetes mellitus requiring insulin, or preoperative creatinine greater than 2.0 mg/dL as defined by the Revised Cardiac Risk Index)
    -   Functional capacity is unknown, poor, or unable to be assessed clinically (estimated less than 4 METs), meaning the patient cannot climb one flight of stairs, walk on level ground at 4 mph, or perform equivalent activities of daily living without symptoms
    -   The test result will change perioperative management (e.g., further revascularization, change in anesthetic plan, or cancellation of the procedure)
-   Exercise stress testing without imaging is appropriate in this setting when the resting ECG is interpretable and the patient can exercise adequately; pharmacologic stress testing may be substituted when exercise capacity is insufficient

Evaluation Before Initiation of a Vigorous Exercise Program in Selected Higher-Risk Patients

-   Prior to initiation of a vigorous or high-intensity unsupervised exercise program in patients with known CAD or multiple cardiovascular risk factors when:
    -   Clinical evaluation alone is insufficient to stratify exercise safety,
    -   The treating clinician has documented a specific clinical question the test will answer, and
    -   The resting ECG is interpretable and exercise capacity is adequate (AUC Score 7)
-   Note: routine pre-exercise testing of asymptomatic, low-risk individuals is not supported

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

-   Detailed description of the presenting symptoms including onset, character, duration, provocating and relieving factors, and functional impact
-   Pretest probability of CAD or clinical risk assessment (e.g., Diamond-Forrester or ACC/AHA pretest probability framework) with documentation of risk factors (age, sex, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, family history of premature CAD)
-   Current resting 12-lead ECG confirming interpretability (no LBBB, WPW, electronically paced rhythm, resting ST depression greater than 1 mm, or LVH with repolarization abnormality)
-   Assessment of the patient's functional capacity confirming ability to exercise to an adequate workload, or clinical documentation of inability to exercise justifying pharmacologic stress modality
-   For known CAD: documentation of prior diagnosis (prior MI, PCI, CABG, or catheterization report), current symptom status, and current medical therapy
-   For post-ACS risk stratification: admission and discharge documentation, selected conservative management strategy, and absence of coronary angiography during index hospitalization
-   For preoperative evaluation: name and date of planned surgical procedure, AHA/ACC surgical risk classification, Revised Cardiac Risk Index calculation, and documented clinical rationale for how the test result will change management
-   For pre-exercise program evaluation: documentation of exercise program type, individual risk factors, and clinical question the test will answer
-   Current medication list, including antianginal medications (beta-blockers, nitrates, calcium channel blockers), which may affect test interpretation
-   Prior cardiac imaging or stress test results if available, with dates

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

General Exclusions

-   A clear, well-documented non-cardiac explanation for the patient's symptoms is present; in this case, stress testing for suspected ischemia is not required
-   Routine screening of asymptomatic low-risk individuals without symptoms or significant risk factors; stress testing is not appropriate for general cardiovascular health screening
-   Patient has known severe left main or three-vessel CAD with planned catheterization already scheduled; proceeding to definitive testing is appropriate and exercise stress testing is redundant
-   Patient is unable to complete an adequate exercise workload and there is no clinical indication for pharmacologic stress ECG testing without imaging
-   Resting ECG is uninterpretable for ischemia due to LBBB, electronically paced rhythm, WPW, or resting ST depression greater than 1 mm; in these patients, a stress test with imaging (nuclear, echo, or CMR) is required to assess for ischemia

Contraindications to Exercise Stress Testing

-   Acute myocardial infarction within 2 days
-   Active unstable angina with ongoing rest pain not yet stabilized
-   Uncontrolled symptomatic cardiac arrhythmia causing hemodynamic compromise
-   Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis
-   Decompensated symptomatic heart failure
-   Acute pulmonary embolism, pulmonary infarction, or deep vein thrombosis
-   Acute myocarditis, pericarditis, or endocarditis
-   Acute aortic dissection
-   Resting systolic blood pressure greater than 200 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure greater than 110 mmHg (relative contraindication; physician judgment applies)
-   Physical disability precluding safe performance of exercise on a treadmill or cycle ergometer

Scope Limitations

-   The exercise treadmill ECG test without imaging is not indicated when imaging is clinically required to localize ischemia, quantify myocardial territory at risk, or assess viability
-   Not appropriate as a repeat or surveillance study in asymptomatic patients with known CAD who are stable and asymptomatic on current medical therapy, without a new clinical question
-   Not covered as routine annual surveillance of stable, treated CAD without new symptoms or change in clinical status
-   Not appropriate for evaluation of acute-onset chest pain in the emergency department setting in patients with intermediate-to-high risk ACS features; dedicated acute coronary syndrome pathways apply
-   Pharmacologic stress ECG alone (without imaging) has limited diagnostic value in most settings and may not meet medical necessity criteria without clear documentation; pharmacologic stress testing is generally paired with an imaging modality when the ECG is uninterpretable
