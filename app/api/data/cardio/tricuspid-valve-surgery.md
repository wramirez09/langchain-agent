MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Tricuspid valve surgery encompasses repair (preferred when feasible) and replacement of the tricuspid valve for significant regurgitation or stenosis. Surgery is considered medically necessary when the clinical presentation meets one of the established indication categories below, consistent with ACC/AHA guidelines for the management of patients with valvular heart disease. Tricuspid valve repair is strongly preferred over replacement whenever the valve anatomy is favorable, given lower operative mortality and superior long-term durability.

Prior authorization approval requires that at least one of the following clinical indication categories is met, that the procedure is performed at an appropriate surgical facility, and that supporting documentation is submitted as specified.

INDICATIONS

Indication 1 — Concomitant tricuspid valve surgery at the time of left-sided valve surgery

Tricuspid valve surgery is indicated for patients undergoing left-sided valve surgery (mitral or aortic) when any of the following is present:

-   Severe tricuspid regurgitation (TR), regardless of symptoms.
-   Moderate TR with tricuspid annular dilation (annular diameter 40 mm or greater by 2D echocardiography, or 21 mm per m² body surface area or greater), even in the absence of symptoms.
-   Mild or moderate TR with prior evidence of progressive annular dilation or progressive RV enlargement on serial imaging.
-   Less-than-severe TR with a tricuspid annular diameter of 40 mm or greater on 2D transthoracic echocardiography (TTE), particularly when pulmonary hypertension or atrial fibrillation is present.

Indication 2 — Symptomatic severe primary (organic) tricuspid regurgitation

Isolated tricuspid valve surgery is indicated for patients with:

-   Severe primary TR (etiology includes rheumatic, myxomatous, carcinoid, radiation-induced, traumatic, or infective endocarditis) causing symptoms (NYHA functional class III or IV) refractory to guideline-directed medical therapy.
-   Symptoms attributable to TR must be documented (dyspnea on exertion, fatigue, lower-extremity edema, ascites, or hepatic congestion) with confirmation that symptoms persist despite optimization of diuretic and neurohormonal therapy.

Indication 3 — Progressive TR with right ventricular dilation or dysfunction

Isolated tricuspid valve surgery is reasonable for patients with:

-   Severe primary TR who are asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic but have progressive RV dilation or dysfunction on serial echocardiographic assessment (right ventricular end-diastolic area index greater than 20 cm²/m², or RVEF less than 45% by cardiac MRI), prior to the development of irreversible RV failure.
-   Moderate-to-severe or severe functional (secondary) TR with significant RV dilation on two consecutive studies obtained at least 6 months apart, when the TR is the primary driver of progressive RV remodeling and not fully explained by the degree of pulmonary hypertension alone.

Indication 4 — Severe TR with signs of right heart failure refractory to medical therapy

Isolated tricuspid valve surgery is indicated for patients with:

-   Severe TR (primary or functional) and at least two of the following clinical signs of right heart failure that persist despite maximal medical therapy (at least 3 months of optimized diuretic regimen with documented trial of an aldosterone antagonist):
    -   Peripheral edema refractory to diuretics.
    -   Ascites requiring paracentesis.
    -   Hepatic congestion with elevated bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, or gamma-glutamyl transferase attributable to venous congestion.
    -   Elevated central venous pressure by clinical examination (jugular venous distension above 10 cm H₂O).
    -   Renal impairment attributable to cardiorenal syndrome in the setting of elevated right-sided filling pressures.

Indication 5 — Symptomatic severe tricuspid stenosis

Tricuspid valve surgery (repair or replacement) is indicated for patients with:

-   Severe tricuspid stenosis defined as a mean gradient of 5 mmHg or greater across the tricuspid valve, or a tricuspid valve area of 1.0 cm² or less, producing symptoms (NYHA class II or greater) refractory to diuresis.
-   Isolated tricuspid balloon valvotomy may be considered as an alternative for patients with predominant tricuspid stenosis without significant TR and favorable valve anatomy; documentation of anatomic suitability is required.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

Clinical and imaging documentation must include all applicable items:

-   Complete transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) report within 6 months of the request, including:
    -   Quantification of TR severity (vena contracta width, effective regurgitant orifice area [EROA], regurgitant volume, proximal isovelocity surface area [PISA] when obtainable).
    -   Tricuspid annular diameter in the apical four-chamber view.
    -   Assessment of right ventricular size (RV end-diastolic diameter or area index) and systolic function (TAPSE, RV S', fractional area change, or estimated RVEF).
    -   Estimated right ventricular systolic pressure (RVSP) and presence or absence of pulmonary hypertension.
    -   Left ventricular size and function (LVEF, LV volumes) to assess for left-sided etiology of pulmonary hypertension.
    -   For tricuspid stenosis: mean gradient, valve area, and qualitative description of leaflet morphology.
-   Cardiac MRI (if available and clinically obtained), reporting RV volumes and ejection fraction, for cases where echocardiographic image quality is suboptimal or where RV function is borderline.
-   Right heart catheterization (RHC) data, when available, reporting right atrial pressure, right ventricular end-diastolic pressure, pulmonary artery pressures, pulmonary capillary wedge pressure, and cardiac output.
-   Documentation of symptom severity using NYHA functional classification, with clinical notes describing functional limitations.
-   Documentation of prior medical therapy and its adequacy:
    -   For patients with heart failure symptoms: current diuretic regimen (loop diuretic dose, aldosterone antagonist use, duration of therapy).
    -   For patients with atrial fibrillation: rate or rhythm control strategy and duration.
-   For functional (secondary) TR: documentation of underlying etiology (mitral valve disease, left heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, atrial fibrillation), and whether the primary left-sided or pulmonary pathology has been optimally treated.
-   Surgical consultation note from a cardiac surgeon with board certification or subspecialty training in cardiothoracic surgery, confirming operative candidacy and documenting operative risk using the Society of Thoracic Surgeons (STS) predicted risk of mortality score.
-   For concomitant procedures: operative plan clearly describing the planned left-sided valve procedure and the anticipated tricuspid intervention.
-   Serial echocardiographic reports (minimum two studies at least 3 to 6 months apart) when the indication is based on progressive RV dilation or dysfunction over time.
-   For tricuspid stenosis: documentation of attempted balloon valvotomy candidacy assessment when applicable.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Tricuspid valve surgery is not covered as a primary procedure for mild or moderate TR in the absence of hemodynamic significance, symptoms attributable to TR, or annular dilation meeting thresholds specified above.
-   Tricuspid valve surgery for functional TR secondary to untreated or undertreated left-sided heart disease or reversible pulmonary hypertension is excluded unless the left-sided condition has been optimally managed and residual significant TR persists.
-   Isolated tricuspid valve surgery for asymptomatic severe primary TR with normal RV size and function is not routinely covered; cases must document clinical rationale based on current guideline Class IIb or better evidence.
-   Transcatheter tricuspid valve interventions (edge-to-edge repair, transcatheter replacement) are subject to separate review criteria; this policy applies to open surgical repair and replacement only.
-   Prophylactic or incidental tricuspid annuloplasty in the absence of significant TR or measurable annular dilation at the time of non-cardiac thoracic surgery is excluded.
-   Repeat tricuspid valve surgery for structural valve deterioration of a prior bioprosthetic replacement is subject to case-by-case review with documentation of prosthetic valve failure by echocardiography or other imaging.
-   Surgery is excluded when the patient's operative risk is prohibitive (STS predicted mortality greater than 15% for isolated TV surgery in the absence of compelling clinical circumstances) and less-invasive alternatives have not been evaluated.
-   Infective endocarditis involving the tricuspid valve requires separate documentation per institutional protocol; intravenous drug use as the precipitating factor requires documentation of addiction treatment enrollment or evaluation.
