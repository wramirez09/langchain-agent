MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Carotid endarterectomy (CEA) is an open surgical procedure in which atherosclerotic plaque is removed from the internal carotid artery (ICA) or common carotid artery to reduce the risk of ipsilateral ischemic stroke. Medical necessity is established when clinical and imaging criteria from SVS and AHA/ASA guidelines are satisfied, the procedure is performed at a center with documented acceptable perioperative stroke and death rates, and the expected benefit of stroke prevention outweighs procedural risk.

CEA is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following general requirements are met:

-   Imaging confirmation of carotid stenosis by one or more accepted modalities (duplex ultrasonography, computed tomography angiography [CTA], magnetic resonance angiography [MRA], or catheter-based digital subtraction angiography [DSA]) with stenosis graded using NASCET (North American Symptomatic Carotid Endarterectomy Trial) criteria
-   The operating surgeon or center has a documented combined perioperative stroke and mortality rate that meets guideline-acceptable thresholds:
    -   3% or less for symptomatic patients
    -   3% or less for asymptomatic patients (ideally less than 3% to preserve net benefit)
-   The patient has an acceptable surgical risk profile and life expectancy sufficient to derive net benefit (generally at least 3 to 5 years for asymptomatic patients)
-   Evaluation by an appropriately qualified vascular surgeon, neurosurgeon, or vascular specialist has been completed
-   Best medical therapy (antiplatelet therapy, statin, antihypertensive, and lifestyle modification) has been considered and is not a contraindication to proceeding


INDICATIONS FOR CAROTID ENDARTERECTOMY

Symptomatic Carotid Stenosis

Symptomatic stenosis is defined as ipsilateral neurological or ocular ischemic events — including ischemic stroke, transient ischemic attack (TIA), or amaurosis fugax — occurring within the prior 6 months and attributable to the stenotic carotid artery.

-   Stenosis 70% to 99% (severe symptomatic stenosis, NASCET criteria)
    -   CEA is strongly indicated and provides the greatest absolute risk reduction
    -   Benefit is maximal when surgery is performed within 2 weeks of the qualifying event (SVS/AHA-ASA)
    -   AUC: 9 (appropriate without reservation)

-   Stenosis 50% to 69% (moderate symptomatic stenosis, NASCET criteria)
    -   CEA is indicated in carefully selected patients with recent qualifying ipsilateral ischemic event
    -   Benefit is greater in men, in patients older than 75 years, and in patients with more recent events
    -   Patient-level surgical risk and center outcomes must support expected net benefit
    -   AUC: 7 (generally appropriate)

-   Near-occlusion (string sign) — stenosis approaching 99% with thread-like residual lumen
    -   Benefit is less certain than for 70–99% stenosis; individualized risk-benefit assessment is required
    -   CEA may be appropriate when the vessel has not already undergone complete thrombotic occlusion
    -   AUC: 6 (uncertain/borderline)

Asymptomatic Carotid Stenosis

Asymptomatic stenosis is defined as ipsilateral carotid stenosis without any attributable neurological or ocular ischemic events within the prior 6 months.

-   Stenosis 70% to 99% (severe asymptomatic stenosis)
    -   CEA is indicated in patients with acceptable surgical risk and sufficient life expectancy to accrue benefit
    -   Patient must be able to tolerate general or regional anesthesia without prohibitive risk
    -   Life expectancy should be at least 3 to 5 years (AHA-ASA recommendation)
    -   Best medical therapy must be concurrently optimized
    -   AUC: 7 (generally appropriate in suitable surgical candidates)

-   Stenosis 60% to 69% (moderate-to-severe asymptomatic stenosis)
    -   CEA may be considered when the patient is young enough to benefit and perioperative risk is low
    -   Shared decision-making incorporating plaque morphology, contralateral disease, and patient preference is required
    -   AUC: 6 (uncertain; individualized decision)

-   Contralateral occlusion with ipsilateral significant stenosis
    -   CEA on the stenotic side may be appropriate when stenosis meets symptomatic or asymptomatic thresholds above, understanding that contralateral occlusion increases surgical complexity and risk
    -   Careful perioperative planning is required; risk-benefit ratio must be explicitly documented


REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation must be submitted with the prior authorization request:

-   Qualifying clinical indication:
    -   For symptomatic patients: date, type, and laterality of the qualifying neurological or ocular ischemic event (stroke, TIA, or amaurosis fugax); neurological evaluation note confirming ipsilateral attribution; event within 6 months of planned procedure
    -   For asymptomatic patients: documentation confirming absence of qualifying ipsilateral ischemic events within the prior 6 months

-   Imaging confirmation of stenosis degree:
    -   Duplex carotid ultrasonography report with ICA peak systolic velocity (PSV) and ICA/CCA velocity ratio, or
    -   CTA neck or MRA neck report with percent stenosis stated using NASCET methodology, or
    -   Catheter-based DSA report for cases where non-invasive imaging is discordant or technically limited
    -   When two non-invasive modalities yield discordant results, a third modality or DSA is required before proceeding

-   Surgical risk assessment:
    -   Anesthesia risk classification (ASA physical status or equivalent)
    -   Cardiac risk documentation (recent ECG, echocardiogram, or stress test if clinically indicated)
    -   Comorbidity list including conditions that may elevate surgical risk (contralateral carotid occlusion, prior neck irradiation, prior ipsilateral CEA, high cervical bifurcation, or hostile neck)

-   Center/operator outcomes:
    -   Documented institutional or surgeon perioperative combined stroke and death rate meeting the guideline-defined threshold (3% or less for symptomatic and asymptomatic patients)

-   Concurrent medical therapy documentation:
    -   Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin alone or dual antiplatelet) prescribed and being administered
    -   High-intensity statin therapy prescribed
    -   Blood pressure management plan documented

-   Life expectancy and functional status (for asymptomatic patients):
    -   Clinician attestation that the patient has a life expectancy of at least 3 to 5 years and functional status sufficient to benefit from stroke prevention


LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Symptomatic stenosis less than 50% (NASCET): CEA is not indicated; the perioperative risk exceeds the long-term benefit in this range, and best medical therapy is preferred.

-   Asymptomatic stenosis less than 60%: CEA is not routinely indicated; medical management is preferred.

-   Complete chronic carotid occlusion: CEA is not applicable; there is no residual lumen amenable to endarterectomy. Revascularization is not indicated and may increase risk of intracerebral hemorrhage.

-   Acute stroke in progress or evolving: CEA is generally not performed acutely during an established completed stroke with significant neurological deficit; emergent CEA in the acute setting requires individualized specialist assessment and is not covered under routine elective PA.

-   Prohibitive surgical risk: CEA is not indicated when the patient's perioperative combined stroke and death risk is estimated to exceed the natural history stroke risk of the stenotic lesion (generally defined as perioperative risk exceeding 6% for symptomatic patients or 3% for asymptomatic patients).

-   Conditions precluding expected benefit:
    -   Life expectancy less than 3 years due to non-vascular comorbidity (for asymptomatic stenosis)
    -   Severe ipsilateral intracranial disease that would limit benefit from extracranial revascularization
    -   Severe cognitive impairment where functional stroke prevention is not meaningful

-   Bilateral simultaneous CEA: Not covered as a single procedure in the same operative session without exceptional individualized clinical justification.

-   Carotid artery stenting (CAS) versus CEA: This guideline addresses CEA specifically; CAS (CPT 37215, 37216) is governed by separate criteria and is generally preferred in high-surgical-risk patients. Coverage as CEA will not extend to stenting procedures.

-   Repeat ipsilateral CEA for restenosis: May be covered when stenosis meets symptomatic or severe asymptomatic thresholds, but carotid artery stenting is often the preferred modality for restenosis after prior CEA; individualized documentation required.

-   Prophylactic CEA before major cardiac surgery (e.g., CABG) in asymptomatic patients: Coverage requires documentation meeting standard asymptomatic criteria above; routine pre-CABG CEA for mild-to-moderate asymptomatic stenosis is not covered.
