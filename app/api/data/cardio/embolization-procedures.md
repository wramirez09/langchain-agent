MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

General clinical criteria for any requested intervention:

-   Diagnosis must be confirmed, or the pretest likelihood established,
    > using a complete evaluation.

    > Evaluation should include:

    -   History and physical examination

        > Relevant laboratory studies, when applicable

        > Relevant diagnostic testing, when applicable

        > Response to prior therapeutic intervention, when applicable

    > Expected benefit must outweigh potential harms, including harms
    > from delay or decreased access.

    > Current guidelines, literature, and standards of care should
    > support the intervention as offering the best net benefit among
    > alternatives.

    > There should be a reasonable likelihood the intervention will
    > change management or improve outcome.

Procedure-specific criteria:

-   Prostate artery embolization:

    -   Medically necessary if either:

        -   Benign prostatic hypertrophy in a patient who is not a
            > surgical candidate and has moderate to severe lower
            > urinary tract symptoms

            > Hematuria of prostatic origin

    > Partial splenic artery embolization:

    -   Medically necessary for gastric variceal bleeding with imaging
        > evidence of chronic splenic vein occlusion.

    > Treatment of primary or metastatic liver malignancy:

    -   Includes TAE, TACE, and SIRT/TARE.

        > Medically necessary if either of the following applies:

        > Primary hepatocellular carcinoma or cholangiocarcinoma, in any
        > of the following:

        -   Unresectable disease when all are met:

            -   Nodule(s) unresectable due to size greater than 4 cm,
                > location adjacent to blood vessels, or multiplicity

                > Minimal or no extrahepatic disease

                > Preserved liver function, Child-Pugh class A or B

            > As a bridge to liver transplant

            > To downstage disease and make transplant eligibility
            > possible

            > Treatment of liver-related symptoms due to tumor bulk

        > Liver metastases, in any of the following:

        -   Liver-only or liver-predominant metastases from
            > neuroendocrine tumor or uveal melanoma

            > Secondary treatment of chemotherapy-resistant or
            > chemotherapy-refractory colorectal cancer with
            > liver-dominant metastases

            > Limited progressive hepatic metastatic disease
            > unresponsive to systemic therapy

            > Treatment of liver-related symptoms due to tumor bulk

    > Treatment of renal tumors:

    -   Medically necessary if either:

        -   Palliative treatment of renal cell carcinoma in patients
            > with local symptoms such as intractable hematuria, when
            > unable to undergo nephrectomy

            > Treatment of renal angiomyolipoma as an alternative to
            > resection or for acute hemorrhage

    > Uterine artery embolization:

    -   Medically necessary for uterine fibroids with:

        -   Excessive bleeding, or

            > Symptoms of pelvic discomfort

        > Symptom examples listed:

        -   Severe pain

            > Chronic lower abdominal pain

            > Low back pressure

            > Bladder pressure with urinary frequency not due to urinary
            > tract infection

    > Arterial aneurysm or malformation:

    -   Medically necessary for arterial aneurysms or malformations,
        > including:

        -   Congenital or acquired arterial malformations

            > Arteriovenous malformations

            > Arteriovenous fistulas

            > Pseudoaneurysms

    > Ovarian or internal iliac vein embolization:

    -   Medically necessary for symptomatic pelvic congestion syndrome
        > when imaging supports pelvic vein reflux.

    > Portal vein embolization:

    -   Medically necessary when hepatic metastases cannot be optimally
        > resected because remnant liver volume would be insufficient.

    > Transhepatic variceal embolization:

    -   Medically necessary in any of the following:

        -   Acute variceal bleeding

            > Cirrhosis with hepatocellular carcinoma

            > Child-Pugh class C liver disease

            > Branch portal vein thrombus

    > Venous malformations, not otherwise specified:

    -   Medically necessary for venous malformations, including:

        -   Congenital or acquired venous malformations

            > Venous hemangiomas

            > Capillary hemangiomas

NOT MEDICALLY NECESSARY / EXPLICIT NONCOVERED USES

-   Testicular (spermatic) vein embolization for varicocele is
    > considered not medically necessary.

    > Indications, diagnoses, or procedures not specifically addressed
    > are considered not medically necessary.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

General documentation requirements:

-   Clinical documentation supporting the request may be required.

    > Documentation must:

    -   Accurately reflect the clinical situation at the time of the
        > request

        > Clearly document the ordering provider's clinical intent

Documentation elements implied by the policy's clinical framework:

-   History and physical exam

    > Relevant laboratory results, when applicable

    > Relevant diagnostic testing, when applicable

    > Prior treatment history and response, when applicable

    > Information showing likely benefit versus risk

    > Evidence that the requested intervention is expected to change
    > management or improve outcome

Condition-specific documentation that should be present when relevant:

-   Imaging showing chronic splenic vein occlusion for splenic artery
    > embolization

    > Imaging showing pelvic vein reflux for ovarian/internal iliac vein
    > embolization

    > Information showing insufficient future liver remnant for portal
    > vein embolization

    > Liver tumor burden, resectability status, extrahepatic disease
    > status, and Child-Pugh class for liver malignancy cases

    > Documentation of inability to undergo nephrectomy for palliative
    > renal tumor embolization

    > Documentation of uterine fibroid symptoms or excessive bleeding
    > for uterine artery embolization

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Drug-eluting bead TACE (DEB-TACE) is not indicated.

    > Indications not specifically addressed are not medically
    > necessary.

    > Multiple interventions ordered at the same time may require
    > peer-to-peer review because appropriateness of additional
    > intervention often depends on the result of the first
    > intervention.

    > Repeat diagnostic testing of the same anatomic area for the same
    > indication may be subject to additional review or peer-to-peer in
    > these situations:

    -   Same facility repeat due to technical issues

        > Different facility repeat due to provider preference or
        > quality concerns

        > Persistent symptoms without clinical change, treatment, or
        > intervention since the previous study

        > Short-interval repeat by different providers for the same
        > patient

    > Repeat therapeutic intervention in the same anatomic area is
    > generally appropriate only if:

    -   The prior intervention was effective or beneficial, and

        > The expected duration of relief has lapsed

    > Repeat therapeutic intervention before the expected duration of
    > relief is not appropriate unless it is confirmed the prior
    > intervention was never administered.

    > Use of an FDA-approved product does not by itself establish
    > medical necessity or guarantee reimbursement.

    > Nonspecific or unlisted procedure codes may require additional
    > documentation and review.
