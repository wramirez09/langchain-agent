MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Surgical aortic valve replacement (SAVR) is a cardiac surgical procedure in which a diseased native aortic valve is excised and replaced with a prosthetic valve — either a bioprosthesis (tissue valve) or a mechanical valve — through an open sternotomy or minimally invasive approach. SAVR is the established definitive treatment for severe aortic stenosis (AS) and severe aortic regurgitation (AR) when valve dysfunction causes symptoms, impairs left ventricular function, or confers high risk of irreversible myocardial injury.

SAVR is considered medically necessary when all of the following conditions are met:

-   The patient has been evaluated by a qualified cardiologist or cardiothoracic surgeon and the diagnosis of severe aortic valve disease has been confirmed by echocardiography or invasive hemodynamic assessment.
-   A Heart Team evaluation has been performed or documented (for elective cases), confirming that surgical intervention is indicated and that SAVR is the preferred or appropriate approach over transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) or medical management alone, based on anatomy, patient age, comorbidities, and surgical risk profile.
-   At least one of the established clinical indications for aortic valve replacement (detailed below) is present, supported by objective hemodynamic or imaging criteria.
-   The patient has no absolute contraindications to open cardiac surgery and is deemed an acceptable surgical candidate by the treating surgical team.

INDICATIONS FOR AORTIC VALVE REPLACEMENT

**Symptomatic Severe Aortic Stenosis**

AVR is indicated (Class I) for all symptomatic patients with severe AS regardless of LVEF, provided surgical risk is not prohibitive:

-   Severe AS defined by one or more of the following hemodynamic thresholds:
    -   Aortic valve area (AVA) less than or equal to 1.0 cm²
    -   Indexed AVA less than or equal to 0.6 cm²/m²
    -   Mean aortic gradient greater than or equal to 40 mmHg
    -   Peak aortic jet velocity greater than or equal to 4.0 m/s
-   Symptoms attributable to AS, including any of the following:
    -   Angina (exertional chest pain)
    -   Syncope or presyncope (exertional or at rest)
    -   Heart failure symptoms (dyspnea, NYHA functional class II, III, or IV; exertional dyspnea; paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea; orthopnea)
-   SAVR is the preferred approach (over TAVR) in patients who are low or intermediate surgical risk, younger than approximately 65 years, have favorable surgical anatomy, or have anatomy unfavorable for TAVR (e.g., unfavorable access, small aortic annulus, bicuspid valve with complex morphology).

**Low-Flow, Low-Gradient Severe Aortic Stenosis with Reduced LVEF (Classical Low-Flow Low-Gradient AS)**

AVR is indicated for symptomatic patients with low-flow, low-gradient AS when the following criteria are met:

-   AVA less than or equal to 1.0 cm² with mean gradient less than 40 mmHg
-   LVEF less than 50%
-   Reduced stroke volume index (less than 35 mL/m²)
-   Low-dose dobutamine stress echocardiography (DSE) demonstrating:
    -   True severe AS (AVA remains less than or equal to 1.0 cm² with increasing flow), OR
    -   Aortic valve calcification with limited valve opening and a peak jet velocity above 4 m/s at any flow rate
-   Symptoms present (heart failure, angina, syncope)
-   The procedure carries acceptable surgical risk and adequate contractile reserve is demonstrated (increase in stroke volume of more than 20% on DSE) when feasible

**Paradoxical Low-Flow, Low-Gradient Severe AS with Preserved LVEF**

AVR may be considered for symptomatic patients with paradoxical low-flow, low-gradient severe AS when:

-   AVA less than or equal to 1.0 cm², mean gradient less than 40 mmHg, peak velocity less than 4 m/s
-   LVEF preserved (greater than or equal to 50%)
-   Stroke volume index less than 35 mL/m²
-   Severe valve calcification (e.g., Agatston calcium score greater than or equal to 2,000 AU in men, greater than or equal to 1,200 AU in women) supporting true severe AS
-   Symptoms present and attributable to AS after exclusion of other etiologies
-   Clinical judgment confirms the stenosis is the primary driver of symptoms

**Asymptomatic Severe Aortic Stenosis — Indications for Early Intervention**

AVR is recommended or reasonable (Class I or IIa) for asymptomatic patients with severe AS in the following circumstances:

-   LVEF less than 50%: AVR is indicated (Class I) for asymptomatic severe AS when resting LVEF is less than 50%, as LV systolic dysfunction is likely attributable to pressure overload from AS.
-   Very severe AS (peak jet velocity greater than or equal to 4.5 m/s or mean gradient greater than or equal to 50–60 mmHg, or AVA less than or equal to 0.6 cm²) in a low surgical risk patient: AVR is reasonable (Class IIa) given the high likelihood of rapid symptom onset and risk of sudden death.
-   Rapid hemodynamic progression in a low surgical risk patient: AVR is reasonable (Class IIa) when AVA is decreasing by more than 0.1–0.3 cm²/year, mean gradient is increasing by more than 5–7 mmHg/year, or jet velocity is increasing by more than 0.3 m/s/year.
-   Exercise stress testing reveals symptoms or hemodynamic abnormalities: AVR is reasonable (Class IIa) when a clinician-supervised exercise test in an asymptomatic patient with severe AS unmasks exertional symptoms, a drop in systolic blood pressure below baseline, or significant ST-segment changes attributable to AS.
-   Concomitant cardiac surgery: AVR is indicated (Class I) for patients with severe AS (AVA less than or equal to 1.0 cm², mean gradient greater than or equal to 40 mmHg, or jet velocity greater than or equal to 4 m/s) undergoing cardiac surgery for another indication (e.g., CABG, aortic surgery, mitral valve surgery). AVR is reasonable (Class IIa) for moderate AS (AVA 1.0–1.5 cm², mean gradient 20–40 mmHg) at the time of other cardiac surgery when the valve is likely to progress to severe status in the near term.
-   Biomarker elevation in asymptomatic severe AS: AVR may be considered when serum BNP is significantly elevated (greater than 3 times normal) in the absence of other explanatory causes.

**Severe Aortic Regurgitation (AR) — Symptomatic**

AVR is indicated (Class I) for symptomatic patients with severe AR regardless of LVEF:

-   Severe AR defined by echocardiographic and Doppler criteria:
    -   Regurgitant fraction greater than or equal to 50%
    -   Regurgitant volume greater than or equal to 60 mL/beat
    -   Effective regurgitant orifice area (EROA) greater than or equal to 0.30 cm²
    -   Color Doppler vena contracta width greater than or equal to 0.6 cm
    -   Holodiastolic flow reversal in the descending aorta
-   Symptoms attributable to AR: dyspnea (NYHA class II, III, or IV), angina, or heart failure
-   Note: when LVEF is severely reduced (less than 25–30%) and/or end-systolic dimension is markedly enlarged (greater than 7.5 cm), operative risk is substantially elevated and Heart Team discussion is required.

**Severe Aortic Regurgitation — Asymptomatic with LV Dysfunction or Dilatation**

AVR is indicated or reasonable for asymptomatic patients with severe AR when:

-   LVEF less than 55% (less than or equal to 50% per some guideline statements): AVR is indicated (Class I) when LVEF declines below this threshold on serial imaging even in the absence of symptoms.
-   Severe LV dilatation: AVR is reasonable (Class IIa) for asymptomatic patients with preserved LVEF who have progressive severe LV dilatation:
    -   LV end-systolic dimension (LVESD) greater than 50 mm (or greater than 25 mm/m² indexed to body surface area)
    -   LV end-diastolic dimension (LVEDD) greater than 65 mm when the surgical risk is low
-   Concomitant cardiac surgery: AVR is indicated (Class I) for patients with severe AR undergoing cardiac surgery for another indication.

**Moderate Aortic Stenosis or Regurgitation at the Time of Concomitant Cardiac Surgery**

-   Moderate AS (mean gradient 20–40 mmHg or jet velocity 3.0–3.9 m/s) at the time of another required cardiac surgical procedure: AVR is reasonable given the likelihood of progression and the avoidance of reoperation.
-   Moderate AR at the time of cardiac surgery for another indication: valve repair or replacement is reasonable (Class IIa) when severe AR progression is anticipated.

**Infective Endocarditis Involving the Aortic Valve**

Surgical intervention is indicated for aortic valve endocarditis under the following circumstances (Class I indications):

-   Valve dysfunction resulting in acute heart failure or cardiogenic shock
-   Fungal or highly resistant organism endocarditis (e.g., MRSA, gram-negative bacilli) not likely to be eradicated with antibiotics alone
-   Persistent bacteremia or fever lasting more than 5–7 days despite appropriate antimicrobial therapy
-   Evidence of perivalvular extension (abscess, pseudoaneurysm, fistula, new heart block)
-   Recurrent embolic events despite appropriate antibiotic therapy, when large mobile vegetations (greater than 10 mm) persist on the aortic valve
-   Prosthetic valve endocarditis (especially early prosthetic valve endocarditis within 1 year of implant) with valve dehiscence, obstruction, or abscess

**Bicuspid Aortic Valve Disease**

-   Patients with bicuspid aortic valve (BAV) and severe AS or severe AR meeting any of the above hemodynamic and symptomatic criteria are candidates for AVR per the same thresholds applied to trileaflet valve disease.
-   SAVR is generally preferred over TAVR in BAV patients given complex anatomy, risk of paravalvular leak, and coronary obstruction concerns with transcatheter approaches, particularly in younger patients.
-   Concomitant aortic root or ascending aortic repair should be considered when the aortic root or ascending aorta is greater than or equal to 4.5–5.0 cm in patients with BAV undergoing AVR.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following clinical documentation must be submitted to support medical necessity review:

-   **Cardiology or cardiothoracic surgery consultation note** documenting:
    -   Symptoms (type, duration, NYHA functional class)
    -   Physical examination findings relevant to aortic valve disease
    -   Clinical impression and rationale for surgical intervention
    -   Assessment of surgical risk (STS Predicted Risk of Mortality score or equivalent)
    -   Heart Team evaluation or multidisciplinary discussion note when applicable
-   **Echocardiogram report** (transthoracic echocardiography, TTE, or transesophageal echocardiography, TEE), obtained within a clinically appropriate interval, documenting:
    -   Aortic valve morphology and severity assessment (AVA by continuity equation, mean and peak gradients, peak jet velocity, dimensionless index)
    -   LVEF (quantitative, by Simpson's biplane or 3D method)
    -   LV dimensions (LVEDD, LVESD)
    -   AR severity grading using integrated quantitative and semi-quantitative Doppler parameters when AR is the primary indication
    -   For low-flow low-gradient AS: documentation of stroke volume index and, if performed, dobutamine stress echocardiography (DSE) report
-   **Cardiac catheterization report** (invasive hemodynamic assessment), if performed:
    -   Invasive hemodynamic confirmation of AS severity (Gorlin-derived AVA, simultaneous LV–aortic pressure tracings) when non-invasive data are discordant or inadequate
    -   Coronary angiography report to define coronary anatomy for operative planning
-   **Exercise stress test report** (if applicable): when performed to unmask symptoms in asymptomatic severe AS, documenting heart rate, blood pressure response, symptoms, and exercise capacity.
-   **Prior medical management records**: documentation that the patient's heart failure, angina, or related symptoms have been appropriately managed medically prior to surgical referral (unless urgent or emergent surgery is required), including diuretics, vasodilators, or guideline-directed medical therapy.
-   **Surgical risk assessment**: STS Predicted Risk of Mortality (STS PROM) score or equivalent, supporting that the patient is an acceptable surgical candidate (typically STS PROM less than 4–8% for SAVR; higher risk patients may still be candidates if TAVR is not anatomically feasible).
-   **Imaging for operative planning**: CT angiography of the chest/aorta if available, for aortic root and annular sizing, coronary anatomy, and surgical planning.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   SAVR is not indicated as a standalone procedure for mild or moderate aortic stenosis in the absence of concomitant cardiac surgery or other extenuating clinical circumstances.
-   SAVR is not covered for asymptomatic severe AS unless at least one of the evidence-based high-risk criteria (LVEF less than 50%, very severe hemodynamics at low surgical risk, rapid progression, abnormal exercise test, significant biomarker elevation, or concomitant cardiac surgery) is documented.
-   SAVR is not appropriate as primary therapy for cardiomyopathy or LV dysfunction when an alternative, non-valvular etiology is the primary driver and aortic valve disease is incidental or only mild to moderate in severity.
-   Medical management alone (vasodilators, diuretics, heart failure therapies) is not a substitute for timely surgical intervention in confirmed symptomatic severe AS or severe AR with LV dysfunction; however, pre-operative optimization with medical therapy is expected when clinically feasible.
-   SAVR is generally not preferred over TAVR in patients with severe AS who are at high or prohibitive surgical risk (STS PROM greater than 8–10% or equivalent frailty/comorbidity burden), unless anatomic factors preclude TAVR.
-   Redo SAVR (reoperation on a prior prosthetic aortic valve) requires documentation of prosthetic valve dysfunction (structural valve deterioration, prosthetic valve endocarditis, valve thrombosis, or paravalvular leak causing hemodynamic compromise) and surgical risk–benefit assessment.
-   Prophylactic AVR solely to prevent future progression, without meeting hemodynamic severity thresholds or clinical indications outlined above, is not considered medically necessary.
-   Aortic balloon valvuloplasty (BAV) is not a substitute for SAVR or TAVR; it may serve as a bridge to definitive valve replacement in hemodynamically unstable patients or as palliation in non-surgical candidates.
-   Coverage does not extend to valve replacement performed as part of purely investigational protocols or trials unless the payer has established separate investigational coverage provisions.
