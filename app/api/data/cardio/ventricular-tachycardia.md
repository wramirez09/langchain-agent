Medical Necessity Criteria

-   General clinical appropriateness requirements:

    -   Diagnosis or pretest likelihood must be established before
        > intervention based on a complete patient evaluation.

        > Evaluation should include history and physical examination
        > and, where applicable, review of relevant laboratory studies,
        > diagnostic testing, and response to prior therapeutic
        > intervention.

        > Expected benefit of the intervention must outweigh potential
        > harms.

        > Current literature, treatment guidelines, or standards of
        > practice should support the intervention as offering the
        > greatest net benefit among alternatives.

        > There should be a reasonable likelihood that the intervention
        > will change management and/or improve patient outcomes.

    > Transcatheter ablation for supraventricular tachycardia is
    > medically necessary when ANY of the following are present:

    -   Arrhythmia has caused sudden cardiac death, syncope, or
        > tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy.

        > Symptomatic arrhythmia requiring emergency room visit with
        > pharmacologic or electrical cardioversion.

        > Symptomatic arrhythmia that correlates temporally with
        > documented sustained SVT.

        > Individual planning pregnancy who has recurrent symptomatic
        > episodes or is pharmacologically controlled with the goal of
        > stopping medication.

        > Individual with congenital heart disease.

    > Transcatheter ablation of accessory pathway for asymptomatic
    > preexcitation is medically necessary when:

    -   Individual has a high-risk EP study.

    > Transcatheter ablation for ventricular arrhythmia is medically
    > necessary when ANY of the following apply:

    -   Recurrent symptomatic sustained VT in an individual with
        > structural heart disease, including prior myocardial
        > infarction, congenital heart disease, or hypertrophic
        > cardiomyopathy, despite or with contraindications to
        > antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Recurrent sustained monomorphic VT in an individual with
        > non-ischemic cardiomyopathy, despite or with contraindications
        > to antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Recurrent symptomatic sustained VT in an individual with
        > arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy, despite or
        > with contraindications to beta blockers.

        > Recurrent ICD shocks for ventricular tachycardia.

        > Symptomatic ventricular arrhythmia in an individual with
        > spontaneous type 1 Brugada syndrome who is not a candidate
        > for, or declines, ICD therapy.

        > Symptomatic outflow tract ventricular arrhythmia in an
        > otherwise normal heart despite, with contraindications to, or
        > after declining antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Symptomatic ventricular arrhythmia arising from the papillary
        > muscles despite, with contraindications to, or after declining
        > antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Idiopathic verapamil-sensitive sustained left VT related to
        > interfascicular reentry despite, with contraindications to, or
        > after declining antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Recurrent idiopathic VT or ventricular fibrillation triggered
        > by PVCs with a consistent QRS morphology.

        > Symptomatic frequent unifocal PVCs greater than 10% of total
        > beats despite, with contraindications to, or after declining
        > antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > LV systolic dysfunction due to frequent unifocal PVCs greater
        > than 20% of total beats despite, with contraindications to, or
        > after declining antiarrhythmic therapy.

        > Syncope due to VT.

        > Symptomatic individual with bundle branch reentrant VT.

Limitations and Exclusions

-   Atrial fibrillation ablation is not addressed in this document.

    > Surgical ablation for cardiac arrhythmia is outside the scope of
    > this document.

    > Ventricular arrhythmia ablation is generally reserved for
    > recurrent symptomatic arrhythmia despite pharmacologic management
    > or when such therapy is contraindicated or declined.

    > Pharmacologic management is defined broadly and includes treatment
    > of underlying precipitating conditions such as heart failure
    > optimization and correction of electrolyte imbalance.

    > The document notes uncertainty in some areas of practice,
    > including:

    -   What frequency of ICD-treated events should prompt referral for
        > ablation.

        > Whether ablation provides mortality benefit compared with ICD
        > alone.

        > Whether antiarrhythmic drugs can be stopped after ablation.

        > Ablation as an alternative to ICD has limited evidence and may
        > have a role only in limited situations or when ICD
        > implantation is contraindicated or refused.
