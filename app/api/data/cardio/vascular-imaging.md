MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

General clinical appropriateness requirements

-   Before any imaging/intervention, the clinician should establish the
    > diagnosis or pretest likelihood based on:

    -   History and physical examination

        > Relevant laboratory studies

        > Prior diagnostic testing

        > Response to prior therapy

    > The expected benefit should outweigh potential harms.

    > Current literature, guidelines, and standards of care should
    > support the requested study as the best option.

    > There should be a reasonable likelihood the imaging will change
    > management or improve outcome.

General vascular indications

-   Congenital/developmental vascular anomalies, not otherwise specified

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Traumatic vascular injury

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Vasculitis

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

Procedure-related imaging

-   Medically necessary for:

    -   Vascular evaluation before TAVI/TAVR or cardiac surgery

        > Vascular anatomic delineation before surgical/interventional
        > procedures not otherwise specified

        > Evaluation of suspected vascular complications after a
        > procedure

    > TAVI/TAVR or cardiac surgery:

    -   Duplex arterial ultrasound for carotid evaluation

        > CT/CTA chest, abdomen, pelvis

        > CTA neck requires initial duplex arterial ultrasound

        > MRA chest, abdomen, pelvis

        > MRA neck requires initial duplex arterial ultrasound

Brain, head, and neck

-   Intracranial aneurysm

    -   Screening if any high-risk criterion:

        -   Two or more first-degree relatives with intracranial
            > aneurysm or subarachnoid hemorrhage

            > Associated high-risk condition such as autosomal dominant
            > polycystic kidney disease or Ehlers-Danlos syndrome type
            > IV

            > Known fibromuscular dysplasia

        > Diagnosis if clinically suspected due to:

        -   CT/MRI suspicious for aneurysm

            > Neurologic signs/symptoms, including headache, plus
            > qualifying risk factors

            > Cranial nerve deficits

            > Focal neurologic deficits unexplained by CT/MRI

            > Headache with thunderclap onset, exertional/Valsalva
            > association, or persistent unexplained headache meeting
            > brain imaging criteria

        > Management of known aneurysm:

        -   New/worsening neurologic symptoms

            > Preoperative evaluation

            > Initial postoperative evaluation

        > Surveillance:

        -   6-12 months after diagnosis, then annually

    > AVM/AVF, head or neck

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Intracranial or extracranial dissection

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Fibromuscular dysplasia

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Intracranial hemorrhage

    -   Adult:

        -   Established hemorrhage with atypical features for
            > hypertensive hemorrhage, or

            > Subarachnoid hemorrhage suggested by lumbar puncture or
            > imaging

        > Pediatric:

        -   Intracerebral hemorrhage

    > Horner's syndrome

    -   Medically necessary when evaluating suspected vascular lesion.

    > Pulsatile tinnitus

    -   Medically necessary when evaluating suspected vascular lesion.

    > Intracranial stenosis/occlusion

    -   Suspected disease with:

        -   Predisposing congenital/genetic disease

            > Need to exclude tandem stenosis/occlusion before carotid
            > revascularization

            > Prior intracranial stenting

        > Known disease with new/progressive symptoms

        > Surveillance for established Moya Moya disease when
        > revascularization is being considered

    > Extracranial carotid stenosis/occlusion

    -   Screening:

        -   Starting 5 years after neck irradiation and every 3 years
            > thereafter

        > Suspected stenosis:

        -   Hollenhorst plaques or retinal neovascularity

        > Known stenosis:

        -   Worsening anterior-circulation neurologic symptoms/signs

        > Surveillance:

        -   Moderate stenosis (50%-69%): every 12 months

            > Severe stenosis (70%+): every 6 months

        > Post-revascularization:

        -   Baseline, every 6 months for 2 years, then annually

        > CTA/MRA neck only when duplex cannot be performed/is
        > nondiagnostic or shows moderate-severe stenosis/occlusion

    > Vertebral/basilar stenosis or occlusion

    -   Suspected posterior-circulation symptoms

        > Syncope only after exclusion of valvular heart disease and
        > rhythm disturbance

        > Subclavian steal syndrome

        > Known disease with worsening posterior-circulation neurologic
        > symptoms/signs

    > Stroke/TIA, intracranial evaluation

    -   No prior intracranial imaging since event, with:

        -   Acute/subacute stroke or TIA within 30 days

            > Chronic stroke/TIA with symptoms attributable to posterior
            > circulation

    > Stroke/TIA, extracranial evaluation

    -   No carotid imaging since event, with:

        -   Acute/subacute stroke/TIA within 30 days

            > Chronic anterior-circulation symptoms in carotid
            > revascularization candidates

            > Chronic posterior-circulation symptoms other than syncope

    > Intracranial venous thrombosis/compression

    -   Suspected venous sinus thrombosis with headache, visual changes,
        > eye pain, or neurologic symptoms plus one of:

        -   Papilledema, cranial nerve palsy, or focal deficit

            > Risk factor for venous thrombosis

            > Elevated D-dimer

            > Suspicious/nondiagnostic CT or MRI

        > History of venous sinus thrombosis with recurrent symptoms

        > Follow-up of known venous sinus thrombosis

        > Exclusion of venous compression by adjacent intracranial mass

    > Extracranial venous thrombosis/compression

    -   Medically necessary after nondiagnostic venous ultrasound.

Chest

-   Acute aortic syndrome

    -   Initial diagnosis of suspected disease

        > Management of known disease

        > Annual surveillance of clinically stable disease

    > Aortic aneurysm

    -   Screening:

        -   Annual evaluation for connective-tissue disease or genetic
            > mutations predisposing to aneurysm, as alternative to echo
            > or when echo is nondiagnostic

        > Diagnosis:

        -   Suspected thoracic aneurysm based on symptoms/signs or
            > nondiagnostic prior imaging

        > Management:

        -   New/progressive symptoms or enlargement

            > 6-month follow-up of newly diagnosed aneurysm

            > Endoleak evaluation

            > Pre-procedure planning

        > Surveillance:

        -   Annual if 4.4 cm or smaller

            > Every 6 months if larger than 4.4 cm

    > Atheromatous disease

    -   Adult only

        > Evaluation of thoracic aorta as source of distal emboli when
        > cardiac source not identified on echocardiography.

    > Pulmonary hypertension

    -   Suspected pulmonary hypertension, including CTEPH

        > Disease extent after diagnosis of CTEPH in surgical candidates

    > Pulmonary embolism

    -   Adult:

        -   Wells score likely (\>4), or

            > Wells score unlikely (≤4) with positive D-dimer, or

            > Pregnancy when PE cannot be excluded by YEARS algorithm:

            -   D-dimer 1000 ng/mL or more, or

                > D-dimer 500 ng/mL or more plus clinical DVT signs
                > after normal compression ultrasound, hemoptysis, or PE
                > as most likely diagnosis

        > Pediatric:

        -   Moderate/high clinical suspicion

            > Concern for recurrent embolism despite adequate therapy

    > Other chest vascular indications

    -   Hematoma

        > Pulmonary AV malformation

        > Pulmonary sequestration

        > Subclavian steal syndrome

        > Superior vena cava syndrome

        > Systemic venous thrombosis/occlusion

        > Thoracic outlet syndrome

Abdomen and pelvis

-   Acute aortic syndrome

    -   Initial diagnosis of suspected disease

        > Management of known disease

        > Annual surveillance of stable disease

    > AVM/AVF

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Hematoma/hemorrhage within abdomen or pelvis

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > IVC and iliac vein evaluation

    -   Suspected/established thrombus in abdomen or pelvis, including
        > IVC/iliac veins

        > Suspected/established IVC or iliac vein mass

        > Suspected/established external compression or stenosis of
        > IVC/iliac veins

        > To establish patency of IVC in patient with IVC filter

        > To evaluate tumor extension into IVC in renal/adrenal tumors

    > Mesenteric ischemia

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Portal hypertension

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

    > Renal artery stenosis / renovascular hypertension

    -   High pretest likelihood required, including:

        -   Refractory hypertension on 4 or more antihypertensives

            > Rapid/persistent worsening of previously controlled
            > hypertension

            > Hypertension developing before age 30

            > Hypertensive crisis

            > Hypertension with renal failure or progressive renal
            > insufficiency

            > Severe hypertension in patients over 55 with CKD or heart
            > failure

            > Hypertension with abdominal bruit suspected from renal
            > artery

            > Unexplained flash pulmonary edema

            > Unexplained atrophic kidney or renal size asymmetry \>1.5
            > cm on ultrasound

    > Stenosis/occlusion of abdominal aorta or branch vessels

    -   Suspected aortoiliac stenosis/occlusion with:

        -   Abdominal/femoral bruit

            > Decreased/absent femoral pulse

            > Atypical lower extremity claudication

            > Leriche syndrome

            > Atheroembolic lower-extremity signs such as
            > ischemic/discolored toes or livedo reticularis

            > Physiologic testing suggesting aortoiliac disease

            > Established femoral or popliteal artery aneurysm

        > Known stenosis:

        -   Presurgical evaluation when endovascular/surgical
            > intervention is being considered

        > Surveillance:

        -   Annual surveillance of surgical bypass grafts

    > Abdominal venous thrombosis/occlusion

    -   Hepatic/portal vein evaluation when venous duplex cannot be
        > performed or is nondiagnostic

        > Evaluation of other abdominal venous structures

    > Visceral artery aneurysm

    -   Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

Lower extremity

-   Peripheral arterial disease

    -   Additional imaging generally reserved for patients being
        > considered for revascularization

        > Surveillance after venous-graft surgical revascularization:

        -   Every 3 months for first 2 years, then annually

        > Surveillance after endovascular revascularization:

        -   Every 4 months during first year, then annually

    > Popliteal artery aneurysm

    -   Suspected aneurysm

        > Known aneurysm with symptoms suggesting change in size or
        > patency

        > Surveillance:

        -   Unrepaired aneurysm \<2 cm in revascularization candidates:
            > annually

            > After open/endovascular repair: 3, 6, and 12 months, then
            > annually

    > Vascular access complications

    -   Suspected complication with:

        -   Pulsatile mass, bruit, or thrill

            > Significant hematoma or abnormal skin changes

            > Severe pain

            > Signs of ischemia or embolism in involved extremity

    > Venous thrombosis/occlusion

    -   Medically necessary when venous ultrasound cannot be performed
        > or is nondiagnostic.

    > Other lower-extremity vascular indications

    -   Arterial entrapment syndrome

        > Aneurysm/dilation

        > AVM/AVF

        > Dissection/intramural hematoma

        > CTA abdominal aorta with bilateral runoff when disease
        > originates in abdominal aorta/branch vessels

MR angiography of the spinal canal

-   Medically necessary applications are limited to:

    -   Preoperative or postoperative imaging

        > Follow-up of prior imaging findings suggestive of a vascular
        > lesion

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

General documentation expected for requests

-   Complete history and physical exam

    > Evidence supporting the suspected diagnosis or pretest probability

    > Relevant lab studies, when applicable

    > Prior diagnostic testing and results

    > Response to prior therapy/intervention

    > Clinical rationale showing expected net benefit

    > Explanation of how imaging will change management or improve
    > outcome

    > Documentation must accurately reflect the clinical situation at
    > the time of request

    > Documentation must clearly show the ordering provider's clinical
    > intent

Condition-specific documentation commonly required, when applicable

-   Signs and symptoms supporting the vascular diagnosis

    > Prior imaging findings that were suspicious, abnormal, or
    > nondiagnostic

    > Duplex ultrasound or physiologic testing results when those are
    > first-line prerequisites

    > Stroke/TIA timing and whether prior imaging has already been
    > performed

    > Wells score, D-dimer, or YEARS algorithm elements for pulmonary
    > embolism evaluation

    > Risk factors such as family history, connective tissue disease,
    > congenital/genetic disorders, prior stenting, prior neck
    > irradiation, thrombosis risk factors, or prior interventions

    > Evidence of progression, recurrence, surveillance interval, or
    > postoperative/postprocedural status

    > If a study "cannot be performed or is nondiagnostic,"
    > documentation of why:

    -   Technical limitation

        > Persistent patient factor

        > Contrast allergy/nephrotoxicity

        > Radiation sensitivity

        > Lack of local availability/expertise

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

General limitations

-   Indications, diagnoses, or imaging modalities not specifically
    > addressed are considered not medically necessary.

    > Repeated testing of the same anatomic area for the same indication
    > should generally be limited to:

    -   Post-intervention evaluation, or

        > Change in clinical status requiring additional testing

    > Repeated testing may be subject to additional review when done
    > because of:

    -   Technical issues at same facility

        > Provider preference/quality concerns at a different facility

    > "Cannot be performed or is nondiagnostic" has specific meaning and
    > does not automatically justify repeat testing unless the
    > limitation is clinically relevant.

Modality-specific limitations

-   CTA limitations:

    -   Ionizing radiation

        > Iodinated contrast risks including allergy and renal
        > compromise

    > MRA limitations:

    -   Pacemakers/defibrillators or other implants

        > Claustrophobia

        > Possible sedation needs in pediatric patients

        > Gadolinium risk in advanced renal disease

        > Motion/respiratory artifact and longer acquisition times in
        > some settings

Specific exclusions / noncovered patterns noted in retrieved text

-   Procedure-related imaging excludes stenting or angioplasty of the
    > dural venous sinus.

    > Routine screening for asymptomatic carotid stenosis is not
    > recommended in low- or average-risk adults.

    > Carotid surveillance is not routinely recommended for less than
    > 50% stenosis.

    > Pulmonary embolism imaging is not indicated in low pretest
    > probability patients who meet rule-out criteria; imaging should
    > not be the initial test in low/intermediate probability patients.

    > MRA/MRI is not reliable as a stand-alone test to exclude acute
    > pulmonary embolism.

    > For pulmonary hypertension/CTEPH, routine advanced imaging is not
    > recommended in asymptomatic patients after VTE, and MRA is not
    > recommended for established chronic pulmonary thromboembolism
    > extent assessment.

    > PAD imaging is not indicated for asymptomatic PAD or intermittent
    > claudication when the patient is not a revascularization
    > candidate. Duplex should not be used as a screening tool or in
    > symptomatic patients with abnormal physiologic testing unless
    > revascularization is planned.

    > MR angiography of the spinal canal is limited to pre/postoperative
    > use or follow-up of prior suspicious findings.

BOTTOM-LINE SUMMARY

-   This is a vascular imaging medical-necessity guideline organized by
    > condition and body region.

    > Approval generally depends on clear clinical suspicion or
    > established disease, appropriate first-line workup, and evidence
    > that imaging will affect treatment decisions.

    > Duplex ultrasound and physiologic testing are often first-line;
    > CTA/MRA are commonly used when:

    -   Initial testing is nondiagnostic

        > The patient is a surgical/endovascular candidate

        > There is known disease requiring management or surveillance

        > Specific high-risk findings or symptoms are present
