MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Renal angiography (catheter-based) provides direct anatomic visualization of the renal arteries through selective arterial catheterization and contrast injection. Because it is an invasive procedure with exposure to iodinated contrast and ionizing radiation, as well as procedural risks including arterial access complications and atheroembolic events, it is reserved for situations in which non-invasive imaging — renal artery duplex ultrasonography, computed tomography angiography (CTA), or magnetic resonance angiography (MRA) — has been performed and is technically inadequate, inconclusive, or discordant with high clinical suspicion, or when catheter-based intervention is anticipated in the same session.

Renal angiography is considered medically necessary when at least one of the following clinical indications is met AND the documentation requirements below are satisfied:

1.  Resistant hypertension with suspected renovascular etiology:
    -   Uncontrolled arterial hypertension defined as blood pressure persistently greater than 140/90 mm Hg despite concurrent use of three or more antihypertensive agents at maximally tolerated doses, including a diuretic, consistent with guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT); AND
    -   Non-invasive imaging (duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA) is technically non-diagnostic, inconclusive, or yields results discordant with clinical suspicion for renal artery stenosis; OR non-invasive imaging demonstrates hemodynamically significant stenosis (typically greater than or equal to 60% luminal narrowing) and catheter-based intervention is being planned in the same session.

2.  Accelerated or malignant hypertension with suspected renovascular cause:
    -   Accelerated hypertension defined as sudden and persistent worsening of previously controlled hypertension associated with end-organ changes on examination or laboratory testing; OR
    -   Malignant hypertension defined as severely elevated blood pressure with associated hypertensive retinopathy (grade III or IV), hypertensive encephalopathy, or microangiopathic hemolytic anemia; AND
    -   Renovascular etiology is clinically suspected and non-invasive imaging has not provided a definitive diagnosis.

3.  Deteriorating renal function with suspected ischemic nephropathy:
    -   Progressive decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) of clinical significance, occurring spontaneously or precipitated by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) initiation, in a patient with established or strongly suspected bilateral renal artery stenosis or stenosis to a solitary functioning kidney; AND
    -   Non-invasive imaging is technically limited or inconclusive, or confirms significant stenosis with intervention anticipated.

4.  Recurrent flash pulmonary edema:
    -   Two or more episodes of acute pulmonary edema disproportionate to the degree of cardiac dysfunction, particularly in a hypertensive patient with known or suspected bilateral renal artery stenosis or high-grade stenosis to a solitary kidney; AND
    -   Non-invasive evaluation has been performed but is insufficient to guide management.

5.  Suspected fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD):
    -   Clinical suspicion for FMD based on presentation (young to middle-aged patient, predominantly female, with difficult-to-control hypertension, renal bruit, or spontaneous renal artery dissection); AND
    -   Non-invasive imaging (CTA or MRA) is non-diagnostic, technically inadequate for characterization of medial fibroplasia, or demonstrates findings suggestive of FMD where catheter angiography is required for definitive diagnosis and/or concurrent percutaneous transluminal renal angioplasty (PTRA); OR
    -   Catheter angiography is required for evaluation of extent of disease in preparation for intervention.

INDICATIONS FOR RENAL ANGIOGRAPHY

Patients are candidates for renal angiography when well-documented clinical evidence supports one of the following and the procedure is expected to inform or directly enable management:

-   Resistant hypertension (blood pressure greater than 140/90 mm Hg on three or more maximally tolerated antihypertensive agents, including a diuretic) with high pretest probability of renovascular hypertension based on clinical features such as:
    -   Onset of hypertension before age 30 or after age 55 without other cause identified
    -   Abdominal or flank bruit
    -   Asymmetry in kidney size (greater than 1.5 cm discrepancy) on imaging
    -   Unexplained hypokalemia or secondary hyperaldosteronism
    -   Worsening renal function on ACE inhibitor or ARB therapy

-   Accelerated hypertension (sudden, persistent worsening of previously controlled hypertension with new or worsening end-organ changes) when renovascular disease is suspected and non-invasive evaluation is non-diagnostic.

-   Malignant hypertension (severely elevated blood pressure with hypertensive retinopathy grade III or IV, encephalopathy, or microangiopathy) when renovascular cause is under evaluation.

-   Clinically significant decline in renal function attributable to suspected ischemic nephropathy from bilateral renal artery stenosis or stenosis to a solitary kidney, particularly when triggered or worsened by renin-angiotensin system blockade.

-   Recurrent flash pulmonary edema in a patient with severe hypertension and bilateral renal artery disease or equivalent anatomy, when non-invasive imaging is inadequate.

-   Fibromuscular dysplasia evaluation when CTA or MRA is technically non-diagnostic or when catheter angiography is required to characterize disease extent or to perform PTRA in the same session.

-   Pre-procedural mapping when renal artery revascularization (stenting or PTRA) is planned and cross-sectional imaging is insufficient for procedural planning.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

All of the following must be present in the medical record at the time of prior authorization request:

-   Clinician's note documenting the specific clinical indication, including:
    -   Documented blood pressure values and the number and names of antihypertensive agents prescribed at maximally tolerated doses (for resistant hypertension cases)
    -   Relevant laboratory data: serum creatinine, estimated GFR (eGFR), electrolytes, urinalysis, plasma renin activity or renin/aldosterone ratio if obtained
    -   Description of any episodes of acute pulmonary edema or accelerated/malignant hypertension with clinical context

-   Results of prior non-invasive renal artery imaging, documenting:
    -   Modality used (duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA)
    -   Date of imaging
    -   Technical adequacy and findings, including estimated percent stenosis and laterality if identified
    -   Reason the non-invasive result is inadequate, inconclusive, or discordant with clinical suspicion (if applicable)

-   Documentation that GDMT has been optimized or that optimization was contraindicated or not tolerated, including prior medication trials and tolerability issues.

-   If FMD is the indication: clinical narrative supporting FMD diagnosis including patient demographics, symptom history, and description of prior imaging findings.

-   Ordering clinician's stated intent: whether the angiography is diagnostic only or whether catheter-based intervention (PTRA, stenting) is anticipated in the same session.

-   For renal function indications: serial eGFR values demonstrating the trajectory of decline and, if applicable, documentation of acute decline following initiation of ACE inhibitor or ARB.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Renal angiography is not covered as a first-line diagnostic modality when non-invasive imaging (duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA) has not yet been attempted, unless the procedure is performed in an emergency setting or non-invasive imaging is contraindicated.

-   Not indicated for routine evaluation of hypertension when clinical features do not suggest a renovascular etiology and pretest probability of renal artery stenosis is low.

-   Not indicated for asymptomatic incidental renal artery findings without hypertension, renal function impairment, or other qualifying clinical indication.

-   Not indicated when the patient's comorbidities or clinical condition preclude revascularization regardless of angiographic findings, unless imaging will meaningfully guide non-interventional management.

-   Not indicated for surveillance or routine follow-up of known renal artery stenosis when clinical status is stable and revascularization is not being contemplated.

-   Not a substitute for non-invasive imaging when non-invasive modalities are technically feasible and adequate given the clinical question.

-   Contrast-induced nephropathy risk must be documented as acceptable or mitigated (adequate pre-hydration, iso-osmolar contrast, dose minimization); gadolinium-based contrast agents for MRA may be preferred over iodinated contrast in patients with borderline renal function when catheter angiography is not the selected modality.

-   Atheroembolic risk is elevated in the setting of extensive aortoiliac atherosclerosis; documentation of this risk acknowledgment is expected in high-risk patients.

-   Renal angiography for renal artery stenting in atherosclerotic renal artery stenosis is performed in the context of established indications for intervention; diagnostic angiography alone to confirm stenosis in a patient with stable blood pressure and preserved renal function on optimal medical therapy does not independently constitute a covered indication when non-invasive imaging has already characterized the lesion.
