MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Renal artery intervention (percutaneous transluminal renal angioplasty with or without stenting) is considered medically necessary when there is anatomically confirmed, hemodynamically significant renal artery stenosis and at least one of the following clinical indications is met, after an adequate trial of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) unless contraindicated or not tolerated.

Hemodynamically significant renal artery stenosis is defined as at least one of:

-   Angiographic diameter stenosis of 70% or greater, OR
-   Angiographic diameter stenosis of 50 to 69% with a resting translesional mean pressure gradient of 10 mmHg or greater (or systolic gradient of 20 mmHg or greater), OR
-   Duplex ultrasound demonstrating peak systolic velocity greater than 200 cm/s with a renal-to-aortic ratio greater than 3.5 consistent with severe stenosis

Stenting is preferred over angioplasty alone for atherosclerotic ostial renal artery stenosis given higher patency rates. Angioplasty alone (with selective stenting) is appropriate for fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD).

INDICATIONS FOR RENAL ARTERY INTERVENTION

Patients may be considered for renal artery intervention when there is well-documented evidence of hemodynamically significant RAS and one or more of the following clinically substantiated indications:

Resistant or Poorly Controlled Renovascular Hypertension

-   Hypertension that is resistant to maximally tolerated medical therapy, defined as uncontrolled blood pressure (systolic greater than 150 mmHg and/or diastolic greater than 90 mmHg) despite concurrent use of three or more antihypertensive agents at optimal doses including a diuretic, with documented adherence.
-   Hypertension that is inadequately controlled due to intolerance or contraindication to three or more antihypertensive medication classes required for blood pressure control.
-   Accelerated or malignant hypertension with evidence of end-organ injury attributable to renovascular etiology.
-   Fibromuscular dysplasia-related hypertension in younger patients (particularly women of childbearing age) where revascularization offers superior long-term blood pressure control compared to chronic pharmacotherapy.

Cardiac Disturbance Syndromes (AUC Score 9)

-   Flash pulmonary edema or acute coronary syndrome with hypertension and moderate to severe RAS with resting translesional mean gradient of 10 mmHg or greater.
-   Hemodynamically significant RAS with recurrent, unexplained congestive heart failure or sudden, unexplained pulmonary edema not adequately explained by primary cardiac etiology (AUC Score 6).
-   Hemodynamically significant RAS with unstable angina with a plausible renovascular contribution to hemodynamic instability (AUC Score 6).
-   Recurrent episodes of flash pulmonary edema in the setting of bilateral RAS or RAS to a solitary functioning kidney, where revascularization is expected to stabilize hemodynamics and reduce hospitalization burden.

Ischemic Nephropathy and Deteriorating Renal Function

-   Progressive decline in renal function (increase in serum creatinine of 30% or greater, or GFR decline of 25% or greater from baseline over 3 to 6 months) attributable to hemodynamically significant RAS and not explained by nephrotoxic agents, contrast nephropathy, or primary parenchymal disease.
-   Bilateral hemodynamically significant RAS or RAS to a solitary functioning kidney associated with chronic kidney disease (CKD stage 3 or worse) where revascularization is intended to preserve or recover renal function.
-   Acute kidney injury precipitated by initiation of an ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker in a patient with bilateral RAS or RAS to a solitary functioning kidney, confirming hemodynamic dependence on angiotensin II for glomerular filtration.
-   Renal atrophy (kidney length less than 8 cm) may indicate advanced irreversible parenchymal loss; however, revascularization may still be considered if there is evidence of preserved cortical viability on imaging (e.g., nuclear medicine renal scan demonstrating residual function).

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation must be present in the medical record to support authorization:

Diagnostic Imaging Confirming RAS

-   Imaging study (one or more) documenting renal artery stenosis severity:
    -   Duplex renal artery ultrasound with peak systolic velocity, end-diastolic velocity, renal-to-aortic ratio, and resistive indices
    -   CT angiography (CTA) of the renal arteries with percentage stenosis estimate
    -   MR angiography (MRA) of the renal arteries
    -   Diagnostic renal angiography with or without translesional pressure measurement
-   For hemodynamic significance in 50 to 69% stenosis: translesional mean pressure gradient documented at time of diagnostic angiography (resting gradient 10 mmHg or greater)

Clinical and Laboratory Documentation

-   Current and historical blood pressure readings, including home blood pressure log or ambulatory blood pressure monitoring if available
-   Complete antihypertensive medication list with doses, duration of therapy, and documented adherence or reasons for intolerance/contraindication
-   Serial serum creatinine and eGFR values (minimum two time points over 3 to 6 months) demonstrating stable, worsening, or recovering renal function
-   Urinalysis and urine protein-to-creatinine ratio to exclude primary glomerular disease
-   Renal size measurements bilaterally (by ultrasound, CT, or MRI) documenting kidney length and cortical thickness

Clinical Indication-Specific Documentation

-   For resistant hypertension: documentation of three or more antihypertensive agents at maximal tolerated doses including a diuretic, with adherence confirmed; documentation of secondary causes excluded (primary aldosteronism, sleep apnea, pheochromocytoma as clinically indicated)
-   For cardiac disturbance syndrome / flash pulmonary edema: hospital records, echocardiogram, and cardiology evaluation documenting recurrent episodes and ruling out primary cardiomyopathy as the sole etiology; cardiology attestation of hemodynamic contribution of RAS
-   For ischemic nephropathy: nephrology or vascular medicine consultation documenting the attributable relationship between RAS and renal function decline; ruling out primary parenchymal causes including biopsy if clinically indicated
-   For FMD: documentation of diagnosis (characteristic imaging appearance), patient age and sex, and clinical rationale favoring intervention over chronic antihypertensive therapy

Procedural Planning

-   Operator specialty qualifications and institutional procedural volume documentation where required by payer
-   Pre-procedural renal function (creatinine, eGFR) within 30 days
-   Contrast allergy premedication plan if applicable
-   Patient informed consent documentation

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

The following scenarios are generally not supported for renal artery intervention under commercial PA criteria:

-   Incidental or moderate RAS (less than 70% diameter stenosis) with well-controlled blood pressure on one to two antihypertensive medications and stable renal function. Observation and medical management are appropriate.
-   Atherosclerotic RAS in the absence of resistant hypertension, cardiac disturbance syndrome, or progressive ischemic nephropathy, where revascularization has not demonstrated superiority over GDMT alone (consistent with ASTRAL and CORAL trial findings).
-   Renal artery stenosis in the setting of end-stage renal disease on chronic dialysis without a specific plan or clinical rationale for renal function recovery or dialysis independence.
-   Severely atrophic kidney (length less than 7 cm bilaterally, or less than 8 cm for the sole functioning kidney) with no evidence of residual cortical viability or salvageable function on functional imaging; revascularization is unlikely to yield clinical benefit.
-   Renal artery stenosis due to non-atherosclerotic, non-FMD causes (e.g., Takayasu arteritis, neurofibromatosis) without specialist guidance from rheumatology or vascular surgery regarding disease activity and intervention timing.
-   Prophylactic or pre-emptive intervention in asymptomatic patients with incidentally discovered RAS and normal blood pressure, stable renal function, and no cardiac disturbance syndrome.
-   Repeat renal artery intervention (re-stenting or re-dilation) for in-stent restenosis without documentation of symptom recurrence or clinical deterioration meeting original medical necessity criteria.
-   Renal artery stenting as an adjunct to renal denervation procedures for hypertension outside of approved clinical trial protocols.
-   Intervention performed for cosmetic angiographic improvement or incidental imaging findings without clinical correlation.
