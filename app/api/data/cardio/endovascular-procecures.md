MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

General clinical requirements

-   Before any intervention, the record should support a confirmed
    > diagnosis or a strong pretest likelihood based on:

    -   history and physical exam

        > relevant laboratory data, if applicable

        > diagnostic testing

        > response to prior treatment

    > The expected benefit of the intervention should outweigh potential
    > harm.

    > The requested service should be supported by accepted practice
    > standards and current clinical evidence.

    > The intervention should be reasonably likely to change management
    > or improve outcomes.

Key treatment definitions used by the policy

-   Optimal medical therapy includes all applicable, unless
    > contraindicated:

    -   antiplatelet therapy

        > high-intensity statin

        > ACE inhibitor or ARB for patients with hypertension

        > treatment of diabetes and hypertension when present

        > tobacco cessation efforts; if the patient continues to smoke,
        > at least 2 cessation approaches should be tried

    > Structured exercise program:

    -   supervised or home-based

        > walking-based program intended to improve tolerance and
        > distance

        > at least 30 minutes per session

        > 3 times weekly

        > initial period of 12 weeks

    > Claudication is exertional muscle pain/cramping relieved by rest.

    > Critical limb ischemia/chronic limb-threatening ischemia includes:

    -   ischemic rest pain, or

        > nonhealing wounds for more than 2 weeks with abnormal
        > hemodynamics, or

        > gangrene

1.  Critical limb ischemia / chronic limb-threatening ischemia Balloon
    > angioplasty and/or stent placement of iliac, femoropopliteal,
    > and/or tibial vessels, or atherectomy of femoropopliteal and/or
    > tibial vessels, is medically necessary when any one of the
    > following is present:

-   Ischemic rest pain with both:

    -   foot pain that worsens with elevation and improves with
        > dependency

        > abnormal hemodynamic findings in the affected limb

    > Ischemic skin ulcer present for more than 2 weeks despite wound
    > care, with abnormal hemodynamic findings

    > Gangrene

2\. Claudication: balloon angioplasty and/or stent placement All of the
following must be met:

-   Significant lifestyle or work limitation due to claudication

    > No other condition significantly limiting walking

    > No improvement after at least 3 months of conservative therapy,
    > including both:

    -   structured exercise program

        > optimal medical therapy

    > Target lesion is in the aortoiliac or femoropopliteal vessels

    > If improvement is seen at 3 months, the policy directs an
    > additional 3 months of conservative therapy before
    > revascularization is considered

3\. Claudication: atherectomy All of the following must be met:

-   Significant lifestyle or work limitation due to claudication

    > No other condition significantly limiting walking

    > No improvement after at least 3 months of conservative therapy,
    > including both:

    -   structured exercise program

        > optimal medical therapy

    > Target lesion is in the femoropopliteal vessels

    > Atherectomy is being done because angioplasty or stenting in that
    > same vessel during the same procedure produced a suboptimal
    > result, defined by the policy as:

    -   flow-limiting dissection, or

        > more than 50% residual stenosis

    > If improvement is seen at 3 months, the policy directs an
    > additional 3 months of conservative therapy before
    > revascularization is considered

4\. Prior revascularization with restenosis Balloon angioplasty and/or
stent placement in iliac, femoropopliteal, and/or tibial vessels, and/or
atherectomy in femoropopliteal and/or tibial vessels, is medically
necessary in a previously treated segment when either applies:

-   Symptomatic patient with hemodynamically significant restenosis at
    > the prior endovascular treatment site

    > Symptomatic or asymptomatic patient with hemodynamically
    > significant stenosis in a venous or prosthetic bypass graft

5\. Treatment to obtain vascular access for another endovascular
procedure Balloon angioplasty and/or stent placement of the iliac
arteries is medically necessary when a stenotic iliac lesion must be
treated to enable access for another medically necessary vascular
procedure, such as:

-   percutaneous coronary intervention

    > endovascular aortic aneurysm repair

    > transcatheter valve repair or replacement

    > other medically necessary vascular procedures in the abdominal,
    > pelvic, or thoracic circulation

6\. Endovascular venous arterialization Medically necessary when all of
the following are supported:

-   critical limb ischemia

    > nonhealing ulcers

    > no other transcatheter treatment option

    > no surgical treatment option

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The policy does not provide a single formal checklist, but the following
documentation is clearly needed to support review:

-   History and physical exam

    > Diagnostic basis for peripheral arterial disease and the
    > indication for intervention

    > Relevant lab data when applicable

    > Diagnostic testing and hemodynamic evidence, such as abnormal ABI,
    > TBI, TcPO2, PVR, or Doppler findings

    > Description of symptoms and severity

    -   rest pain details for CLI/CLTI

        > ulcer duration and wound care history

        > presence of gangrene if applicable

        > degree of lifestyle or vocational limitation for claudication

    > Evidence that no other condition is the main cause of walking
    > limitation in claudication cases

    > Documentation of conservative treatment for claudication,
    > including:

    -   structured exercise program details

        > optimal medical therapy components tried or contraindications

        > duration of therapy, including at least 3 months before
        > intervention

    > For tobacco users, cessation treatment efforts

    > Lesion location and treated vascular territory

    > For atherectomy in claudication:

    -   proof that angioplasty or stenting in the same vessel during the
        > same procedure had a suboptimal result

        > residual stenosis percentage or documentation of flow-limiting
        > dissection

    > For restenosis cases:

    -   prior revascularization history

        > evidence of hemodynamically significant restenosis or bypass
        > graft stenosis

        > symptom status

    > For procedures done to gain access:

    -   stenotic iliac lesion

        > planned medically necessary downstream procedure requiring
        > access

    > For endovascular venous arterialization:

    -   critical limb ischemia

        > nonhealing ulcers

        > documentation that no other transcatheter or surgical option
        > exists

    > The submitted documentation must accurately reflect the patient's
    > condition at the time of request and clearly show the ordering
    > clinician's intent.

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

Explicit exclusions

-   Endovenous femoral-popliteal arterial revascularization with
    > transcatheter placement of intravascular stent is not medically
    > necessary for any indication.

    > Intravascular lithotripsy is not medically necessary for any
    > indication.

Procedure/location limitations

-   This guideline does not address acute limb ischemia; that condition
    > is treated as an emergency/inpatient issue.

    > For claudication, intervention in the anterior tibial artery,
    > tibioperoneal trunk, peroneal artery, posterior tibial artery, and
    > pedal arteries is not medically necessary.

    > For claudication, atherectomy is limited to femoropopliteal
    > disease and only when used as a secondary treatment during the
    > same procedure after a suboptimal angioplasty or stent result.

Additional review/appropriateness limitations

-   Multiple interventions requested at the same time may require
    > additional review or peer-to-peer discussion.

    > Repeat diagnostic testing may be subject to review when repeated
    > without clinical change, repeated due to provider preference,
    > repeated because of technical issues, or repeated by multiple
    > providers over a short time.

    > Repeat therapeutic intervention in the same area is generally
    > appropriate only if prior treatment was beneficial and the
    > expected period of benefit has ended; early repeat treatment is
    > not appropriate unless the prior treatment was never actually
    > given.
