MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

1.  General clinical appropriateness framework

-   Before any intervention, the clinician must confirm the diagnosis or
    > establish pretest likelihood based on a complete patient
    > evaluation.

    > The evaluation must include:

    -   History

        > Physical examination

        > Relevant laboratory studies, when applicable

        > Relevant diagnostic testing

        > Response to prior therapeutic intervention

    > The anticipated benefit of the intervention must outweigh
    > potential harms, including harms from delay or reduced access.

    > Current treatment guidelines, clinical literature, and standards
    > of care should support the intervention as offering the greatest
    > net benefit among alternatives.

    > There must be a reasonable likelihood the intervention will change
    > management or improve outcome.

2\. Definitions and prerequisite clinical criteria

-   Significant coronary artery stenosis is defined as any of the
    > following on invasive coronary angiography or CCTA:

    -   Left main coronary stenosis 50% or greater

        > Non-left-main epicardial coronary stenosis 90% or greater

        > 40% to 89% stenosis in any epicardial coronary artery with
        > either:

        -   FFR or FFR-CT 0.8 or less

            > iFR 0.89 or less

    > Low complexity coronary disease:

    -   Focal stenosis and/or SYNTAX score 22 or less

    > High complexity coronary disease:

    -   Diffuse stenosis(es) and/or SYNTAX score greater than 22

    > Unstable angina for elective outpatient coronary angiography
    > requires recent hospitalization for that condition.

    > In scenarios based on noninvasive testing, only testing performed
    > since the most recent PCI or CABG should be considered.

3\. Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) prerequisites

-   Risk factor management:

    -   Tobacco cessation/avoidance

        > Regular physical activity

        > Healthy weight maintenance

        > Healthy diet

        > Evidence-supported medications absent contraindication:

        -   Aspirin and/or P2Y12 antagonist

            > High-intensity statin at maximum tolerated dose

            > Ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitor, or bempedoic acid if statin
            > intolerance or LDL goal not reached

            > Beta blocker when indicated

            > ACE inhibitor or ARB when indicated

            > Antidiabetic therapy in diabetic patients

    > Symptom control:

    -   Beta blockers are first-line unless contraindicated

        > Calcium channel blockers and/or long-acting nitrates are
        > alternatives when beta blockers are contraindicated or not
        > tolerated, and are add-on options when symptoms persist

        > Ranolazine may be used when other antianginal therapy is
        > contraindicated/not tolerated or when symptoms persist despite
        > treatment

    > Revascularization is generally reserved for patients with
    > persistent unacceptable symptoms despite maximally tolerated
    > medical therapy.

4\. Noninvasive risk stratification criteria

High risk

-   Severe resting LV dysfunction with LVEF less than 35% not explained
    > by noncoronary causes

    > Resting perfusion abnormalities involving 10% or more of
    > myocardium without prior MI

    > Stress ECG findings with:

    -   ST depression 2 mm or more at low workload or persisting into
        > recovery

        > Exercise-induced ST elevation

        > Exercise-induced VT/VF

    > Severe stress-induced LV dysfunction:

    -   Peak exercise LVEF less than 45%, or

        > Drop in LVEF with stress 10% or more

    > Stress-induced perfusion abnormalities involving 10% or more of
    > myocardium or multiple vascular territories

    > Stress-induced LV dilation

    > Inducible wall motion abnormality involving more than 2 segments
    > or 2 coronary beds

    > Wall motion abnormality at low-dose dobutamine or low heart rate

    > CAC score greater than 400

    > Multivessel obstructive CAD 70% or greater, or left main stenosis
    > 50% or greater, on CCTA

Intermediate risk

-   Mild/moderate resting LV dysfunction with LVEF 35% to 49%

    > Resting perfusion abnormalities involving 5% to 9.9% of myocardium
    > without prior MI

    > ST depression 1 mm or more with exertional symptoms

    > Stress-induced perfusion abnormalities involving 5% to 9.9% of
    > myocardium or one vascular territory without LV dilation

    > Small wall motion abnormality involving 1 to 2 segments in 1
    > coronary bed

    > CAC score 100 to 399

    > One-vessel CAD with 70% or greater stenosis or moderate stenosis
    > in 2 or more arteries on CCTA

Low risk

-   Low-risk treadmill score 5 or greater, or no new ST changes or
    > exercise-induced chest pain at maximal exercise

    > Normal or small myocardial perfusion defect involving less than 5%
    > of myocardium

    > Normal stress or no change of limited resting wall motion
    > abnormalities during stress

    > CAC score less than 100

    > No coronary stenosis greater than 50% on CCTA

5\. Clinical indications for PCI in patients who have not undergone CABG

Non-left-main PCI: one or two vascular territories at a single sitting

-   Appropriate when there is significant stenosis of each vessel
    > treated and ANY of the following apply:

    -   A. Abnormal stress testing with ANY of:

        -   Persistence or recurrence of unacceptable ischemic symptoms
            > despite optimal medical therapy

            > NYHA class III or IV CHF

            > LVEF 35% or less

            > PCI within the past year

        > B. Newly recognized LV systolic dysfunction with LVEF 40% or
        > less when ischemia is thought to be the cause

        > C. STEMI within the preceding 45 days with residual
        > non-culprit stenosis not addressed during the STEMI admission

        > D. Anginal equivalent symptoms, heart failure, sustained
        > ventricular arrhythmia, or abnormal stress test within 90 days
        > of inpatient management of acute coronary syndrome when PCI
        > was not performed during the acute event

        > E. Abnormal CCTA with ANY of:

        -   Persistence or recurrence of unacceptable ischemic symptoms
            > despite optimal medical therapy

            > NYHA class III or IV CHF

            > LVEF 35% or less

            > PCI within the past year

        > F. Recently resuscitated from sudden cardiac death or
        > documented ventricular fibrillation or sustained ventricular
        > tachycardia

        > G. Prior cardiac transplant

Non-left-main PCI: three vascular territories at a single sitting

-   Appropriate in scenarios A-G above when there is significant
    > stenosis of each vessel treated and ANY of the following also
    > apply:

    -   Low complexity coronary disease

        > High complexity coronary disease with documentation of the
        > patient's refusal to undergo CABG

        > High complexity coronary disease with comorbid conditions
        > making CABG contraindicated

Left main PCI

-   PCI of an unprotected left main coronary artery is appropriate when
    > there is significant stenosis and EITHER:

    -   Documented refusal to undergo CABG, or

        > CABG is contraindicated due to comorbid conditions

Additional note

-   CABG should be considered as an alternative in patients with
    > two-vessel disease involving the proximal LAD, especially in
    > diabetic patients or those with significant LV systolic
    > dysfunction.

6\. Clinical indications for PCI in patients who have undergone CABG

CABG within the preceding year

-   ALL of the following are required:

    -   Ischemic symptoms, including symptomatic ventricular tachycardia

        > Stenosis 70% or greater in native vessel(s) or bypass graft(s)

        > Intermediate- or high-risk findings on noninvasive stress
        > testing

        > If stress testing was not performed or was indeterminate, FFR
        > 0.8 or less or iFR 0.89 or less may substitute

CABG more than one year previously

-   ALL of the following are required:

    -   Persistence or recurrence of unacceptable ischemic symptoms
        > despite optimal medical therapy, or symptomatic ventricular
        > tachycardia

        > Stenosis 70% or greater in native vessel(s) or bypass graft(s)

        > Abnormal noninvasive stress testing with low-, intermediate-,
        > or high-risk findings

        > If stress testing was not performed or was indeterminate, FFR
        > 0.8 or less or iFR 0.89 or less may substitute

7\. Other medically necessary scenarios

Patients scheduled for percutaneous valvular procedures such as
TAVR/TAVI or mitral valve repair

-   PCI is appropriate when ANY of the following apply:

    -   Left main CAD, except complex bifurcation disease

        > Non-complex triple-vessel disease, such as SYNTAX score less
        > than 33

        > Complex triple-vessel disease in a patient who is not a CABG
        > candidate

        > One- or two-vessel proximal CAD

Patients scheduled for renal transplantation, with low CAD complexity

-   PCI is appropriate when ANY of the following apply:

    -   Persistent symptoms despite maximal antianginal therapy

        > Persistent symptoms despite any antianginal therapy in
        > non-diabetic patients with intermediate- or high-risk findings
        > on noninvasive stress testing

        > Ischemic symptoms in non-diabetic patients not taking
        > antianginal therapy who have left main disease, triple-vessel
        > disease, or proximal LAD disease, with intermediate- or
        > high-risk findings on noninvasive stress testing

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

1.  Explicitly stated documentation requirements

-   Clinical documentation supporting the request may be required.

    > Documentation must:

    -   Accurately reflect the clinical situation at the time of the
        > requested service

        > Sufficiently document the ordering provider's clinical intent

    > If the required elements of appropriateness are not established,
    > peer-to-peer review will likely be needed.

2\. Clinical information that should be documented to support medical
necessity

-   Complete history and physical examination

    > Relevant laboratory data, if applicable

    > Diagnostic testing results

    > Response to prior therapy

    > Evidence that benefit outweighs harm

    > Support from accepted guidelines/literature/standard practice

    > Evidence that the intervention is likely to change management or
    > improve outcome

    > Coronary anatomy and stenosis severity from invasive angiography
    > or CCTA

    > FFR, FFR-CT, or iFR results when used to establish significant
    > stenosis or substitute for stress testing

    > Stress test findings and risk level when criteria depend on
    > abnormal, intermediate-, or high-risk testing

    > Documentation of GDMT and antianginal therapy, including failure,
    > intolerance, or persistence of unacceptable symptoms despite
    > therapy

    > LV ejection fraction

    > NYHA heart failure class where relevant

    > Timing of prior PCI, STEMI, ACS, or CABG

    > Presence of ventricular arrhythmia, sudden cardiac death
    > resuscitation, or prior cardiac transplant where relevant

    > Documentation of coronary complexity, including SYNTAX score when
    > three-vessel disease is being considered

    > Documentation of patient refusal of CABG or comorbid
    > contraindication to CABG when required

    > For valvular-procedure or renal-transplant scenarios,
    > documentation of the planned procedure and qualifying CAD
    > pattern/complexity

    > For repeat testing or simultaneous interventions, rationale
    > showing why additional or repeated intervention is necessary

    > For unstable angina in the elective outpatient setting,
    > documentation of recent hospitalization for that condition

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

1.  Explicit exclusion

-   Percutaneous transluminal coronary lithotripsy is considered not
    > medically necessary in all scenarios.

2\. Scope limitations

-   The guideline addresses elective PCI for stable ischemic heart
    > disease, not emergency PCI for acute coronary syndromes.

3\. Clinical limitations

-   In asymptomatic patients, coronary angiography with intent to
    > perform PCI is seldom justified.

    > For most stable CAD subgroups, revascularization has not been
    > shown to reduce mortality or myocardial infarction; its main
    > benefit is symptom relief.

    > Stable CAD patients with advanced chronic kidney disease have
    > increased revascularization risk and generally have not been shown
    > to benefit, except possibly some patients with left main CAD.

    > Advanced age, advanced malignancy, and coagulopathy are relative
    > contraindications.

    > Contrast allergy/atopy and renal impairment require special
    > consideration because of contrast-related risk.

    > Radiation exposure should be minimized because PCI uses
    > fluoroscopy.

4\. Testing and repeat-service limitations

-   Simultaneous ordering of multiple diagnostic or therapeutic
    > interventions will often require peer-to-peer review.

    > Repeat diagnostic testing of the same anatomic location for the
    > same indication should generally be limited to:

    -   Evaluation after an intervention, or

        > Change in clinical status requiring further management
        > decisions

    > Repeat diagnostic testing may require additional review or
    > peer-to-peer discussion when due to:

    -   Technical issues at the same facility

        > Request at a different facility based on provider preference
        > or quality concerns

        > Persistent symptoms without clinical change, treatment, or
        > intervention since the prior study

        > Same-anatomic-area testing by different providers over a short
        > time

    > Repeat therapeutic intervention in the same anatomic area is
    > generally appropriate only when the prior intervention was
    > effective and the expected duration of relief has lapsed.

    > A repeat therapeutic intervention requested before the expected
    > duration of relief is not appropriate unless it can be confirmed
    > the prior intervention was never administered.

5\. Procedure-selection cautions

-   CABG may be a better alternative in selected patients, including:

    -   Two-vessel disease involving the proximal LAD

        > Some diabetic patients

        > Some patients with significant LV systolic dysfunction

        > Complex multivessel disease

    > Separate diagnostic coronary angiography and PCI based only on
    > facility operational requirements should be avoided where
    > possible.

BOTTOM-LINE SUMMARY

-   Elective PCI is considered appropriate when there is documented
    > significant coronary stenosis and the patient meets one of the
    > guideline's scenario-based criteria, usually after appropriate
    > evaluation and medical therapy.

    > The strongest documentation themes are: objective confirmation of
    > disease severity, current symptoms and functional impact, prior
    > medical management, stress/imaging/hemodynamic findings, prior
    > revascularization history, and clear rationale for why PCI is
    > expected to improve management or outcome.
