MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI) is a non-invasive nuclear cardiology technique that uses radiotracers — typically technetium-99m or thallium-201 for SPECT, or rubidium-82 or N-13 ammonia for PET — to assess regional myocardial perfusion at stress and rest. MPI identifies areas of ischemia (reversible perfusion defect) and infarction (fixed perfusion defect), provides left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) and wall motion data, and guides decisions on medical therapy, revascularization, and risk stratification.

MPI is considered medically necessary when clinical information supports one of the recognized appropriate-use scenarios described below, and when the results are expected to meaningfully change or guide clinical management. When stress echocardiography (SE) is equally applicable and expected to yield optimal imaging, it is preferred over MPI due to lower cost and absence of ionizing radiation. MPI is indicated over SE when there is a documented clinical reason that SE is less suitable, is contraindicated, or is anticipated to yield suboptimal or equivocal results.

PET myocardial perfusion imaging is indicated when neither SE nor SPECT MPI has provided, or is expected to provide, optimal imaging due to patient- or technical-related factors (for example, morbid obesity, prior equivocal or non-diagnostic SPECT, large breasts or implants, mastectomy, chest wall deformity, pleural or pericardial effusion, or prior thoracic surgery).

All criteria are substantiated by evidence-based medical literature consistent with ACC/AHA/ASNC Appropriate Use Criteria. AUC scores are referenced where applicable.

INDICATIONS FOR MYOCARDIAL PERFUSION IMAGING

Symptomatic Patients Without Known CAD — Suspected CAD

-   Intermediate or high pretest probability of obstructive CAD based on clinical assessment (for example, Diamond-Forrester criteria or Duke Clinical Score), with typical or atypical anginal symptoms or anginal equivalent (exertional dyspnea, diaphoresis), when:
    -   The resting ECG is uninterpretable or expected to render exercise ECG non-diagnostic due to:
        -   Left bundle branch block (LBBB)
        -   Ventricular paced rhythm
        -   Pre-excitation (Wolff-Parkinson-White pattern)
        -   ST-segment depression greater than 1 mm at rest
        -   Left ventricular hypertrophy with repolarization abnormality
        -   Digoxin use with ST-segment changes
    -   The patient is unable to achieve adequate exercise workload (inability to exercise or inability to reach 85% of maximal predicted heart rate) due to orthopedic, neurological, vascular, or deconditioning-related limitations — requiring pharmacologic stress
    -   The patient has intermediate pretest probability and an interpretable ECG with ability to exercise, but MPI is requested for additional localization or when SE is not expected to provide adequate imaging (AUC Score 7 for equivocal imaging scenarios)
-   High pretest probability of CAD with symptoms, when non-invasive functional imaging is preferred over direct invasive angiography as the first-line evaluation
-   Low or intermediate pretest probability with inability to exercise (pharmacologic stress only), when functional ischemia testing is clinically indicated

Known or Established CAD — New, Worsening, or Recurrent Symptoms

-   New onset or worsening anginal symptoms or anginal equivalent in a patient with known CAD (prior MI, prior coronary angiography confirming stenosis, or prior revascularization), when:
    -   Stress ECG alone is non-diagnostic or uninterpretable (see ECG criteria above)
    -   Localization of ischemia is required to guide revascularization decision-making
    -   The patient is unable to exercise adequately
-   Change in symptom pattern (increased frequency, decreased threshold, rest symptoms) in established CAD where functional imaging is needed to guide management
-   Evaluation of patients with known multivessel CAD to determine physiologic significance of individual lesions or to guide the selection of revascularization target

Risk Stratification After Abnormal or Inconclusive Prior Testing

-   Abnormal or equivocal results on a prior stress ECG (exercise treadmill test), stress echocardiogram, or non-diagnostic nuclear test, when MPI is expected to provide incremental diagnostic or prognostic information
-   Coronary CT angiography (CCTA) demonstrating intermediate-severity stenosis (40–90%) where functional significance is not established and invasive FFR is not planned, and MPI is the preferred non-invasive functional test
-   Elevated coronary artery calcium score (CAC greater than 400 Agatston units, or CAC in the 75th percentile or higher for age/sex/race) in a symptomatic patient where functional ischemia assessment is warranted
-   Evaluation of known perfusion abnormality or known wall motion abnormality when clinical status or symptoms have changed and re-assessment is needed

Pre-operative Evaluation for Elevated-Risk Surgery

-   Pre-operative cardiac risk assessment in a patient scheduled for elevated-risk non-cardiac surgery (for example, major vascular surgery, thoracic surgery, intraperitoneal or intrathoracic procedures) when all of the following apply:
    -   The patient has poor functional capacity, defined as unable to perform activities requiring 4 or more metabolic equivalents (METs) without symptoms, AND
    -   The result is expected to change perioperative management (for example, cancellation or modification of surgery, initiation of additional medical therapy, or referral for revascularization), AND
    -   At least one of the following clinical risk factors is present based on the Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI) or equivalent validated tool:
        -   History of ischemic heart disease
        -   History of compensated or prior heart failure
        -   History of cerebrovascular disease
        -   Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus
        -   Preoperative serum creatinine greater than 2.0 mg/dL
        -   High-risk surgical procedure
-   Pre-operative MPI is not routinely indicated for low-risk surgery or for patients with good functional capacity (4 or more METs without symptoms), regardless of the presence of clinical risk factors

Post-Revascularization — Recurrent Symptoms or Suspected Restenosis

-   Recurrent or new symptoms (chest pain, exertional dyspnea, or anginal equivalent) following percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), when:
    -   Symptoms recur after an initial symptom-free period following revascularization, OR
    -   Symptoms fail to resolve as expected following revascularization
    -   Timing: generally at least 5 years after asymptomatic CABG, or earlier if new or worsening symptoms are present; for PCI, generally at least 2 years asymptomatic before routine surveillance, but symptomatic patients may be evaluated at any time
-   Detection of ischemia in a revascularized territory or non-revascularized territory to guide additional management in symptomatic patients

Equivocal or Expected Suboptimal SPECT Imaging — Upgrade to PET

-   All criteria for MPI are met AND there is clinical reason to expect equivocal or suboptimal SPECT imaging quality due to:
    -   Morbid obesity or high BMI
    -   Large breasts or breast implants
    -   Mastectomy with chest wall change
    -   Chest wall deformity
    -   Pleural or pericardial effusion
    -   Prior thoracic surgery
    -   Previously equivocal or non-diagnostic SPECT result in the same patient
-   (AUC Score 7 for upgraded modality in equivocal imaging scenarios)

Cardiac Sarcoidosis, Cardiomyopathy, and Selected Non-CAD Indications

-   Evaluation of known or suspected cardiac sarcoidosis when functional perfusion and metabolic assessment (FDG-PET in combination with perfusion PET or SPECT) is needed to detect active inflammation or scar
-   Risk stratification in patients with non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (LVEF less than 40%) when ischemic versus non-ischemic etiology is uncertain and coronary anatomy is known or has been recently assessed
-   Evaluation of myocardial viability in patients with known CAD and significant LV dysfunction (LVEF 35% or less) when revascularization is being considered and viability assessment will influence the decision (PET perfusion-metabolism protocol preferred)

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

-   Ordering clinician's office note or clinical summary documenting the clinical indication, symptom description (onset, character, frequency, exertional or rest, associated symptoms), and functional status (METs)
-   Relevant cardiac history including prior MI, prior coronary angiography or CCTA findings, prior revascularization (PCI or CABG with dates and vessels treated), and prior stress test results with dates
-   Current resting ECG with interpretation, or documentation explaining why resting ECG is uninterpretable or expected to be non-diagnostic on exercise
-   Reason for inability to exercise, if pharmacologic stress is to be used (for example, orthopedic limitation, neurological impairment, peripheral vascular disease, inability to achieve adequate heart rate)
-   Documentation of why stress echocardiography is not preferred or is expected to yield suboptimal imaging, when SPECT or PET MPI is requested in a scenario where SE would be a reasonable alternative
-   For pre-operative evaluation: surgical procedure planned with risk level, functional capacity assessment (METs), Revised Cardiac Risk Index or equivalent clinical risk factor documentation, and explanation of how MPI results will change perioperative management
-   For post-revascularization studies: prior revascularization records, prior post-procedure stress test results (if any), and description of recurrent or new symptom pattern
-   For PET upgrade from SPECT: prior SPECT result (if available) or specific patient factor(s) expected to cause suboptimal SPECT image quality
-   Current medication list including anti-anginal agents, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, nitrates, and antiplatelet therapy
-   For repeat MPI: prior MPI report(s) with dates and results, and documentation of clinical change (new symptoms, change in symptom pattern, clinical deterioration, or change in management plan) that justifies repeat testing

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   Routine screening of asymptomatic, low-risk individuals without known CAD or significant risk factors is not covered.
-   Pre-operative evaluation for low-risk elective surgery (for example, superficial surgery, endoscopic procedures, cataract surgery) is not supported, regardless of cardiac history.
-   Pre-operative evaluation in patients with good functional capacity (4 or more METs without cardiac symptoms) is not supported unless extraordinary clinical circumstances are documented and results are expected to alter management.
-   Routine surveillance MPI in asymptomatic patients after revascularization, without clinical change, is not covered. Repeat testing in the absence of new or worsening symptoms or a documented change in clinical status is not medically necessary.
-   Repeat MPI within 12 months of a prior study is generally not covered unless there is a documented significant change in clinical status, new symptoms, new revascularization, or a new clinical indication that was not present at the time of the prior study.
-   MPI for evaluation of palpitations, arrhythmia, or syncope in the absence of symptoms or signs suggesting ischemia is not a supported indication.
-   MPI is not appropriate as the sole evaluation for acute chest pain or suspected acute coronary syndrome (ACS) in the emergency setting; acute coronary syndrome evaluation follows separate clinical pathways.
-   MPI as a follow-up to a normal prior MPI, without interval change in symptoms or clinical status, is not supported.
-   MPI in patients with low pretest probability of CAD (asymptomatic with no risk factors, or atypical non-cardiac chest pain with low clinical suspicion) and a normal interpretable resting ECG and ability to exercise is not indicated; standard exercise ECG is the preferred initial test.
-   MPI is not covered for the purpose of reassuring an asymptomatic patient or for clearance for non-cardiac activities (for example, return to sports, life insurance, or occupational clearance) in the absence of a documented clinical indication.
-   Frequent serial MPI studies (more often than clinically indicated by guideline-supported intervals) to monitor stable known CAD without change in symptoms or management plan are not supported.
-   SPECT MPI and PET MPI should not be performed for the same clinical indication within the same authorization period without documentation of why both modalities are needed.
