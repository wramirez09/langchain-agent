MEDICAL NECESSITY CRITERIA

Transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) is a catheter-based procedure in which a prosthetic valve is delivered percutaneously (or via limited surgical access) and deployed within the native or bioprosthetic aortic valve to restore competent valve function. TAVR is indicated for patients with severe, symptomatic aortic stenosis who have been evaluated by a multidisciplinary heart team and determined to be appropriate candidates based on surgical risk, anatomy, and patient-specific factors.

TAVR is considered medically necessary when ALL of the following criteria are met:

-   Severe aortic stenosis confirmed by transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) meeting at least one of the following hemodynamic thresholds:
    -   Aortic valve area (AVA) 1.0 cm² or less, or indexed AVA 0.6 cm²/m² or less
    -   Mean aortic valve gradient 40 mmHg or greater, OR peak aortic jet velocity 4.0 m/s or greater
    -   For low-flow, low-gradient severe AS (AVA 1.0 cm² or less with mean gradient less than 40 mmHg): documented low-flow state (stroke volume index 35 mL/m² or less) with either reduced LVEF (classic low-flow) or preserved LVEF with small LV cavity (paradoxical low-flow), supported by multimodality imaging or dobutamine stress echocardiography as appropriate
-   Symptomatic aortic stenosis with symptoms directly attributable to AS, including at least one of the following:
    -   Exertional dyspnea or decreased exercise tolerance (NYHA functional class II, III, or IV)
    -   Heart failure symptoms or signs
    -   Exertional angina or anginal equivalent
    -   Exertional syncope or pre-syncope
    -   Abnormal hemodynamic response (fall or inadequate rise in blood pressure) on exercise testing
-   Heart team evaluation completed, including at minimum a structural/interventional cardiologist and a cardiac surgeon, with documented consensus that TAVR is the preferred treatment approach
-   Surgical risk assessment by the Society of Thoracic Surgeons Predicted Risk of Mortality (STS-PROM) calculator documented in the medical record; TAVR is supported across the following risk strata:
    -   Prohibitive surgical risk: STS-PROM 8% or greater, OR patient deemed inoperable due to comorbidities not fully captured by the STS score (for example, severe frailty, hostile chest, porcelain aorta, severe pulmonary hypertension, liver cirrhosis, extreme age with multiple comorbidities)
    -   High surgical risk: STS-PROM 8% or greater, or Heart Team consensus of high risk based on clinical factors
    -   Intermediate surgical risk: STS-PROM 4% to less than 8%, with Heart Team consensus that TAVR is equivalent or preferred
    -   Low surgical risk: STS-PROM less than 4%, in patients aged 65 or older, with anatomy favorable for TAVR and Heart Team consensus that long-term outcomes are expected to be equivalent to or better than surgical aortic valve replacement (SAVR)
-   Anatomic suitability confirmed by dedicated cardiac computed tomography angiography (CCTA) for annular sizing, access route assessment, and procedural planning
-   Absence of a valid contraindication to TAVR (see Limitations and Exclusions)

For valve-in-valve (ViV) TAVR (transcatheter valve replacement within a failed bioprosthetic aortic valve), TAVR is considered medically necessary when:

-   Structural valve deterioration of a previously implanted surgical or transcatheter bioprosthetic aortic valve is confirmed by echocardiography (stenosis, regurgitation, or mixed dysfunction)
-   Symptoms attributable to the failing bioprosthesis are present (NYHA class II or greater, or objective evidence of hemodynamic deterioration)
-   Heart team evaluation documents that ViV TAVR is preferred over redo surgical valve replacement based on surgical risk, anatomy, and prosthesis-patient interaction analysis
-   CCTA confirms adequate valve-in-valve geometry and the absence of coronary obstruction risk

INDICATIONS FOR TRANSCATHETER AORTIC VALVE REPLACEMENT

Patients are candidates for TAVR when there is well-documented clinical and imaging evidence supporting one of the following indications, and the Heart Team has confirmed that TAVR is the appropriate intervention:

-   Symptomatic severe calcific aortic stenosis in patients at prohibitive or high surgical risk for whom TAVR is expected to provide a meaningful improvement in quality of life or survival:
    -   Prohibitive surgical risk defined as greater than 50% risk of death or major irreversible morbidity at 30 days from surgery, or clinical characteristics that are not captured by the STS score but individually or collectively confer prohibitive operative risk
    -   High surgical risk defined as STS-PROM 8% or greater
-   Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis in patients at intermediate surgical risk (STS-PROM 4% to less than 8%) when the Heart Team agrees TAVR is at least equivalent to SAVR for the individual patient
-   Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis in patients at low surgical risk (STS-PROM less than 4%) aged 65 or older, when cardiac CT confirms favorable anatomy and the Heart Team consensus supports TAVR
-   Asymptomatic severe aortic stenosis is generally not a covered indication for TAVR under commercial payer criteria; coverage in this setting requires documented clinical trial enrollment or specific payer exception
-   Valve-in-valve TAVR for symptomatic structural degeneration of a previously implanted bioprosthetic aortic valve, when redo sternotomy risk is deemed high or prohibitive or when ViV anatomy is favorable and Heart Team prefers the transcatheter approach
-   Severe aortic stenosis requiring aortic valve replacement in patients with prior cardiac surgery (hostile chest, prior CABG with patent grafts at risk, prior mediastinal radiation) when surgical risk is elevated and TAVR anatomy is suitable

REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION

The following documentation must be present in the medical record to support prior authorization for TAVR:

-   Heart team note:
    -   Written consensus note from the multidisciplinary Heart Team (structural/interventional cardiologist and cardiac surgeon at minimum) documenting:
        -   Confirmation of severe, symptomatic AS
        -   Surgical risk assessment with calculated STS-PROM score and the input variables used
        -   Frailty assessment using a validated tool (for example, Essential Frailty Toolset, Fried criteria, or Clinical Frailty Scale) if clinical frailty was a factor in the decision
        -   Rationale for selecting TAVR over SAVR or other management strategies
        -   Planned access approach (transfemoral preferred; alternative access if transfemoral is not feasible, with documentation of the reason)
-   Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE):
    -   Complete Doppler echocardiographic report confirming severe AS with measurement of AVA (by continuity equation), mean gradient, and peak aortic jet velocity
    -   Left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) and left ventricular size and wall motion
    -   Assessment of co-existing valvular pathology (mitral regurgitation, aortic regurgitation)
    -   For low-flow, low-gradient AS: documentation of stroke volume index; if LVEF is reduced, dobutamine stress echocardiography report demonstrating true severe AS (AVA 1.0 cm² or less at peak stress) may be required
-   Transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE) or cardiac MRI (when performed):
    -   TEE or CMR may be submitted in addition to TTE when obtained for procedural planning, intraprocedural guidance, or clarification of valve morphology or regurgitation severity
-   Cardiac CT angiography (CCTA) for TAVR planning:
    -   Dedicated cardiac and aortoiliac CT angiography report confirming:
        -   Aortic annulus measurements (area-derived diameter, perimeter-derived diameter, annular area) for prosthesis sizing
        -   Assessment of left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) anatomy, supra-annular structures, and aortic root dimensions
        -   Coronary artery height and risk of coronary obstruction
        -   Characterization of aortic valve morphology (tricuspid versus bicuspid) and calcium distribution
        -   Iliofemoral vascular access assessment (minimum luminal diameter, tortuosity, calcification) for transfemoral approach planning; alternative access route assessment if transfemoral is not feasible
-   Coronary artery assessment:
    -   Invasive coronary angiography (left heart catheterization) or coronary CT angiography within 12 months documenting the presence or absence of significant obstructive coronary artery disease (CAD)
    -   If significant CAD is present, Heart Team documentation that CAD has been addressed or that revascularization is not warranted prior to or concurrent with TAVR
-   Symptom documentation:
    -   Clinician note with documented NYHA functional class or description of exertional symptoms (dyspnea, angina, syncope, or pre-syncope) attributable to AS
    -   For exercise-induced symptoms: exercise test report if physiologic testing was used to elicit or confirm symptoms
-   Additional records as applicable:
    -   Prior operative report if redo cardiac surgery history is relevant
    -   Pulmonary function tests if severe COPD was a factor in surgical risk assessment
    -   Liver function studies or hepatology consultation if cirrhosis was a factor
    -   Neurological evaluation if recent stroke or TIA was a factor in the risk-benefit analysis

LIMITATIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

-   TAVR is not covered for asymptomatic severe AS in the absence of clinical trial enrollment or documented extraordinary clinical circumstances agreed upon by the Heart Team and payer
-   TAVR is not covered when the aortic stenosis is mild or moderate (AVA greater than 1.5 cm², mean gradient less than 25 mmHg, peak velocity less than 3.0 m/s) unless additional hemodynamic severity criteria are met
-   TAVR is not covered when symptoms are attributed to a non-AS etiology and have not been adequately evaluated or treated
-   TAVR is not covered in the absence of a formal Heart Team evaluation and documented consensus note from both a qualified interventional/structural cardiologist and a cardiac surgeon
-   TAVR is not covered when the patient has a mechanical aortic valve in place; valve-in-valve TAVR applies only to bioprosthetic valves
-   TAVR is not covered when active endocarditis of the aortic valve or prosthesis is present or suspected
-   TAVR is not covered when there is a known allergy or contraindication to anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy that cannot be safely managed perioperatively, unless the Heart Team documents a clear plan
-   TAVR is not covered when life expectancy is less than 12 months due to non-cardiac comorbidities and TAVR is unlikely to provide a meaningful improvement in quality of life or functional status
-   TAVR is not covered for pure native aortic regurgitation (without stenosis) except in the context of clinical trial enrollment; current evidence does not support routine commercial use for isolated native AR
-   TAVR is not covered at facilities that do not meet minimum structural heart disease program requirements (on-site cardiac surgery capability or a formally approved TAVR program with documented Heart Team infrastructure)
-   Repeat TAVR within a bioprosthetic or transcatheter valve (redo ViV) is reviewed on a case-by-case basis and requires documentation of structural valve deterioration, anatomic feasibility by CCTA, and Heart Team consensus that coronary obstruction risk, valve-in-valve geometry, and patient-specific factors support the procedure
-   Concurrent procedures (for example, percutaneous coronary intervention at time of TAVR, WATCHMAN left atrial appendage closure, mitral intervention) require separate documentation of medical necessity for each additional intervention
