\## General Medical Necessity Requirements

Upper endoscopy is generally supported when all of the following are
documented:

\- Diagnosis is confirmed or there is a reasonable pretest likelihood
based on:

\- History

\- Physical exam

\- Relevant lab studies

\- Prior diagnostic testing

\- Response to prior treatment

\- Expected benefit outweighs potential harms

\- Request is supported by accepted guidelines/literature/standards of
care

\- Endoscopy is reasonably likely to change management or improve
outcome

\### Required Clinical Support

\- Documentation must:

\- Accurately reflect the patient's clinical status at the time of
request

\- Clearly show the ordering clinician's intent

\### Repeat / Multiple Procedure Limits

\- Multiple procedures ordered at the same time may require additional
review when necessity depends on the first procedure's result.

\- Repeat diagnostic testing is generally limited to:

\- Follow-up after intervention, or

\- Change in clinical status

\- Repeat diagnostic testing may require additional review when:

\- Repeated at the same facility due to technical issues

\- Repeated at a different facility due to provider preference or
quality concerns

\- Repeated for persistent symptoms without clinical
change/treatment/intervention

\- Repeated by different providers over a short interval

\- Repeat therapeutic intervention is generally appropriate only when:

\- Prior intervention was beneficial, and

\- Expected duration of benefit has lapsed

\## General Indications

\### Alarm Features

Upper endoscopy is medically necessary for any of the following:

\- Dysphagia or odynophagia

\- Nausea or vomiting for 7 days or more

\- Unexplained weight loss of at least 5% of usual body weight within
the prior 12 months

\- Anorexia or early satiety

\- Upper GI symptoms with a first-degree relative who has upper GI
malignancy

\- Hematemesis, coffee-ground emesis, or melena

\### General Therapeutic Upper Endoscopy

Upper endoscopy is medically necessary for:

\- Treatment of bleeding lesions such as:

\- Ulcers

\- Tumors

\- Vascular abnormalities

\- Planned dilation of a known stenotic lesion of the:

\- Esophagus

\- Stomach

\- Duodenum

\- Planned resection of a known lesion

\- Endoscopic placement of feeding or drainage tubes

\- Management of upper GI foreign bodies or food impaction

\- Palliation of stenosing neoplasms, including:

\- Ablation

\- Self-expanding metal stent placement

\## Condition-Specific Medical Necessity Criteria

\### Achalasia

In established achalasia, upper endoscopy is medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation of alarm features

\- Endoscopic dilation

\- Endoscopic botulinum toxin injection when the patient cannot undergo
dilation or surgery

\### Barrett's Esophagus

\#### One-Time Screening

Medically necessary only when all apply:

\- No life-limiting comorbidities

\- Patient is willing and able to undergo surveillance and eradication
treatment

\- Male with chronic GERD (\> 5 years) and/or frequent GERD (at least
weekly)

\- At least 2 of the following risk factors:

\- Age 50 or older

\- Caucasian race

\- Central obesity

\- Current or past smoking

\- First-degree relative with Barrett's esophagus or esophageal
adenocarcinoma

\#### Surveillance for Untreated Barrett's

For patients without life-limiting comorbidities and willing/able to
undergo eradication treatment:

\- Barrett's without dysplasia:

\- Every 3 years

\- Barrett's reported as indefinite for dysplasia:

\- Repeat after 3 months of PPI

\- If still indefinite, annual surveillance until definitive result

\- Barrett's with low-grade dysplasia:

\- Repeat at 6 months, then annually

\- If 2 sequential endoscopies are negative for dysplasia, then every 3
years

\- Barrett's with high-grade dysplasia:

\- Every 3 months only if unable to undergo immediate eradication
therapy

\#### Treatment

Medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic resection of Barrett's with or without dysplasia

\- Radiofrequency ablation or cryoablation, repeated until complete
eradication of intestinal metaplasia, for:

\- Low-grade dysplasia

\- High-grade dysplasia

\#### Surveillance After Treatment

Medically necessary for:

\- Confirmation of eradication of intestinal metaplasia

\- After eradication of low-grade dysplasia:

\- At 6, 12, and 24 months

\- Then every 3 years

\- After eradication of high-grade dysplasia:

\- Every 3 months in year 1

\- Every 6 months in year 2

\- Then annually

\### Bleeding and Anemia

\#### New or Recurrent Upper GI Bleeding

Medically necessary for:

\- Hematemesis or coffee-ground emesis

\- Melena

\- Unexplained rectal bleeding or positive fecal occult blood test when
either applies:

\- Known risk factors for upper GI bleeding

\- Non-diagnostic colonoscopy

\#### Treatment of Bleeding Lesions

Medically necessary for treatment of bleeding lesions such as:

\- Ulcers

\- Tumors

\- Vascular abnormalities

\#### Unexplained Iron Deficiency Anemia

Medically necessary only when both are present:

\- Hemoglobin:

\- \< 13 g/dL in men, or

\- \< 12 g/dL in women

\- Ferritin \< 45 ng/mL

\- If chronic inflammatory disease is present, alternate evidence of low
iron stores may be used:

\- Increased total iron-binding capacity

\- Low serum iron

\- Low transferrin saturation

\#### Pernicious Anemia

In established pernicious anemia, medically necessary for:

\- Initial diagnosis

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\### Caustic Ingestion

Medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation of acute injury

\- Surveillance for esophageal cancer beginning 10 years after ingestion
and every 2 years thereafter

\### Celiac Disease

\#### Diagnosis

Medically necessary for either:

\- Suspected celiac disease with positive IgA or IgG serology

\- Symptomatic suspected celiac disease regardless of serology when any
risk factor is present:

\- Type 1 diabetes

\- First-degree relative with confirmed celiac disease

\- Documented autoimmune disorder

\- Diarrhea with malabsorption findings such as:

\- Unexplained nutritional deficiencies

\- Loss of 5% or more of usual weight

\#### Management of Established Celiac Disease

Medically necessary for either:

\- Persistent/recurrent signs or symptoms when both apply:

\- At least 1 year documented compliance with gluten-free diet

\- Non-diagnostic serology

\- Asymptomatic assessment of healing when both apply:

\- At least 2 years documented gluten-free diet

\- Non-diagnostic serology

\### Crohn's Disease

Medically necessary for diagnosis/management in either:

\- Suspected Crohn's disease:

\- Upper GI signs/symptoms present, or

\- Colonoscopy non-diagnostic

\- Established Crohn's disease:

\- New or persistent upper GI signs/symptoms

\### Duodenal Masses

Medically necessary for:

\- Established duodenal carcinoma

\- Established ampullary or non-ampullary duodenal adenomas

\### Dyspepsia

For predominant epigastric discomfort lasting at least 4 weeks,
medically necessary for:

\- Initial evaluation when alarm features are present

\- Initial evaluation at age 55 or older

\- Age 54 or younger with persistent symptoms after both:

\- H. pylori testing/treatment

\- 4 weeks of PPI treatment

\### Eosinophilic Esophagitis

In established disease, medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation of alarm features

\- Reassessment to direct change in therapy or perform dilation after 8
weeks of any of:

\- PPI

\- Topical or systemic corticosteroids

\- Elimination diet

\- After initial dilation:

\- Repeat dilation until adequate resolution of stricture

\### Esophageal Malignancies

In biopsy-confirmed esophageal cancer, medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- Signs or symptoms of recurrent or progressive disease

\- Following resection without esophagectomy of squamous cell cancer

\- Management of T1a esophageal adenocarcinoma with either:

\- Radiofrequency ablation or cryoablation until complete eradication of
intestinal metaplasia

\- Surveillance after confirmed eradication:

\- Every 3 months in year 1

\- Every 6 months in year 2

\- Then annually

\### Esophageal Varices

In cirrhosis or portal hypertension, medically necessary for:

\- Initial screening after diagnosis

\- Surveillance of non-bleeding varices in patients not on prophylactic
beta-blockers:

\- No varices: every 2 years

\- Small varices (\< 5 mm): annually

\- Varices with high-risk stigmata: annually

\- Decompensated cirrhosis or continued alcohol use: annually

\- Treatment of bleeding or non-bleeding varices with either:

\- Endoscopic ligation until eradication

\- Sclerotherapy if ligation not technically feasible, until eradication

\- After eradication by ligation or sclerotherapy:

\- Initial endoscopy after 3 months

\- Continued surveillance every 6 months

\### Gastric Intestinal Metaplasia (GIM)

\#### GIM Without Dysplasia

After H. pylori testing/treatment, medically necessary for:

\- Initial follow-up at 1 year for mapping biopsies if not done at
diagnosis

\- Surveillance every 3 years when any risk factor is present:

\- Higher-risk racial or ethnic group for gastric cancer

\- First-degree relative with gastric cancer

\- Incomplete GIM

\- Extensive GIM

\#### GIM With Low-Grade Dysplasia

Medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- Repeat endoscopy for mapping biopsies if not done at diagnosis

\- Surveillance after H. pylori testing/treatment:

\- Annually until no dysplasia and no visible lesions

\- Then every 3 years

\#### GIM With High-Grade Dysplasia

Medically necessary for either:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- Surveillance after H. pylori testing/treatment:

\- At 6 months

\- At 12 months

\- Then annually for 3 years

\- Then every 3 years

\### Gastric Malignancies

\#### Gastric Adenocarcinoma / Carcinoma in Situ

Medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- New signs/symptoms of recurrence or progression

\- Carcinoma in situ after endoscopic resection:

\- At 6 months

\- At 12 months

\- Then annually for 3 years

\- Then every 3 years

\- Invasive gastric adenocarcinoma after endoscopic resection:

\- At 6 months

\- At 12 months

\- Then annually for 5 years

\- Then every 3 years

\#### MALT Lymphoma

In established MALT lymphoma, medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- New signs/symptoms of recurrence/progression

\- After chemotherapy, immunotherapy, or radiotherapy to direct
management

\- After H. pylori treatment alone:

\- Every 3 months for first 2 years

\- Then every 6 months until 5 years

\- Then annually

\#### Upper GI Neuroendocrine Tumors

In established upper GI NET, medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- New signs/symptoms of recurrence/progression

\- Type 1 and 2 gastric NET:

\- Annual surveillance

\- Type 3 gastric NET after resection:

\- At 3 months

\- Then every 12 months for 10 years total

\- Duodenal NET:

\- Follow-up endoscopy as needed to direct further management

\#### Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumors (GIST)

In established GIST, medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- New signs/symptoms of recurrence/progression

\- Annual surveillance endoscopy if not treated with resection

\### Gastric Polyps

\#### Hyperplastic Polyps

After H. pylori testing/treatment, medically necessary for:

\- Annual surveillance until negative for polyp or dysplasia

\#### Adenomatous Polyps

Medically necessary for:

\- Endoscopic lesion resection

\- No dysplasia or low-grade dysplasia:

\- 1 year after resection

\- Then every 3 years

\- High-grade dysplasia:

\- At 6 months

\- At 12 months

\- Then annually for 3 years

\- Then every 3 years

\### Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

Medically necessary for:

\- GERD symptoms with alarm features

\- Persistent GERD after 8 weeks of PPI treatment

\- Follow-up after initial endoscopy showing Los Angeles class C or D
erosive esophagitis after another 8-week course of PPI

\### Genetic / Familial Conditions Associated With Upper GI Malignancy

\#### Adenomatous Polyposis Syndromes

In confirmed syndromes, medically necessary for:

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- FAP or AFAP:

\- Begin at age 20

\- Then surveillance by Spigelman stage for duodenal polyps

\- MAP:

\- Begin at age 30

\- Then surveillance by Spigelman stage

\##### Modified Spigelman Scoring

\- Polyp number:

\- 0 = 0 points

\- 1 to 4 = 1 point

\- 5 to 20 = 2 points

\- \> 20 = 3 points

\- Polyp size:

\- No polyps = 0 points

\- 1 to 4 mm = 1 point

\- 5 to 10 mm = 2 points

\- \> 10 mm = 3 points

\- Histology:

\- No adenomas = 0 points

\- Tubular adenoma = 1 point

\- Tubulovillous adenoma = 2 points

\- Villous adenoma = 3 points

\- Dysplasia:

\- No dysplasia = 0 points

\- Low grade = 1 point

\- High grade = 3 points

\##### Spigelman Stage Surveillance

\- 0 points / Stage 0:

\- Every 3 to 5 years

\- 1 to 4 points / Stage I:

\- Every 2 to 3 years

\- 5 to 6 points / Stage II:

\- Every 1 to 2 years

\- 7 to 8 points / Stage III:

\- Every 6 to 12 months

\- 9 to 12 points / Stage IV:

\- Expert surveillance every 3 to 6 months

\#### Lynch Syndrome

In confirmed Lynch syndrome, medically necessary for:

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- Screening beginning at age 30 and every 3 years if any of:

\- MLH1, MSH2, PMS2, or EPCAM mutation

\- First-degree relative with upper GI cancer

\- Higher-risk racial or ethnic group for gastric cancer

\#### Peutz-Jeghers Syndrome

Medically necessary for:

\- Initial screening

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- Surveillance every 2 years

\#### Juvenile Polyposis Syndrome

Medically necessary for:

\- Initial screening

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- Surveillance every 2 years

\- 1-year follow-up of any polyp

\#### Li-Fraumeni Syndrome

Medically necessary for:

\- Screening at age 25, or 5 years before first known familial colon
cancer diagnosis if earlier

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- Surveillance every 2 years

\#### Hereditary Diffuse Gastric Cancer

With confirmed genetic testing, medically necessary for either:

\- Preoperative evaluation for prophylactic gastrectomy

\- Surveillance every 6 months if unable to tolerate prophylactic
gastrectomy

\#### Tylosis

Medically necessary for:

\- Screening at diagnosis

\- New upper GI signs/symptoms

\- Annual surveillance

\### Imaging Abnormalities

For lesions seen on other imaging and not previously evaluated by
endoscopy, medically necessary for:

\- Suspected neoplastic lesion

\- Gastric or esophageal ulcer

\- Upper tract stricture or obstruction

\### Peptic Ulcer Disease

Medically necessary for:

\- Gastric or esophageal ulcer on other imaging not previously evaluated
by endoscopy

\- Alarm features

\- Follow-up of known PUD after 8 weeks of PPI and H. pylori
testing/treatment when any apply:

\- Persistent symptoms

\- Suspicious ulcer features:

\- Associated mass lesion

\- Elevated or irregular borders

\- Abnormal adjacent mucosal folds

\- Gastric ulcer \> 3 cm

\- Duodenal ulcer \> 2 cm

\- Persistent unexplained ulcers:

\- Repeat every 8 weeks until healing is documented

\### Postoperative / Post-Procedure Complications

Medically necessary for endoscopic diagnosis and management of:

\- Post-surgical complications

\- Post-procedural complications

\### Preoperative Evaluation Before Bariatric Surgery in Asymptomatic
Patients

Medically necessary for:

\- Suspected esophagitis or Barrett's esophagus before sleeve
gastrectomy

\- Suspected gastric lesions before Roux-en-Y gastric bypass after H.
pylori testing/treatment in high-risk patients with any of:

\- Age 55 or older

\- Positive H. pylori testing

\- First-degree relative with gastric cancer

\- Higher-risk racial or ethnic group for gastric cancer

\## Required Documentation

\### General Documentation

\- History and physical examination

\- Relevant laboratory results

\- Relevant prior diagnostic testing

\- Prior treatment history and response

\- Clear indication and intended management impact

\- Current symptoms and duration

\- Documentation that the request reflects the current clinical
condition

\### Common Condition-Specific Documentation

\- Alarm symptoms:

\- Dysphagia

\- Odynophagia

\- Weight loss

\- Persistent nausea/vomiting

\- Early satiety/anorexia

\- GI bleeding symptoms

\- GERD:

\- Duration/frequency of symptoms

\- Prior 8-week PPI trial

\- Prior endoscopy findings if erosive esophagitis

\- Dyspepsia:

\- Symptom duration of at least 4 weeks

\- Age

\- H. pylori testing/treatment

\- 4-week PPI trial if age 54 or younger

\- Bleeding / anemia:

\- Hemoglobin

\- Ferritin or alternate iron studies if inflammation present

\- FOBT results

\- Colonoscopy result when applicable

\- Celiac disease:

\- Serology results

\- Symptoms

\- Risk factors

\- Gluten-free diet compliance duration

\- Crohn's disease:

\- Upper GI symptoms

\- Colonoscopy result if non-diagnostic

\- Esophageal / gastric malignancy:

\- Pathology confirmation

\- Stage/grade when applicable

\- Recurrence/progression symptoms

\- Barrett's esophagus:

\- Dysplasia grade

\- Prior pathology

\- Treatment history

\- Confirmation of eradication status

\- Esophageal varices:

\- Cirrhosis/portal hypertension diagnosis

\- Variceal size

\- High-risk stigmata

\- Beta-blocker treatment status/intolerance

\- GIM / gastric polyps:

\- H. pylori testing/treatment

\- Dysplasia status

\- Mapping biopsy status

\- Risk factors for gastric cancer

\- Genetic syndromes:

\- Confirmed diagnosis or genetic testing

\- Family history

\- Age at screening

\- Spigelman stage where applicable

\- Imaging abnormalities:

\- Imaging report showing lesion, ulcer, stricture, or obstruction

\- Pre-bariatric evaluation:

\- Planned procedure type

\- H. pylori status/treatment

\- High-risk features

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\### Explicit Exclusions

\- Endoscopic ultrasound

\- Placement of pH probe

\### Scope Limitations

\- Not for emergency department use

\- Not for inpatient use

\- Not for pediatric patients age 17 or younger

\### Additional Notable Limitations / Non-Covered Situations

\- Confirmation of H. pylori eradication alone is not an independent
indication for upper endoscopy

\- Routine repeat Barrett's screening after a negative screening exam is
not recommended

\- Routine Barrett's screening of women or the general population is not
recommended

\- Routine ablative therapy for non-dysplastic Barrett's esophagus is
not recommended

\- Routine EGD for extra-esophageal symptoms alone (chronic cough,
laryngitis, asthma) without typical GERD symptoms is not recommended

\- Routine EGD in adult patients suspected of Crohn's disease is not
recommended unless:

\- Upper GI symptoms are present, or

\- Colonoscopy is non-diagnostic

\- Routine biopsy/follow-up of duodenal ulcers is generally not
recommended when uncomplicated and responsive to treatment

\- Findings that generally do not warrant endoscopic evaluation:

\- Asymptomatic or uncomplicated sliding hiatal hernia

\- Uncomplicated duodenal ulcer responsive to therapy

\- Deformed duodenal bulb when symptoms are absent or respond adequately
to ulcer treatment

\- Routine endoscopic surveillance for esophageal cancer in achalasia is
not recommended

\- Routine endoscopic screening related to upper airway squamous cell
cancer is not recommended

\- Combined beta-blocker therapy plus EGD surveillance is not
recommended for non-bleeding varices managed prophylactically

\- Repeated testing without clinical change, new findings, or management
impact may be subject to denial or additional review

\## Practical Summary

Upper GI endoscopy is supported when there is a clear outpatient adult
indication, adequate clinical documentation, and a reasonable
expectation that the procedure will diagnose disease, direct treatment,
or provide necessary surveillance. The guideline is highly structured
around:

\- Alarm features

\- Failure of appropriate medical therapy

\- Histology-confirmed dysplasia or malignancy

\- Bleeding/anemia thresholds

\- Confirmed hereditary cancer syndromes

\- Specific surveillance intervals after treatment or for high-risk
conditions
