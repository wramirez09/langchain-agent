\## Medical Necessity Criteria

\### General Clinical Framework

Proton beam therapy requests should meet the following general medical
necessity standards:

\- The diagnosis must be confirmed or have a well-established pretest
likelihood based on:

\- history and physical examination

\- relevant laboratory studies when applicable

\- relevant diagnostic testing

\- response to prior therapeutic intervention when applicable

\- The expected benefit must outweigh potential harms, including harms
from delay or reduced access.

\- Current literature, accepted treatment guidelines, and standards of
practice should support the intervention as offering the greatest net
benefit among alternatives.

\- There should be a reasonable likelihood that the intervention will
change management and/or improve outcome.

\### Additional Request Standards

\- For requests \*\*outside approved criteria\*\*, a \*\*case-control
plan comparison alone is not sufficient\*\*.

\- A \*\*direct, patient-specific IMRT isodose comparison\*\* is
required for consideration.

\- When proton therapy is considered appropriate, it should be
administered as \*\*monotherapy\*\*.

\- Proton therapy is not covered simply because it is part of a clinical
trial or registry unless the medical necessity criteria are otherwise
met.

\### Specific Approved Clinical Indications

\#### 1. Base of Skull Tumors: Chordoma / Chondrosarcoma

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- It is being used as \*\*postoperative therapy\*\*, and

\- The patient has had \*\*biopsy or partial resection\*\*, and

\- The tumor is:

\- chordoma, or

\- low-grade (grade I or II) chondrosarcoma, and

\- The tumor is located in the:

\- basisphenoid region / skull base

\- cervical spine

\- sacral/lower spine, and

\- There is \*\*residual localized tumor\*\*, and

\- There is \*\*no evidence of metastasis\*\*.

\#### 2. Sinonasal Carcinoma

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- The tumor is \*\*locally advanced\*\*, and

\- It \*\*involves the base of skull\*\*, and

\- Proton therapy is needed to spare one or more critical structures:

\- orbit

\- optic nerve

\- optic chiasm

\- brainstem.

\#### 3. Central Nervous System Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM)

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when \*\*any\*\* of the following are
met:

\- Intracranial AVM is \*\*not amenable to surgical excision\*\* or
other conventional treatment, or

\- The lesion is \*\*adjacent to critical structures\*\* such as:

\- optic nerve

\- brain stem

\- spinal cord.

\#### 4. Central Nervous System Tumors

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when \*\*all\*\* of the following are
met:

\- The condition involves CNS tumors, including but not limited to:

\- primary CNS malignancies

\- metastatic CNS malignancies

\- gliomas

\- The lesion is \*\*adjacent to critical structures\*\* such as:

\- optic nerve

\- brain stem

\- spinal cord

\- Other standard radiation techniques, such as:

\- IMRT

\- standard stereotactic modalities

would \*\*not sufficiently reduce the risk of radiation damage\*\* to
the critical structure.

\#### 5. Hepatobiliary Cancer: Hepatocellular Carcinoma / Intrahepatic
Cholangiocarcinoma

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- The disease is:

\- unresectable

\- non-metastatic

\- hepatocellular carcinoma or intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma

\- Treatment is being given with \*\*curative intent\*\*.

\#### 6. Ocular Melanoma

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- The melanoma involves the \*\*uveal tract\*\*, including:

\- iris

\- choroid

\- ciliary body

\- There is \*\*no evidence of metastasis\*\*

\- There is \*\*no extrascleral extension\*\*.

\#### 7. Pediatric Patients

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- The patient is \*\*younger than 21 years\*\*, and

\- Radiation therapy is required to treat the tumor.

\#### 8. Re-irradiation

Proton beam therapy is appropriate when:

\- Previously treated fields require repeat irradiation, and

\- The dose tolerance of surrounding normal structures would be exceeded
with:

\- 3D conformal radiation therapy, or

\- IMRT.

\## Required Documentation

\### Core Documentation

Requests may require documentation that:

\- accurately reflects the patient's clinical status at the time of the
request

\- clearly documents the ordering clinician's intent.

\### Expected Supporting Clinical Records

Based on the general framework, supporting documentation should include:

\- history and physical examination

\- relevant laboratory results when applicable

\- relevant diagnostic testing

\- prior treatment history

\- response to prior therapy when applicable

\- diagnosis and staging details

\- anatomic relationship to critical structures when relevant

\- metastatic status when relevant

\- resectability status when relevant

\- curative intent when relevant

\- age documentation for pediatric cases.

\### Additional Documentation That May Be Needed

\- \*\*Patient-specific direct IMRT isodose comparison\*\* for requests
outside approved criteria.

\- Documentation supporting simultaneous multiple interventions if
requested together.

\- Documentation explaining need for repeat diagnostic testing or repeat
therapeutic intervention when applicable.

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\### Conditions Specifically Listed as Not Medically Necessary

Proton beam therapy is not medically necessary for:

\- breast cancer

\- esophageal cancer

\- gastric cancer

\- gynecologic cancer

\- head and neck cancer other than the specific approved sinonasal
indication

\- hepatobiliary cancers not otherwise listed as approved

\- lung cancer

\- lymphoma (Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin)

\- pancreatic cancer

\- prostate cancer.

\### General Limitations

\- For clinical situations with insufficient high-quality evidence
compared with photon-based therapy, proton therapy is considered not
medically necessary.

\- Outside approved criteria, a case-control plan comparison is not
enough; a direct IMRT isodose comparison is required.

\- Use in a clinical trial or registry is not covered unless standard
criteria are met.

\- Nonspecific or unlisted procedure codes may require additional
documentation and review.

\### Repeat Testing / Repeat Treatment Limitations

Repeated diagnostic testing may require additional review or peer
discussion in situations such as:

\- repeat testing at the same facility due to technical issues

\- repeat testing at a different facility due to provider preference or
quality concerns

\- repeat testing of the same anatomic area with persistent symptoms but
no clinical change, treatment, or intervention

\- repeat testing of the same anatomic area by different providers over
a short period.

Repeated therapeutic intervention in the same anatomic area is generally
appropriate only when:

\- prior intervention was effective or beneficial, and

\- the expected duration of relief has lapsed.

A repeat intervention before the expected duration has lapsed is not
appropriate unless it can be confirmed that the prior intervention was
never administered.
