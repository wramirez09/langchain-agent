SECTION-BY-SECTION EXTRACTION

1\) General abdominal and pelvic indications

\- Congenital/developmental conditions NOS

\- Adults: CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI abdomen/pelvis.

\- Pediatrics: ultrasound first for hepatobiliary/genitourinary
anomalies; ultrasound recommended first for pancreatic anomalies; CT/MRI
when extra imaging is needed; MRI preferred for uterine anomalies; MRCP
preferred for biliary/pancreatic duct anomalies.

\- Abscess / infectious-inflammatory condition NOS

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI abdomen/pelvis.

\- Trauma NOS

\- CT abdomen/pelvis.

\- MRI abdomen/pelvis only if CT cannot be performed or is
nondiagnostic.

\- Tumor / neoplasm NOS

\- Adults: pelvic ultrasound first for palpable pelvic mass in female at
birth; ultrasound first for testicular mass in male at birth; CT
abdomen/pelvis for other scenarios or after nondiagnostic pelvic
ultrasound; MRI abdomen for further characterization of abdominal mass
on prior imaging.

\- Pediatrics: ultrasound first for palpable pelvic mass; ultrasound
recommended first for abdominal mass; CT or MRI for palpable abdominal
mass or after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Exclusion: known malignancy management/screening goes to Oncologic
Imaging.

2\) Female reproductive / obstetric indications

\- Adenomyosis: MRI pelvis after nondiagnostic pelvic ultrasound.

\- Adnexal mass: MRI pelvis after nondiagnostic pelvic ultrasound.

\- Endometriosis: MRI pelvis after nondiagnostic pelvic ultrasound for
suspected disease, or for management of established disease.

\- Obstetric indications:

\- Covered uses: fetal anomalies, pre-fetal intervention assessment,
placental complications, monochorionic twin complications, pelvimetry,
other obstetrical complications.

\- Ultrasound first for fetal/placental conditions.

\- Fetal MRI only in 2nd or 3rd trimester after nondiagnostic
ultrasound.

\- MRI pelvis for pelvimetry/other obstetrical complications.

\- Uterine leiomyomata:

\- MRI pelvis after nondiagnostic ultrasound when leiomyosarcoma is
suspected or before fertility-sparing procedure.

\- Exception: excludes MR-guided focused ultrasound pathway.

3\) Gastrointestinal indications

\- Appendicitis

\- Adults, nonpregnant: CT abdomen/pelvis.

\- Pregnancy: ultrasound first; MRI if ultrasound nondiagnostic; CT only
if ultrasound nondiagnostic and MRI contraindicated/unavailable.

\- Pediatrics: ultrasound first/preferred; CT or MRI if ultrasound
cannot be performed or is nondiagnostic.

\- Bowel obstruction

\- Pediatric radiographs required first.

\- CT when additional imaging is needed.

\- MRI in pediatrics or in adults when CT cannot be done or is
nondiagnostic.

\- Constipation, pediatric only

\- Needs symptoms for 2+ weeks, nondiagnostic radiographs, and one of:
failed medical management, failure to thrive, fever, vomiting, or
post-barium enema/anal manometry concern for anal stenosis, infant
impaction, or tight empty rectum.

\- CT or MRI abdomen/pelvis.

\- Diverticulitis

\- CT abdomen/pelvis for diagnosis/management.

\- Enteritis or colitis NOS

\- Includes ischemic/infectious/neutropenic colitis, Henoch-Schonlein
purpura, radiation enteritis.

\- Excludes IBD.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis.

\- GI bleeding

\- Only for suspected small bowel source after nondiagnostic endoscopy
and colonoscopy.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis, or MRI if CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic.

\- Inflammatory bowel disease

\- Diagnostic use after nondiagnostic colonoscopy in selected Crohn
disease scenarios.

\- Management use for new/worsening symptoms or complications such as
stricture, abscess, toxic megacolon, fistula.

\- CT or MRI abdomen/pelvis.

\- Documentation should include colonoscopy status, CRP or fecal
calprotectin if relevant, and upper endoscopy if upper GI symptoms are
present.

\- Perianal fistula/abscess

\- When physical exam is incomplete/inadequate.

\- MRI pelvis preferred; CT pelvis acceptable.

4\) Hepatobiliary indications

\- Biliary tract dilatation or obstruction

\- MRI/MRCP for unexplained biliary duct dilation, biochemical
obstruction after nondiagnostic ultrasound, or annual evaluation in
Caroli disease/syndrome.

\- Cholecystitis

\- Only after nondiagnostic ultrasound or for complications of acute
cholecystitis/cholecystectomy.

\- CT abdomen; MRI abdomen in pediatrics or if CT cannot be
done/nondiagnostic.

\- Not recommended for acute uncomplicated cholecystitis.

\- Choledocholithiasis

\- MRI/MRCP after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Diffuse liver disease

\- Covered for suspected liver disease with abnormal clinical
findings/LFTs after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Covered for iron overload/hemochromatosis when therapy decisions are
being made.

\- Covered for advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis assessment in high-risk NAFLD
or when ultrasound elastography cannot be done/is nondiagnostic.

\- CT abdomen, MRI abdomen, or MR elastography depending on scenario.

\- Exclusions/limits: HCC screening redirected to Oncologic Imaging;
LiverMultiScan not recommended in guideline excerpts.

\- Focal liver lesion

\- For indeterminate lesions not biopsied/not fully characterized,
especially when \>1 cm or with high-risk factors.

\- Follow-up 3-6 months when risk factors exist or lesion is enhancing,
poorly defined, or enlarging.

\- Benign lesions only for symptom change or surveillance of hepatic
adenoma.

\- CT abdomen or MRI abdomen.

\- Limits: simple cyst with benign ultrasound features may not need
advanced imaging/surveillance; routine CT/MRI monitoring of benign focal
lesions is not recommended unless there is major clinical change.

\- Hepatomegaly

\- CT abdomen, or MRI abdomen in pediatrics/adults when CT cannot be
done or is nondiagnostic, after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Jaundice

\- Adults: when unexplained by liver/biliary function tests.

\- Pediatrics: after nondiagnostic ultrasound and unexplained by
liver/biliary function tests.

\- CT abdomen or MRI/MRCP.

\- Primary sclerosing cholangitis

\- MRI/MRCP for diagnosis and management.

5\) Osseous indications

\- Avascular necrosis, bilateral hip

\- MRI pelvis after negative/inconclusive radiographs or for preop
planning with femoral head collapse.

\- CT pelvis only if MRI or bone scan unavailable/contraindicated.

\- Axial spondyloarthropathy

\- MRI pelvis when radiographs are negative/equivocal and inflammatory
back pain has been present at least 3 months with qualifying features.

\- Also for baseline pre-therapy imaging or reevaluation after at least
3 months of TNF inhibitors without improvement.

\- Sacroiliitis NOS

\- CT or MRI pelvis after pelvic/sacral radiographs when predisposition
exists and radiographs are negative/equivocal, or radiographs are
equivocal.

\- Developmental hip dysplasia, pediatric only

\- CT pelvis for preoperative planning.

\- Osseous tumor

\- CT pelvis; MRI pelvis in pediatrics or when CT cannot be
done/nondiagnostic in adults.

\- Osteoid osteoma

\- CT pelvis after negative/inconclusive hip radiographs.

\- Osteomyelitis

\- Adults: after nondiagnostic radiographs.

\- Pediatrics: diagnosis and management.

\- MRI pelvis preferred; CT pelvis if MRI/bone scan unavailable or
contraindicated.

\- Septic arthritis

\- Adults: after nondiagnostic radiographs.

\- Pediatrics: diagnosis and management.

\- MRI pelvis preferred; CT pelvis if MRI/bone scan unavailable or
contraindicated.

\- Pelvic fracture

\- Sacral insufficiency fracture: CT or MRI pelvis.

\- Stress or traumatic fracture: CT or MRI pelvis after nondiagnostic
pelvic/sacral radiographs.

6\) Pancreatic indications

\- Pancreatic duct dilatation

\- MRI/MRCP if seen on ultrasound or CT.

\- Indeterminate solid pancreatic mass

\- CT pancreas protocol or MRI abdomen for diagnosis, management,
surveillance.

\- Indeterminate cystic pancreatic mass

\- Applies only to asymptomatic cystic lesions.

\- Adult surgical candidates: imaging if EUS/FNA not done or
nondiagnostic; surveillance varies by age and cyst size.

\- Pediatrics: diagnosis, management, surveillance.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI/MRCP abdomen.

\- Limitation: symptomatic lesions follow symptom-based indications;
enlarging cysts often require EUS/FNA.

\- Pancreatitis

\- Only for suspected complications or recurrent acute pancreatitis of
uncertain etiology.

\- CT abdomen/abdomen-pelvis, MRI abdomen when CT cannot be done, MRCP
for recurrent disease and suspected duct anomaly.

\- Limitation: mild acute/uncomplicated pancreatitis usually does not
need cross-sectional imaging aside from ultrasound for
gallstones/biliary calculi.

7\) Renal, adrenal, urinary tract indications

\- Indeterminate adrenal mass

\- Adults: \>2 cm, new/growing in 12 months, biochemical evidence, or
indeterminate ultrasound mass; surveillance depends on size and benign
imaging features.

\- Pediatrics: diagnosis/management/surveillance in neonates when
ultrasound cannot be done or is nondiagnostic, and in non-neonates.

\- CT abdomen or MRI abdomen.

\- Limit: benign-feature lesions generally need no follow-up; known
malignancy redirected to Oncologic Imaging.

\- Bladder or urethral diverticula

\- MRI pelvis.

\- Hematuria

\- Adults: traumatic, macroscopic, or qualifying microscopic hematuria.

\- Pediatrics: traumatic or atraumatic after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MR urogram when CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic.

\- Documentation should include urinalysis, RBC/HPF, risk category,
renal ultrasound, cystoscopy if applicable.

\- Hydronephrosis

\- CT abdomen or abdomen/pelvis after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- MR urogram in pediatrics or when CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic.

\- Nephrocalcinosis

\- CT abdomen after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Polycystic kidney disease

\- CT abdomen/abdomen-pelvis or MRI abdomen after nondiagnostic
ultrasound to assess total kidney volume and therapy decisions.

\- Pyelonephritis

\- CT abdomen or MRI abdomen when CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic for:
diabetes/history of stones or renal surgery, no improvement after 72
hours antibiotics, or xanthogranulomatous pyelonephritis.

\- Renal masses

\- Includes indeterminate renal mass, growth, suspicious features,
symptomatic benign mass, and surveillance.

\- CT abdomen or MRI abdomen.

\- Limits: simple cysts with benign ultrasound features need no advanced
imaging; known malignancy or renal cancer screening in genetic
predisposition redirected to Oncologic Imaging.

\- Urinary tract calculi

\- Adult initial evaluation in patients without stone history.

\- Adult recurrence/management depends on stone type/history, age,
symptoms, planning for PCNL/ureteroscopy/SWL, persistent hydronephrosis,
pregnancy.

\- Pediatrics after nondiagnostic ultrasound or KUB.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis when advanced imaging indicated.

\- Limits: radiograph or ultrasound required first in pregnancy and
pediatric patients; CT generally avoided in uncomplicated renal colic in
otherwise healthy patients under 50 with known stone history.

8\) Splenic indications

\- Splenic mass, benign

\- CT or MRI abdomen after nondiagnostic ultrasound or rapid growth.

\- Splenic mass, indeterminate

\- CT or MRI abdomen for ultrasound-indeterminate lesion, enlargement,
suspicious features, or surveillance every 6 months up to 1 year.

\- Splenomegaly

\- CT abdomen or MRI abdomen in pediatrics/adults when CT cannot be
done/nondiagnostic after nondiagnostic ultrasound.

\- Exclusion

\- Known primary malignancy redirected to Oncologic Imaging for
indeterminate splenic mass.

9\) Miscellaneous conditions

\- Hemoperitoneum

\- CT abdomen/pelvis.

\- Hernia

\- For suspected complications or presurgical planning.

\- Pediatric ultrasound first.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI when CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic.

\- Lymphadenopathy

\- For diagnosis when lymphoproliferative disorder suspected, management
if suspicious nodes enlarge or clinical/lab findings support concern,
and surveillance at 3 and 12 months.

\- Suspicious features: \>1 cm short-axis, necrosis, hypervascularity,
abnormal morphology/loss of fatty hilum/thick cortex, nodal cluster.

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI if CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic.

\- Pelvic floor disorders

\- MRI pelvis, dynamic MRI preferred.

\- Uses: urinary/bowel incontinence due to pelvic floor disorder,
suspected pelvic organ prolapse, chronic constipation with nondiagnostic
anorectal manometry or balloon expulsion.

\- Retroperitoneal conditions

\- CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI when CT cannot be done/nondiagnostic for
fibrosis, inflammation, bleeding, mass.

\- Sports hernia

\- MRI pelvis only if all are met: progressive pain worsened by
Valsalva/movement, no detectable inguinal/ventral hernia, symptoms \>=6
weeks, nondiagnostic radiographs, failed \>=6 weeks conservative
therapy, surgical candidate.

10\) Perioperative evaluation

\- Transplant-related imaging

\- Covered scenarios:

\- living donor single pre-transplant evaluation

\- annual surveillance on liver transplant waiting list

\- single evaluation before lung, kidney, or hematopoietic stem cell
transplantation

\- suspected post-transplant complications

\- CT abdomen or CT abdomen/pelvis.

\- MRI abdomen may replace CT abdomen for liver transplant waiting-list
surveillance.

\- Limitation: if clinical condition changes before transplant, use
relevant sign/symptom-based indication.

11\) Nonspecific signs and symptoms

\- Abdominal and/or pelvic pain, undifferentiated

\- Adults:

\- acute pain with surgical abdomen/severe undifferentiated
pain/guarding, or unexplained after history, exam, labs, and ultrasound
when localized to RUQ or female pelvis

\- nonacute pain unexplained after history, exam, labs, ultrasound when
localized, upper endoscopy for epigastric pain unless inflammatory
markers elevated, and colonoscopy when pain is associated with
defecation plus stool-form/frequency change

\- Pediatrics:

\- acute unexplained pain after history, exam, labs, ultrasound

\- chronic/recurrent pelvic pain after nondiagnostic ultrasound

\- chronic/recurrent abdominal pain after nondiagnostic ultrasound plus
red flags

\- Imaging:

\- CT abdomen for upper quadrant/epigastric pain

\- CT abdomen/pelvis for lower quadrant/generalized pain

\- CT pelvis or MRI pelvis for pelvic pain

\- MRI abdomen in pediatrics or if adult CT cannot be done

\- Lab documentation may include metabolic profile, CBC, CRP, ESR,
urinalysis.

\- Limitation: use the most likely diagnosis pathway whenever possible;
guideline applies only when diagnosis remains unclear.

\- Weight loss

\- CT abdomen/pelvis for unintentional weight loss \>5% in 12 months if
persistent after negative comprehensive evaluation or if abnormal
findings suggest malignancy.

\- Prior evaluation should include history/physical, age-appropriate
cancer screening, chest radiography, and initial labs.

\- Limitation: CT screening alone is low value; endoscopy/colonoscopy
should be considered first when GI source is suspected.
