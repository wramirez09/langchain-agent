\## General Clinical Appropriateness Framework

\### Core Medical Necessity Standards

A request should be supported by all of the following:

\- A complete clinical evaluation, including history and physical exam.

\- Review of relevant labs, prior imaging, prior diagnostic testing, and
response to prior treatment when applicable.

\- A reasonable likelihood that the requested study will change
management or improve outcome.

\- Expected benefit greater than potential harm.

\- Support from accepted practice standards and current evidence.

\### General Rules

\- If a condition, indication, or imaging modality is not specifically
addressed, it is considered not medically necessary.

\- Duplex ultrasound is often preferred first when it can answer the
question.

\- CTA/MRA are commonly used when ultrasound is nondiagnostic, cannot be
performed, or when pre-intervention mapping is needed.

\- Findings unrelated to the clinical question should be interpreted in
context before more imaging is ordered.

\## Required Documentation

Requests should include enough detail to show why the study is needed.
Common required elements include:

\- Current symptoms, signs, and duration.

\- Suspected or established diagnosis.

\- Relevant exam findings.

\- Prior imaging and test results.

\- Why a first-line study was nondiagnostic or could not be performed,
if applicable.

\- Medical or surgical management question being answered.

\- Whether revascularization or another intervention is being
considered.

\- Post-procedure status when the request is for baseline, follow-up, or
surveillance.

\- Documentation of conservative treatment when required.

\- Documentation of surveillance interval and prior repair history when
follow-up imaging is requested.

\## Medical Necessity Criteria

\## 1. General Vascular

\### Congenital or Developmental Vascular Anomalies

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management when not otherwise
addressed in another condition-specific section.

\- Usual study options:

\- CTA by anatomic location

\- MRA by anatomic location

\- CT brain when relevant

\- CT or MRI chest as alternatives in chest cases

\### Traumatic Vascular Injury

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Usual study options:

\- CTA or MRA by anatomic location

\- CT chest when appropriate

\- Duplex arterial ultrasound for upper or lower extremity trauma

\### Vasculitis

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Usual study options:

\- CTA or MRA by anatomic location

\- MRI brain when relevant

\- CT chest when relevant

\## 2. Procedure-Related Imaging

\### Pre-TAVR/TAVI or Cardiac Surgery Vascular Evaluation

\- Medically necessary for preoperative vascular assessment.

\- Typical studies:

\- Carotid duplex ultrasound

\- CT/CTA chest, abdomen, pelvis

\- MRA chest, abdomen, pelvis

\- Neck CTA/MRA generally requires initial carotid duplex

\### Vascular Mapping Before Other Surgical or Interventional Procedures

\- Medically necessary when vascular anatomy must be defined before a
procedure.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA by procedural site

\- Exclusion:

\- Not applicable for dural venous sinus stenting or angioplasty
planning

\### Evaluation of Suspected Vascular Complications After a Procedure

\- Medically necessary when post-procedure vascular injury or
complication is suspected.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA by affected site

\## 3. Brain, Head, and Neck

\### Intracranial Aneurysm

\- Medically necessary for high-risk screening in patients such as:

\- Two or more first-degree relatives with intracranial aneurysm or
subarachnoid hemorrhage

\- Conditions associated with increased aneurysm risk

\- Known fibromuscular dysplasia

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis when there is reasonable suspicion,
such as:

\- Prior CT or MRI suspicious for aneurysm

\- Neurologic symptoms with relevant risk factors

\- Cranial nerve deficits

\- Surveillance:

\- Follow-up after diagnosis and after treatment is supported

\- Usual interval is initial follow-up at 6 to 12 months, then every 1
to 2 years if clinically appropriate

\- Imaging approach:

\- CTA head or MRA head

\- CT head is preferred first if subarachnoid hemorrhage is suspected

\### Extracranial Carotid or Vertebral Aneurysm

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA neck

\- MRA neck

\### Arteriovenous Malformation or Fistula

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA head or neck

\- MRA head or neck

\- CT head or MRI brain

\- Duplex ultrasound for extracranial lesions

\### Dissection, Intracranial or Extracranial

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound for extracranial dissection

\- CTA head/neck

\- MRA head/neck

\### Fibromuscular Dysplasia

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA head

\- CTA or MRA neck

\### Intracranial Hemorrhage

\- Adult:

\- Medically necessary when hemorrhage is established and features are
atypical for hypertensive bleed, or when subarachnoid hemorrhage is
suggested.

\- Pediatric:

\- Medically necessary for intracerebral hemorrhage.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA head

\- MRA head

\### Horner Syndrome

\- Medically necessary when a vascular lesion is suspected.

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound if extracranial dissection is a concern

\- CTA neck

\- MRA neck

\### Pulsatile Tinnitus

\- Medically necessary when a vascular lesion is suspected.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA head

\- CTA or MRA neck

\### Intracranial Stenosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Suspected disease in patients with predisposing congenital/genetic
disease

\- Pre-carotid revascularization evaluation for tandem intracranial
disease

\- Prior intracranial stenting

\- Known disease with new or progressive symptoms

\- Moyamoya surveillance when revascularization is being considered

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA head

\- MRA head

\### Extracranial Carotid Stenosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary in carotid revascularization candidates for:

\- Screening beginning 5 years after neck irradiation, then every 3
years

\- Suspected stenosis with retinal embolic findings

\- Known disease with worsening anterior circulation symptoms

\- Surveillance of established asymptomatic disease:

\- Moderate stenosis: about every 12 months

\- Severe stenosis: about every 6 months

\- Post-revascularization:

\- Baseline

\- Every 6 months for 2 years

\- Then annually

\- Imaging approach:

\- Duplex ultrasound is first line

\- CTA/MRA neck if ultrasound is nondiagnostic or shows moderate/severe
disease

\### Vertebral or Basilar Artery Stenosis/Occlusion

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Posterior circulation symptoms

\- Syncope after cardiac causes are excluded

\- Subclavian steal syndrome

\- Known disease with worsening posterior circulation symptoms

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA head

\- CTA or MRA neck

\### Stroke or TIA - Intracranial Evaluation

\- Medically necessary when there has been no intracranial vascular
imaging since the event and:

\- Stroke/TIA is acute or subacute

\- Chronic stroke/TIA has posterior circulation symptoms

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA head

\- MRA head

\### Stroke or TIA - Extracranial Evaluation

\- Medically necessary when there has been no carotid imaging since the
event and:

\- Stroke/TIA is acute or subacute

\- Chronic anterior circulation symptoms in patients who may need
carotid revascularization

\- Chronic posterior circulation symptoms

\- Imaging approach:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA/MRA neck for acute/subacute events and posterior circulation
cases

\- CTA/MRA neck for chronic anterior circulation disease when duplex is
nondiagnostic or cannot be done

\### Trigeminal Neuralgia

\- Medically necessary when a vascular lesion is suspected.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA head

\- MRA head

\### Intracranial Venous Thrombosis or Compression

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Suspected venous sinus thrombosis with symptoms plus exam, risk
factor, D-dimer, or suspicious/nondiagnostic brain imaging

\- Recurrence concern in known prior thrombosis

\- Follow-up of known thrombosis

\- Excluding compression from adjacent mass

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA or MRA head

\- CT brain or MRI brain

\### Extracranial Venous Thrombosis or Compression

\- Medically necessary after nondiagnostic venous ultrasound.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA neck

\- MRA neck

\## 4. Chest

\### Acute Aortic Syndrome

\- Includes dissection, rupture, intramural hematoma, penetrating ulcer,
and pseudoaneurysm.

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Initial diagnosis

\- Management of known disease

\- Annual surveillance of stable disease

\- Typical studies:

\- CT/CTA chest

\- MRA chest

\### Thoracic Aortic Aneurysm

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Annual screening in selected genetic/connective tissue disorders when
echo is inadequate or used as an alternative

\- Diagnosis of suspected aneurysm

\- New or progressive symptoms

\- 6-month stability check after a new diagnosis

\- Endoleak evaluation

\- Pre-procedure planning

\- Surveillance:

\- Annually if 4.4 cm or smaller

\- Every 6 months if larger than 4.4 cm

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA chest

\- MRA chest

\- CT chest or MRI chest as alternatives in some scenarios

\### Atheromatous Disease of the Thoracic Aorta

\- Adult only.

\- Medically necessary when searching for an aortic source of distal
emboli after cardiac source has not been found.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA chest

\- MRA chest if CTA cannot be done or is nondiagnostic

\### Pulmonary Hypertension

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation of suspected pulmonary hypertension, including chronic
thromboembolic disease

\- Extent-of-disease evaluation in chronic thromboembolic pulmonary
hypertension when surgery is being considered

\- Typical studies:

\- CT chest

\- CTA chest

\### Pulmonary Embolism

\- Adult:

\- Medically necessary when PE is likely by Wells score

\- Medically necessary when PE is unlikely by Wells score but D-dimer is
positive

\- Pregnancy: medically necessary when PE cannot be excluded by
pregnancy-adapted algorithm

\- Pediatric:

\- Medically necessary with moderate/high suspicion or concern for
recurrence despite therapy

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA chest preferred

\- CT chest as alternative

\- Important limitation:

\- MRA/MRI is not reliable enough to replace CTA or V/Q imaging as a
stand-alone exclusion test for PE

\### Other Chest Vascular Conditions

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management of:

\- Hematoma

\- Pulmonary AVM

\- Pulmonary sequestration

\- Subclavian steal syndrome

\- Superior vena cava syndrome

\- Systemic venous thrombosis/occlusion

\- Thoracic outlet syndrome

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA chest

\- MRA chest

\- CT chest or MRI chest as alternatives for SVC syndrome or thoracic
outlet syndrome

\## 5. Abdomen and Pelvis

\### Acute Aortic Syndrome

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Initial diagnosis

\- Management of known disease

\- Annual surveillance of stable disease

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA abdomen or abdomen/pelvis

\- MRA abdomen with or without pelvis

\- CT or MRI as alternatives in some cases

\### Abdominal Aortic or Iliac Aneurysm

\- Screening:

\- One-time evaluation in selected age/risk groups and in patients with
aneurysm elsewhere

\- Diagnosis:

\- Supported by pulsatile mass/bruit, suggestive but nondiagnostic
imaging, decreased femoral pulses, claudication, suggestive physiologic
testing, or distal embolic signs

\- Management:

\- New or worsening symptoms

\- Pre-procedure planning

\- Baseline and 12-month follow-up after endograft repair

\- Every 6 months when endograft enlarges or has endoleak

\- Surveillance:

\- AAA 4.5 cm or greater: every 6 months

\- AAA 3.5 to 4.4 cm: at 6 months and 12 months, then annually

\- AAA 3.0 to 3.4 cm: at 1 year, then every 3 years

\- Iliac aneurysm 3 cm or greater: every 6 months

\- Iliac aneurysm less than 3 cm: annually

\- Post-endograft stable repair: annual duplex and periodic CT

\- Post-open repair stable: every 5 years

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CT abdomen/pelvis

\- CTA abdomen/pelvis

\- MRI/MRA abdomen/pelvis

\### Mesenteric Ischemia

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA abdomen/pelvis

\- MRA abdomen/pelvis

\### Portal Hypertension

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA abdomen

\- MRA abdomen

\### Renal Artery Stenosis / Renovascular Hypertension

\- Medically necessary only in high pretest probability situations, such
as:

\- Resistant hypertension on multiple medications

\- Rapid worsening of previously controlled hypertension

\- Hypertension in younger patients

\- Hypertensive crisis

\- Hypertension with renal dysfunction

\- Severe hypertension with CKD or heart failure in older patients

\- Abdominal bruit suggesting renal artery disease

\- Flash pulmonary edema

\- Unexplained atrophic kidney or size asymmetry

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA abdomen

\- MRA abdomen

\### Aortoiliac / Abdominal Aortic Branch Vessel Stenosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary for suspected disease with:

\- Abdominal or femoral bruit

\- Reduced femoral pulse

\- Atypical buttock/thigh claudication

\- Leriche-type symptoms

\- Distal embolic signs

\- Suggestive physiologic testing

\- Established femoral or popliteal aneurysm

\- Also medically necessary for:

\- Presurgical evaluation when intervention is being considered

\- Annual surveillance of surgical bypass grafts

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA abdomen/pelvis

\- MRA abdomen/pelvis

\### Abdominal Venous Thrombosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Hepatic or portal vein evaluation when venous ultrasound is not
feasible or nondiagnostic

\- Evaluation of other major abdominal venous structures

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA abdomen or abdomen/pelvis

\- MRA abdomen with or without pelvis

\### Visceral Artery Aneurysm

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis, management, and surveillance of
aneurysm involving:

\- Renal artery

\- Celiac artery

\- Splenic artery

\- Hepatic artery

\- Mesenteric arteries and branches

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA abdomen/pelvis

\- MRA abdomen/pelvis

\## 6. Upper Extremity

\### Physiologic Testing for PAD

\- Medically necessary for:

\- New or worsening symptoms such as claudication, cold painful hand,
finger ulcer/discoloration, nonhealing ulcers, gangrene, or absent
pulses with infection

\- Arterial entrapment or positional obstruction

\- Arm/hand trauma with suspected vascular injury

\- Preoperative evaluation before dialysis access or arterial harvest

\- Suspected complication of arterial access

\- Post-revascularization baseline and early follow-up

\- Annual surveillance beginning 1 year after bypass grafting

\- Typical studies:

\- Limited, complete, or noninvasive physiologic studies

\### Upper Extremity PAD

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Suspected PAD with inconclusive physiologic testing

\- Known PAD with rest ischemia or distal embolic signs

\- Atypical symptoms with inconclusive physiologic testing

\- Persistent claudication despite conservative therapy when
revascularization is being considered

\- Baseline after revascularization

\- New/worsening symptoms after revascularization

\- Post-revascularization when surveillance physiologic testing is
inconclusive

\- Surveillance:

\- At 6 months, then annually after surgical revascularization

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA upper extremity

\- MRA upper extremity

\### Vascular Access Procedures

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Native artery evaluation before AV fistula/graft creation

\- Planned arterial harvesting

\- Suspected access complications such as pulsatile mass, bruit/thrill,
significant hematoma/skin change, severe pain, or distal
ischemia/embolism

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA or MRA upper extremity if ultrasound is not feasible or
nondiagnostic

\### Upper Extremity Venous Thrombosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary when ultrasound cannot be performed or is
nondiagnostic.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA upper extremity

\- MRA upper extremity

\### Other Upper Extremity Vascular Conditions

\- Medically necessary when imaging is essential to diagnose or direct
management of:

\- Aneurysm

\- Arterial entrapment syndrome

\- AVM/AVF

\- Dissection or intramural hematoma

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA upper extremity

\- MRA upper extremity

\## 7. Lower Extremity

\### Physiologic Testing for PAD

\- Medically necessary for:

\- New or worsening claudication

\- Rest pain with reduced pulses

\- Nonhealing ulcers or gangrene

\- Absent pulses

\- Acute limb ischemia

\- Baseline evaluation in newly diagnosed PAD

\- Pre-revascularization baseline

\- Post-revascularization baseline and surveillance

\- Surveillance intervals:

\- After surgical revascularization: every 3 months for 2 years, then
annually

\- After endovascular revascularization: every 4 months in year 1, then
annually

\- Typical studies:

\- Limited, complete, or noninvasive physiologic studies

\### Lower Extremity PAD

\- Screening:

\- Not indicated

\- Diagnosis:

\- Inconclusive physiologic testing with symptoms

\- Critical limb ischemia symptoms

\- Management:

\- New/worsening rest pain, wounds, gangrene, ischemic toes, livedo,
sudden severe ischemic symptoms

\- Persistent claudication after 3 months of conservative therapy in
patients being considered for revascularization

\- Baseline after revascularization

\- New/worsening pain after revascularization when physiologic testing
is nondiagnostic

\- Surveillance physiologic testing that is inconclusive, borderline, or
abnormal

\- Surveillance:

\- Venous graft: every 3 months for 2 years, then annually

\- Endovascular repair: every 4 months during year 1, then annually

\- Typical studies:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\- CTA lower extremity

\- MRA lower extremity

\- CTA abdominal aorta with bilateral runoff when inflow disease is
suspected

\### Popliteal Artery Aneurysm

\- Medically necessary for:

\- Suspected aneurysm

\- Known aneurysm with possible change in size or patency

\- Surveillance of small unrepaired aneurysms in revascularization
candidates

\- Post-open or post-endovascular repair at 3, 6, and 12 months, then
annually

\- Typical study:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\### Lower Extremity Vascular Access Complications

\- Medically necessary when there are signs of complication such as:

\- Pulsatile mass

\- Bruit or thrill

\- Significant hematoma or abnormal skin changes

\- Severe pain

\- Distal ischemia or embolic signs

\- Typical study:

\- Duplex ultrasound

\### Lower Extremity Venous Thrombosis or Occlusion

\- Medically necessary when venous ultrasound cannot be performed or is
nondiagnostic.

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA lower extremity

\- MRA lower extremity

\### Other Lower Extremity Vascular Conditions

\- Medically necessary when imaging is essential to diagnose or direct
management of:

\- Arterial entrapment syndrome

\- Aneurysm or dilation

\- AVM/AVF

\- Dissection or intramural hematoma

\- Typical studies:

\- CTA lower extremity

\- MRA lower extremity

\- CTA abdominal aorta with runoff when disease may originate proximally

\## 8. MR Angiography of the Spinal Canal

\- This technology is considered limited and still developing.

\- Current medically necessary uses are restricted to:

\- Preoperative or postoperative imaging

\- Follow-up of prior imaging findings suggesting a vascular lesion

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\### General Limitations

\- Conditions or modalities not specifically addressed are not medically
necessary.

\- Repeat testing of the same area for the same indication is limited
unless:

\- There has been an intervention, or

\- There has been a meaningful change in clinical status, or

\- A different technique is needed to clarify the original result

\- Multiple tests ordered at the same time may need additional review.

\- Ongoing services may depend on results from previously authorized
services.

\### Modality Limitations

\- Duplex ultrasound is operator dependent.

\- CTA limitations:

\- Radiation exposure

\- Iodinated contrast risks

\- MRA limitations:

\- Motion artifact

\- Device incompatibility

\- Claustrophobia/sedation issues

\- Gadolinium risk in advanced renal disease

\### Specific Exclusions / Noncovered or Not Indicated Situations

\- Dural venous sinus stenting/angioplasty planning is excluded from the
nonspecific pre-procedure vascular mapping indication.

\- Screening for lower extremity PAD is not indicated.

\- Advanced imaging is not indicated for asymptomatic lower extremity
PAD or intermittent claudication when the patient is not a
revascularization candidate.

\- Routine screening for asymptomatic carotid disease is generally not
supported outside specific high-risk scenarios.

\- MRA is not recommended as a stand-alone test to exclude pulmonary
embolism.

\- MR angiography of the spinal canal has very limited supported use.

\- Patient symptoms/signs

\- Suspected or confirmed vascular diagnosis

\- Relevant history and exam

\- Why first-line testing was insufficient, if applicable

\- Planned intervention/revascularization, if applicable

\- Conservative treatment history, if required

\- Post-op or surveillance timing details
