\## Core Medical Necessity Criteria

\### General Clinical Appropriateness Requirements

Advanced imaging is medically necessary when all of the following are
supported:

\- The diagnosis has been confirmed, or there is a documented and
meaningful pretest likelihood of disease.

\- The patient has had a complete clinical evaluation, which may
include:

\- History

\- Physical examination

\- Relevant laboratory studies

\- Prior diagnostic testing

\- Review of response to prior treatment when applicable

\- The expected benefit of imaging is likely to outweigh potential
harms.

\- The requested study is supported by accepted clinical guidelines,
published literature, or standards of care.

\- The imaging result is reasonably likely to change management or
improve patient outcome.

\### Clinical Documentation Standard

Submitted documentation should:

\- Reflect the patient's current clinical condition at the time of the
request.

\- Clearly show the ordering clinician's intent.

\- Support why the requested study is necessary now.

\### Multiple Studies Ordered at the Same Time

Simultaneous ordering of multiple diagnostic or therapeutic studies may
require additional review when:

\- The need for the second or third study depends on the result of the
first.

\- Another study may be more appropriate based on the clinical
situation.

\- One study is more likely to improve outcomes than the alternatives.

\### Repeat Diagnostic Testing

Repeat testing of the same anatomic area for the same indication is
generally limited to:

\- Follow-up after an intervention

\- A meaningful change in clinical status

\- Clarification of a prior finding using a different technique or
protocol

Additional review may be needed when repeat imaging is requested because
of:

\- Technical problems at the same facility

\- Provider preference for a different facility

\- Persistent symptoms without clinical change since prior imaging

\- Multiple requests by different providers over a short period

\### PET / PET-CT and Radiotracer Rules

\- PET imaging may use FDA-approved radiotracers.

\- If a specific non-FDG radiotracer has its own criteria, those
criteria apply.

\- If no specific non-FDG criteria exist, the general clinical
appropriateness framework applies.

\- PET/CT is most useful when imaging results are expected to affect
staging, treatment planning, recurrence assessment, or other management
decisions.

\## Required Documentation

\### General Documentation Requirements

Include, as applicable:

\- Relevant history and physical findings

\- Working diagnosis or documented malignancy

\- Signs, symptoms, or objective evidence supporting clinical suspicion

\- Phase of care:

\- Screening

\- Diagnosis

\- Initial staging / diagnostic workup

\- Management

\- Surveillance

\- Prior imaging reports with dates and findings

\- Relevant laboratory results

\- Pathology or biopsy results when available

\- Prior treatment history

\- Response to prior treatment if applicable

\- Explanation of why standard imaging is:

\- Contraindicated

\- Unavailable

\- Nondiagnostic

\- Expected to be suboptimal

\- Rationale for repeat imaging, if requested

\- Rationale for simultaneous studies, if requested

\### Documentation That May Be Needed for Specific Requests

Depending on indication, include:

\- PSA values and trends

\- AFP values and trends

\- Tumor stage

\- Treatment completion date

\- Evidence of recurrence or persistence

\- Radiation planning need

\- Salvage therapy candidacy

\- Conventional imaging results within the required timeframe

\- Prior PET/CT date to confirm interval restrictions are met

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\### General Limitations

\- The presence of an FDA-approved drug, device, or radiotracer alone
does not establish medical necessity.

\- Imaging must be expected to influence care; otherwise it may not be
appropriate.

\- Repeated imaging without a meaningful change in condition may be
denied or require added review.

\- Multiple imaging studies ordered together may require peer review.

\### Modality-Related Limitations

\- CT may be limited by contrast allergy, nephrotoxicity risk, and
radiation exposure.

\- MRI may be limited by:

\- Implanted devices

\- Claustrophobia

\- Motion artifact

\- Sedation needs

\- Gadolinium risk in advanced renal disease

\### General Imaging Selection Rule

\- MRI may be medically necessary when CT criteria are otherwise met but
CT is contraindicated or expected to be suboptimal.

\### PET / PET-CT Exclusion Pattern

\- PET/CT is not automatically appropriate for surveillance.

\- In several disease-specific sections, PET/CT is reserved for:

\- Equivocal findings

\- Nondiagnostic conventional imaging

\- Radiation planning

\- Recurrence assessment

\- Treatment selection

\## Condition-Specific Criteria

\## 1) Hepatobiliary and Biliary Tract Cancers

\### Medical Necessity Criteria

\#### MRI Abdomen With or Without MRCP

Indicated for either of the following:

\- Known cirrhosis or hepatitis B with positive or rising AFP

\- Documented hepatobiliary cancer

Also indicated for:

\- Management

\- Surveillance, but not more than every 6 months

\#### FDG-PET/CT

Indicated when:

\- Standard imaging cannot be performed, or

\- Standard imaging is nondiagnostic regarding extent of disease

Also indicated in management when:

\- Standard imaging cannot be performed, or

\- Standard imaging is nondiagnostic for recurrent or progressive
disease

Not indicated for:

\- Surveillance

\### Required Documentation

\- History of cirrhosis or hepatitis B, if applicable

\- AFP value and trend

\- Documentation of hepatobiliary malignancy, if established

\- Prior conventional imaging reports and why they are insufficient

\- Clinical purpose of imaging:

\- Initial extent of disease

\- Recurrence assessment

\- Progression assessment

\- Ongoing management

\### Limitations and Exclusions

\- PET/CT is not indicated for routine surveillance.

\- MRI may be preferred when CT contrast is contraindicated or CT
quality is expected to be poor.

\### Additional Note

\- Positive AFP threshold referenced in the section: AFP 200 ng/mL or
greater.

\## 2) Lung Cancer --- Small Cell

\### Medical Necessity Criteria

\#### Diagnostic Workup

\- Brain MRI with contrast is indicated regardless of stage during
initial evaluation.

\- PET/CT may improve staging accuracy in selected patients.

\- If PET/CT identifies lesions that would change stage, pathologic
confirmation is still required when upstaging would result.

\#### Management

\- Treatment response assessment is typically performed with CT of the
chest, abdomen, and pelvis.

\- PET/CT is not recommended for routine follow-up.

\#### Surveillance

\- CT of the chest and abdomen may be used every 3 to 4 months as
clinically indicated.

\- PET/CT has no routine role in surveillance after treatment.

\### Required Documentation

\- Histologic diagnosis

\- Disease stage or suspected stage

\- Prior imaging and staging workup

\- Reason for requesting PET/CT instead of or in addition to
conventional imaging

\- Documentation supporting recurrence, progression, or treatment
response assessment

\- Pathology plan if PET findings would upstage disease

\### Limitations and Exclusions

\- PET/CT is not indicated for routine surveillance.

\- PET findings that change stage should not be used alone when
pathologic confirmation is required.

\- MRI may be used when CT is contraindicated or likely to be
suboptimal.

\## 3) Hodgkin Lymphoma

\### Medical Necessity Criteria

\#### CT Neck / CT Chest / CT Abdomen and Pelvis

Diagnostic workup:

\- Indicated

\- May consider omitting if PET/CT has already been completed

Management:

\- Indicated

\- May consider omitting if PET/CT is being used to assess response to
chemotherapy

Surveillance:

\- Indicated

\- Not to exceed 2 years following completion of treatment

\#### FDG-PET/CT

Diagnostic workup:

\- Indicated

\- Especially useful as an adjunct to CT

Management:

\- Indicated for:

\- Radiation planning for definitive treatment

\- Radiation planning for consolidative treatment

Surveillance:

\- Not indicated

\### Required Documentation

\- Documented Hodgkin lymphoma

\- Phase of care

\- Prior CT and/or PET/CT findings

\- Need for radiation planning if PET/CT requested during management

\- Date treatment ended for surveillance requests

\- Need for response assessment after chemotherapy if applicable

\### Limitations and Exclusions

\- PET/CT is not indicated for surveillance.

\- CT surveillance should not continue beyond 2 years after treatment
completion based on the section shown.

\- Some CT studies may be omitted if PET/CT already provides the needed
information.

\## 4) Prostate Cancer --- PSMA PET/CT

\### Medical Necessity Criteria

\#### Diagnostic Workup

PSMA-targeted PET/CT is indicated for:

\- Unfavorable intermediate-risk disease or high-risk disease

\- Conventional imaging is equivocal or nondiagnostic

\- Confirmation may influence decisions about prostatectomy and/or
radiation therapy

\#### Management

Indicated when \*\*all\*\* of the following are met:

\- Original clinical stage was T1 to T3 and NX or N0

\- Patient was treated with prostatectomy and/or radiation therapy

\- There is biochemical recurrence or persistent disease

\- If PSA is 10 ng/mL or higher, conventional imaging within 60 days is
negative or nondiagnostic

\- Patient is a candidate for curative-intent salvage therapy

\- PET/CT has not been performed within the previous 3 months

Also indicated for:

\- Evaluation of metastatic castrate-resistant disease for radioligand
therapy when the patient was previously treated with any of the
following:

\- Abiraterone

\- Apalutamide

\- Enzalutamide

\- Darolutamide

\#### Surveillance

\- Not indicated

\### Definitions Included in the Section

\#### Biochemical Persistence / Recurrence After Prostatectomy

\- Persistence: PSA remains detectable within the first 3 months after
surgery

\- Recurrence: PSA becomes detectable again and rises to at least 0.2
ng/mL, with confirmatory elevation

\#### Biochemical Recurrence After Radiation Therapy

\- Rise of 2 ng/mL or more above PSA nadir

\#### Conventional Imaging Examples

\- Bone scan

\- CT abdomen and/or pelvis

\- MRI pelvis

\- Multiparametric prostate MRI

\### Required Documentation

\- Original clinical stage

\- Prior treatment details

\- PSA values and trend

\- Type and date of conventional imaging

\- Whether conventional imaging was negative, equivocal, or
nondiagnostic

\- Confirmation that PET/CT has not been done within 3 months

\- Evidence the patient is a candidate for salvage therapy when
applicable

\- Drug history showing prior use of listed androgen-receptor pathway
inhibitors if radioligand therapy evaluation is requested

\### Limitations and Exclusions

\- Not indicated for routine surveillance.

\- If PSA is under 10 ng/mL, the negative/nondiagnostic conventional
imaging requirement does not apply.

\- MRI may be used when CT is contraindicated or expected to be
suboptimal.

\## Practical Summary of What Supports Approval

A request is strongest when it clearly shows:

\- Why imaging is needed now

\- What specific question the imaging is meant to answer

\- How results will change treatment or management

\- Why standard imaging is insufficient, if PET/CT is being requested

\- Why repeat or multiple studies are necessary, if applicable

\-\--

\## Practical Summary of Common Denial Risks

Requests are weaker when they show:

\- No clear diagnosis or inadequate clinical suspicion

\- No evidence that imaging will change management

\- Repeat imaging without change in symptoms or status

\- Multiple studies ordered without justification

\- PET/CT requested for routine surveillance when the section does not
support it

\- Missing labs, staging details, prior imaging, or treatment history
