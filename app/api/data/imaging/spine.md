\## Medical Necessity Criteria

\## General Clinical Requirements

Advanced spine imaging is medically necessary only when:

\- The clinical evaluation supports a reasonable suspicion of disease or
a need for management change.

\- The expected benefit of imaging outweighs risks.

\- Imaging is likely to change management and/or improve outcome.

\- The requested study is supported by accepted clinical practice.

\### General Prerequisites for Spine Imaging

\- Evidence of nerve root or cord compression, such as:

\- Objective muscle weakness

\- Sensory abnormality matching a dermatome/myotome

\- Reflex changes

\- Spasticity

\- Conservative management, when applicable, including:

\- Physical therapy, and

\- At least 1 complementary conservative treatment

\### Physical Therapy Requirement

Must include any of the following:

\- Physical therapy by a qualified provider

\- Supervised home treatment program with all of the following:

\- Patient-specific/tailored program

\- Initial active instruction by physician or allied health provider

\- Redemonstration that the patient can perform exercises

\- Compliance documented or attested at follow-up

\- Exception allowed when clearly documented, such as intractable pain
preventing therapy

\### Complementary Conservative Treatment Requirement

Must include at least 1 of the following when not contraindicated:

\- Prescription-strength anti-inflammatory medications

\- Analgesics

\- Nerve membrane stabilizers

\- Muscle relaxants

\- Epidural steroid injection

\- Alternative therapies where applicable:

\- Acupuncture

\- Chiropractic care

\- Massage therapy

\- Activity modification

\- Trial period of rest

\### Reevaluation / Failure of Conservative Management

\- Reevaluation should usually include physical examination

\- Telephone or electronic follow-up may substitute when necessary

\- Failure of conservative management means:

\- Full course completed, and

\- No significant improvement, or worsening, and

\- More invasive therapy is being considered

\### Repeat Diagnostic Imaging

Repeated imaging of the same area for the same indication is generally
limited to:

\- Evaluation after an intervention

\- A change in clinical status requiring new management decisions

Additional review may be needed for:

\- Repeat testing due to technical issues

\- Repeat testing at another facility for preference/quality concerns

\- Persistent symptoms without clinical change or treatment since prior
study

\- Multiple providers ordering duplicate studies in a short interval

\## Condition-Specific Medical Necessity Criteria

\## Congenital and Developmental Conditions

\### Congenital Spinal Cord Anomalies Not Otherwise Listed

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis and management when imaging results will affect treatment
decisions

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine (preferred)

Notes:

\- Spina bifida occulta should not be imaged unless symptomatic and
concern exists for tethered cord

\### Congenital Vertebral Defects

Includes:

\- Skeletal dysplasia

\- Segmentation anomalies

\- Fusion anomalies

Medical necessity:

\- Following nondiagnostic radiographs when imaging will affect
treatment

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine (preferred)

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\### Craniocervical Junction Abnormalities

Includes:

\- Atlantoaxial instability

\- Occipital instability

\- Basilar invagination

Medical necessity:

\- Following nondiagnostic radiographs in patients with any high-risk
condition:

\- Down syndrome

\- Grisel syndrome

\- Skeletal dysplasia

\- Rheumatoid arthritis

Imaging:

\- CT cervical spine

\- MRI cervical spine

\### Scoliosis

Medical necessity:

\- Congenital scoliosis

\- Juvenile scoliosis

\- Neuromuscular scoliosis

\- Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis with any of the following:

\- Early onset before age 10

\- Atypical curve pattern:

\- Left thoracic

\- Right lumbar

\- Neurologic signs or symptoms

\- Presurgical planning with Cobb angle at least 40 degrees

\- Persistent significant neurogenic symptoms with functional impairment
not responsive to at least 3 months of conservative management

\- Severe degenerative scoliosis for presurgical planning when:

\- Cobb angle at least 30 degrees, or sagittal vertical axis greater
than 5 cm

\- And either:

\- Documented progression with persistent axial pain and functional
impairment despite at least 3 months of conservative management

\- Persistent significant neurogenic symptoms with functional impairment
despite at least 3 months of conservative management

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

Notes:

\- Lower thresholds may be appropriate in high-risk patients

\- MRI should be favored in pediatric patients requiring extensive spine
imaging to reduce radiation

\### Spinal Dysraphism and Tethered Cord

Includes:

\- Closed dysraphism:

\- Lipomyelocele

\- Lipomyelomeningocele

\- Dermal sinus

\- Open dysraphism:

\- Meningocele

\- Myelocele

\- Myelomeningocele

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis and management in patients older than 5 months

\- Diagnosis and management after nondiagnostic ultrasound in patients 5
months or younger

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine when MRI cannot be performed

Notes:

\- Only lumbar imaging is required when evaluating tethered cord

\## Infectious and Inflammatory Conditions

\### Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (Pediatric Only)

Medical necessity:

\- Management of established disease when radiographs are insufficient
to guide therapy

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine when MRI cannot be performed
or is nondiagnostic

\### Multiple Sclerosis or Other White Matter Disease

Medical necessity:

\#### Diagnosis

\- Signs or symptoms of cervical and/or thoracic myelopathy

\- Initial evaluation of clinically isolated syndrome or a new clinical
attack without established MS diagnosis

\- Suspected MS when brain MRI has not established another cause and
does not satisfy diagnostic criteria

\#### Management

\- New or recurrent signs or symptoms

\- Recent or current natalizumab use

\- New baseline before starting or changing therapy

\- After change in disease-modifying therapy:

\- Initial imaging at 3 to 6 months

\- Follow-up at 6 to 12 months

\- Other white matter diseases

\#### Surveillance

\- Clinically isolated syndrome or radiologically isolated syndrome:

\- At 3 to 6 months after presentation

\- At 6 to 12 months after presentation

\- Annually thereafter

\- Annual evaluation in stable patients with known cervical or thoracic
cord lesions and no therapy change

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical or thoracic spine

\### Spinal Infection

Includes:

\- Epidural abscess

\- Arachnoiditis

\- Discitis

\- Vertebral osteomyelitis

Medical necessity:

\- Suspected infection with spinal pain or neurologic abnormalities and
any of the following:

\- Recent spinal intervention

\- Decubitus ulcer or wound overlying the spine

\- Fever

\- Elevated ESR or CRP

\- Known bloodstream infection

\- Risk factors:

\- Diabetes mellitus

\- Intravenous drug use

\- Malignancy

\- HIV

\- Dialysis

\- Management of known infection with poor response to therapy based on
clinical and ESR/CRP assessment

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine when MRI cannot be performed
or is nondiagnostic

\- FDG-PET/CT for chronic osteomyelitis, or when MRI cannot be performed
and CT is nondiagnostic

\### Axial Spondyloarthropathy

Includes:

\- Ankylosing spondylitis

\- Reactive arthritis

\- Psoriatic arthritis

\- Spondyloarthropathy associated with inflammatory bowel disease

\- Juvenile-onset spondyloarthritis

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis when all of the following are present:

\- Inflammatory back pain for at least 3 months

\- Radiographs and MRI of sacroiliac joints are negative or equivocal
for sacroiliitis

\- Management when either:

\- Patient on biologic therapy has unclear disease activity after full
clinical and laboratory evaluation and MRI progression would change
management

\- Suspected fracture in known spinal ankylosis

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine (preferred)

Inflammatory back pain features may include:

\- Insidious onset

\- Improves with exercise

\- No improvement with rest

\- Occurs at night

\- Onset before age 40

\## Trauma

\### Cervical Injury - Adult

Medical necessity:

\#### Initial Diagnosis

\- Any of the following:

\- Abnormal radiographs suggesting fracture

\- Posterior midline cervical tenderness after initial radiographs

\- Altered consciousness or intoxication

\- Focal neurologic deficit

\- High-risk mechanism including penetrating neck trauma

\- Distracting injuries including other fractures

\- Trauma within 48 hours with limited neck motion or unstable vital
signs

\- Age over 65 years

\- Known cervical spine disease predisposing to fracture:

\- Inflammatory arthritis

\- Osteoporosis

\- Prior cervical spine surgery

\- Malignancy or primary bone neoplasm

\#### Management

\- Post-traumatic neurologic deficit

\- Soft tissue injury suggested by CT or radiography

\- Worsening pain unexplained by CT

\- Follow-up of known fracture

\- Presurgical planning

Imaging:

\- CT cervical spine for initial diagnosis or management

\- MRI cervical spine for management, except follow-up of known fracture

\### Cervical Injury - Pediatric

Medical necessity:

\- Initial diagnosis of acute significant trauma:

\- After nondiagnostic radiographs in children age 3 or older meeting
PECARN or NEXUS criteria

\- Or children under age 3 with Pieretti-Vanmarcke weight score of 1 or
greater

\- Diagnosis or management with any of the following:

\- Post-traumatic neurologic deficit

\- Soft tissue injury suggested by CT or radiography

\- Worsening pain unexplained by CT

\- Follow-up of known fracture

\- Presurgical planning

Imaging:

\- CT cervical spine

\- MRI cervical spine

\### Thoracic or Lumbar Injury

Medical necessity:

\#### Initial Diagnosis

\- High-risk patient with any of the following:

\- Midline thoracolumbar tenderness

\- High-energy mechanism

\- Age over 60

\- Unexaminable patient:

\- Intoxicated

\- GCS less than 15

\- Distracting injury

\- Ankylosing spondylitis

\- DISH

\- Or after nondiagnostic spine radiographs

\#### Management

\- Post-traumatic neurologic deficit

\- Ligamentous injury suggested by CT or radiography

\- High-risk patient with worsening pain unexplained by CT

\- Follow-up of symptomatic fracture with new/worsening symptoms

\- Presurgical planning

Imaging:

\- CT thoracic or lumbar spine

\- MRI thoracic or lumbar spine for management, except follow-up of
symptomatic fracture

\-\--

\## Tumor

\### Spinal Mass / Tumor

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis or management of a mass in:

\- Spinal cord

\- Vertebrae

\- Adjacent soft tissue

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

Note:

\- Documented malignancy management may fall under separate oncology
imaging guidance

\## Miscellaneous Conditions

\### Cauda Equina Syndrome

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis and management when imaging results will affect treatment

Concerning associated signs/symptoms:

\- Severe bilateral sciatica

\- Saddle or genital sensory disturbance

\- Bladder dysfunction

\- Bowel dysfunction

\- Sexual dysfunction

Imaging:

\- CT lumbar spine

\- MRI lumbar spine

\### Myelopathy

Medical necessity:

\- Evaluation of myelopathic signs or symptoms

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine as an alternative in pediatric
patients or when MRI cannot be performed in adults

\### Osteoporosis and Osteopenia

Medical necessity:

\#### Screening / Diagnostic

\- Women older than 64

\- Postmenopausal women under 65 with increased risk factors

\- Men age 70 or older

\- Men age 50 to 69 with clinical risk factors

\- Persons receiving medications associated with osteoporosis

\- New fracture after age 50

\- Diseases/conditions associated with osteoporosis, including:

\- Anorexia nervosa

\- Chronic liver disease

\- Chronic renal failure

\- Cushing syndrome

\- Delayed menarche or untreated premature menopause

\- Heavy alcohol use

\- Hypercalciuria

\- Hypogonadism

\- Inflammatory bowel disease

\- Low-trauma fractures or vertebral fractures

\- Malabsorption syndromes

\- Primary hyperparathyroidism

\- Prolonged immobilization

\- Radiographic osteopenia

\- Rheumatoid arthritis

\- Thyroid disease

\- Patients considering osteoporosis therapy if bone mineral density
will aid decision-making

\#### Management

\- Every 2 to 3 years in treated osteoporosis/osteopenia

\- Every 3 to 5 years in untreated individuals who met initial criteria
and have no interval risk factor

Imaging:

\- CT bone density

\### Paget Disease

Medical necessity:

\- Determine extent of disease after suggestive radiographs

\- Monitor treatment response in patients with normal baseline bone
turnover markers

\- Evaluate malignant transformation of pagetoid lesions

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine for malignant transformation

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine for malignant transformation

\### Spinal Cord Infarction

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis and management when imaging will affect treatment

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine when MRI cannot be performed
or is nondiagnostic

\### Spondylolysis (Pars Defect)

Medical necessity:

\- Suspected spondylolysis after nondiagnostic lumbar radiographs

Imaging:

\- CT lumbar spine

\- MRI lumbar spine

\### Spontaneous Intracranial Hypotension

Medical necessity:

\- Localize CSF leak before epidural blood patch in confirmed or
suspected cases

\- Guide repeat blood patch placement or presurgical planning in
refractory symptoms

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine when MRI cannot be performed
or is nondiagnostic

\### Syringomyelia

Includes:

\- Syrinx

\- Hydromyelia

\- Hydrosyringomyelia

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis

\- Management

\- Single surveillance study to confirm stability

Imaging:

\- MRI cervical or thoracic spine

\- CT cervical or thoracic spine when MRI cannot be performed or is
nondiagnostic

Notes:

\- Repeat MRI with contrast may be appropriate if the syrinx was first
seen on a noncontrast study

\- Surveillance assumes no new/worsening symptoms and prior imaging
stability

\### Vertebral Compression Fracture

Medical necessity:

\- New symptomatic fracture on radiograph when vertebroplasty or
kyphoplasty is being considered

\- Compression fracture with history of malignancy

\- Previously treated compression fracture with new back pain

\- Suspected fracture in known spinal ankylosis

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine (preferred)

\## Procedural Imaging

\### Postoperative / Postprocedural Imaging

Includes:

\- Delayed hardware failure

\- Healing related to prior surgery

\- Other postoperative/postprocedural conditions not addressed elsewhere

Medical necessity:

\- Diagnosis and management after nondiagnostic radiographs

Imaging:

\- CT cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine (excluding CT discography)

\- MRI cervical, thoracic, or lumbar spine

Notes:

\- Acute postoperative complications should be handled under spinal
infection criteria

\- Pain after surgery beyond the perioperative period should be handled
under pain indications

\## Pain, Radiculopathy, Spinal Stenosis

\### Important Use Limitation for This Section

These pain-based criteria should not be used when there is:

\- Infection

\- Malignancy

\- Other systemic pathology

\- Acute trauma

Use the condition-specific section instead.

\### Neck Pain or Cervical Radiculopathy

Medical necessity:

\- Neurologic exam findings suggesting cervical root or cord compression
not previously imaged or new since prior imaging

\- Pain or radiculopathy after at least 6 weeks of conservative
management

Imaging:

\- CT cervical spine (excluding CT discography) when MRI cannot be
performed or is nondiagnostic

\- MRI cervical spine

\### Mid-Back Pain or Thoracic Radiculopathy

Medical necessity:

\- Neurologic exam findings suggesting thoracic root or cord compression
not previously imaged or new since prior imaging

\- Pain or radiculopathy after at least 6 weeks of conservative
management

Imaging:

\- CT thoracic spine (excluding CT discography) when MRI cannot be
performed or is nondiagnostic

\- MRI thoracic spine

\### Low Back Pain or Lumbar Radiculopathy - Adult

Medical necessity:

\- Neurologic exam findings suggesting lumbar root or cord compression
not previously imaged or new since prior imaging

\- Pain or radiculopathy after at least 6 weeks of conservative
management

Imaging:

\- CT lumbar spine (excluding CT discography) when MRI cannot be
performed or is nondiagnostic

\- MRI lumbar spine

\### Low Back Pain or Lumbar Radiculopathy - Pediatric

Medical necessity:

\- Pain with nondiagnostic radiographs and any of the following:

\- Constant

\- Occurs at night

\- Radicular

\- Longer than 4 weeks and not responsive to conservative management

\- Neurologic exam findings suggesting lumbar root or cord compression
not previously imaged or new since prior imaging

Imaging:

\- CT lumbar spine (excluding CT discography) when MRI cannot be
performed or is nondiagnostic

\- MRI lumbar spine

\### Spinal Stenosis and Spondylolisthesis

Medical necessity:

\- Acute onset neurogenic claudication when patient is not a candidate
for conservative management because of intractable pain

\- Chronic neurogenic claudication not responsive to at least 6 weeks of
conservative management

\- Spondylolisthesis with instability on lumbar radiographs

Imaging:

\- CT lumbar spine (excluding CT discography) when MRI cannot be
performed or is nondiagnostic

\- MRI lumbar spine

\## Required Documentation

\## Core Documentation Required for Most Requests

\- Complete history and physical examination

\- Clear clinical indication for imaging

\- Ordering provider intent

\- Prior relevant imaging results

\- Whether imaging is for diagnosis, management, or surveillance

\- Evidence that imaging is expected to change management

\## When Conservative Management Applies

Document:

\- Type of physical therapy used

\- Home exercise/supervised program details

\- Complementary conservative treatments tried

\- Dates and duration of treatment

\- Clinical reevaluation findings

\- Response to treatment

\- Failure or worsening despite treatment

\- Reason for any exception to conservative management requirements

\## When Nerve Root or Cord Compression Is Claimed

Document:

\- Objective weakness

\- Sensory deficit

\- Reflex abnormality

\- Spasticity

\- Dermatome/myotome correlation

\## When Prior Testing Must Be Shown as Inadequate

Document:

\- Nondiagnostic radiographs, ultrasound, CT, or MRI as applicable

\- Why MRI cannot be performed, if CT is requested instead

\- Why prior imaging was insufficient to guide management

\## Condition-Specific Documentation Examples

\### Spinal Infection

\- Spinal pain or neurologic abnormalities

\- Fever, ESR, CRP, bloodstream infection, wound/ulcer

\- Risk factors such as diabetes, IV drug use, HIV, malignancy, dialysis

\- Response to therapy if imaging is for management

\### Axial Spondyloarthropathy

\- Duration of inflammatory back pain

\- Sacroiliac radiograph/MRI results

\- Clinical and laboratory evaluation

\- Biologic therapy status

\- Whether MRI findings would alter management

\### Multiple Sclerosis / White Matter Disease

\- Symptoms/signs of myelopathy

\- Brain MRI findings if relevant

\- Established diagnosis or new clinical attack

\- Current or planned disease-modifying therapy

\- Whether request is diagnostic, management-related, or surveillance

\### Trauma

\- Mechanism of injury

\- Neurologic findings

\- Vital sign instability if applicable

\- Radiograph/CT findings

\- High-risk factors

\- Age-specific trauma criteria when pediatric

\### Osteoporosis / Osteopenia

\- Age, sex, and risk factors

\- Fracture history

\- Relevant comorbidities

\- Medication exposures

\- Whether imaging is screening, diagnostic, or follow-up

\### Pain / Radiculopathy / Stenosis

\- Duration of symptoms

\- Neurologic findings

\- Conservative treatment details

\- Functional impairment

\- Whether surgery or intervention is being considered

\## Limitations and Exclusions

\- Indications, diagnoses, or imaging modalities not specifically
addressed are considered not medically necessary.

\- Pain-based criteria should not be used when infection, malignancy,
systemic pathology, or trauma is present.

\- Spina bifida occulta should not be imaged unless symptomatic with
concern for tethered cord.

\- Only lumbar imaging is needed when evaluating tethered cord.

\- MRI is preferred for most soft tissue spine indications.

\- CT is generally used when:

\- MRI cannot be performed

\- MRI is nondiagnostic

\- Bony detail is needed

\- Contrast MRI may be needed for:

\- Infection

\- Tumor

\- Postsurgical evaluation

\- Contraindications to MRI may include:

\- Unsafe implanted devices

\- Claustrophobia

\- Repeated imaging of the same anatomic area without clinical change,
treatment, or intervention may be subject to extra review.

\- Simultaneous ordering of multiple studies may require additional
review.

\- Nonspecific or unlisted procedure codes may require extra
documentation.

\- The code list is not all-inclusive.

\- Authorization requirements may vary by health plan.

\- Early or routine imaging for uncomplicated pain without qualifying
findings is generally not supported.

\- For pediatric back pain without red flags, imaging is generally not
recommended.

\- For documented malignancy management, separate oncology imaging
guidance may apply.

\## Bottom Line Summary

Advanced spine imaging is generally medically necessary when:

\- There is objective neurologic compromise,

\- A defined high-risk condition is present,

\- Conservative treatment has failed when required,

\- Prior imaging is nondiagnostic when that is a prerequisite,

\- Or imaging is needed to guide intervention, surgery, or management of
serious disease.

MRI is generally preferred, while CT is mainly an alternative when MRI
is not possible, nondiagnostic, or when bone detail is the primary need.
