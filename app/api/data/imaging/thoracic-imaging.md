\# 1. Medical Necessity Criteria

\## A. General Clinical Requirements

Advanced imaging is generally appropriate when all of the following are
supported:

\- A confirmed diagnosis or a reasonable pretest likelihood based on:

\- History

\- Physical examination

\- Relevant laboratory studies when applicable

\- Prior diagnostic testing when applicable

\- Response to prior treatment/intervention when applicable

\- Expected benefit outweighs potential harm

\- The requested study is supported by current standards of care or
accepted clinical literature

\- The study is likely to change management or improve outcome

\### Repeat Testing

Repeat imaging of the same anatomic area for the same indication is
generally limited to:

\- Evaluation after an intervention

\- Change in clinical status requiring reassessment

\- Clarification of a prior finding using a different technique/protocol

\### Concurrent Multiple Tests

Ordering multiple diagnostic or therapeutic interventions at the same
time may require added review when:

\- Medical necessity depends on the result of the first study

\- One requested intervention may be more appropriate than another

\- One requested intervention is more likely to improve outcomes

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management

\## C. Infectious and Inflammatory Conditions

\### Pneumonia

Medically necessary in any of the following:

\- Radiographs show no improvement after at least 4 weeks of medical
treatment

\- Recurrence of pneumonia in the same location within 6 months

\- Evaluation of known or suspected complications after nondiagnostic
radiographs

\- Immunosuppressed patient with signs/symptoms of pneumonia

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Other infectious or inflammatory conditions

Medically necessary for diagnosis and management of:

\- Lung abscess

\- Sternal wound infection or dehiscence

\- Mediastinitis

\- Other infectious/inflammatory conditions not otherwise listed

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\## E. Tumor or Neoplasm

\### Chest wall mass

Medically necessary in any of the following:

\- Palpable chest wall mass with nondiagnostic radiograph or ultrasound

\- Chest wall mass seen on prior imaging when more information is needed
to decide on biopsy or surgery

\- Preoperative planning after biopsy

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest

\### Pulmonary nodule or mass

\#### Pulmonary nodules detected on lung cancer screening CT

\- Follow-up according to the current Lung-RADS pathway

\#### Calcified nodules on diagnostic chest CT

\- Follow-up is at the ordering provider\'s discretion unless there is a
benign calcification pattern

\#### Noncalcified nodules on diagnostic chest CT

\##### Younger than age 35

\- Nodules 1 cm or larger, or

\- Suspicious morphology, including irregular or spiculated margins

\##### Age 35 or older

\- Solid nodules: follow size/risk-based pathway

\- Subsolid nodules: follow size/type-based pathway

\#### Nodules found on incomplete thoracic CT

\- Less than 6 mm:

\- Follow low-risk/high-risk approach

\- 6 mm to 8 mm:

\- 3 to 12 month follow-up with complete chest CT, then based on
characterization

\- Greater than 8 mm or suspicious morphology:

\- Complete chest CT, then follow-up based on characterization

\#### FDG-PET / FDG-PET-CT criteria

Use when both are met:

\- Nodule is well-demarcated, solid or part-solid, and lacks a benign
calcification pattern

\- Size is greater than 8 mm in greatest diameter

\#### Solid noncalcified pulmonary nodule follow-up

\- Less than 6 mm, low risk:

\- No follow-up

\- Less than 6 mm, high risk:

\- Optional follow-up at 12 months

\- 6 mm to 8 mm, solitary:

\- 6 to 12 months

\- 18 to 24 months

\- 6 mm to 8 mm, multiple:

\- 3 to 6 months

\- 18 to 24 months

\- More than 8 mm, solitary:

\- 3 months

\- 6 months

\- 18 to 24 months unless PET/CT or tissue sampling gives a diagnosis

\- More than 8 mm, multiple:

\- 3 to 6 months

\- 18 to 24 months

\- Any size with documented 24 months stability:

\- No follow-up

\#### High-risk features for pulmonary nodules

\- Any smoking history

\- First-degree relative with lung cancer

\- Significant exposure to asbestos, uranium, or radon

\#### Subsolid noncalcified pulmonary nodule follow-up

\- Less than 6 mm:

\- Solitary ground glass: no routine follow-up

\- Solitary part-solid: no routine follow-up

\- Multiple subsolid:

\- 3 to 6 months

\- 24 months

\- 48 months

\- 6 mm or greater:

\- Solitary ground glass:

\- 6 to 12 months

\- Then every 2 years for total of 5 years

\- Solitary part-solid:

\- 3 to 6 months

\- Then yearly for 5 years

\- Multiple subsolid:

\- 3 to 6 months

\- Then based on most suspicious nodule

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- FDG-PET / FDG-PET-CT when criteria above are met

\### Lymphadenopathy

Thoracic lymphadenopathy is defined as at least one lymph node greater
than 1 cm in short axis.

Medically necessary for diagnosis, management, or surveillance in any of
the following:

\- Palpable thoracic or supraclavicular lymph nodes not amenable to
percutaneous biopsy

\- Associated clinical or lab findings suggestive of malignancy,
especially lymphoma or testicular carcinoma

\- Mediastinal or hilar lymph nodes with any of the following:

\- Suspected by non-advanced imaging such as chest radiography

\- Single follow-up at least 3 months after discovery of node short axis
greater than 1.4 cm without suspicious features

\- Suspicious features:

\- Necrosis

\- Loss of fatty hilar morphology

\- Heterogeneous or hypervascular enhancement

\- Irregular borders

\- Interval enlargement

\- Multiple enlarged ipsilateral mediastinal nodes

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- FDG-PET/CT when multiple abnormal nodes are present and CT cannot
identify the best node for biopsy

\### Other thoracic mass lesions

Medically necessary for diagnosis and management of:

\- Mediastinal mass

\- Pancoast tumor

\- Pleural mass

\- Thymoma

\- Benign tumors (pediatric only)

Imaging study:

\- Adult:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest when CT is insufficient for problem solving or for chest
wall extension in Pancoast tumor

\- Pediatric:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest

\## F. Parenchymal Lung Disease

\### Bronchiectasis

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- Consider HRCT technique

\### Bronchiolitis obliterans

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Interstitial lung disease (ILD), non-occupational, including
idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF)

Medically necessary in any of the following:

\#### Diagnosis when any are present

\- Persistent breathlessness on exertion

\- Bilateral inspiratory crackles on physical exam

\- Clubbing of fingers

\- Suggestive findings on other diagnostic tests:

\- Chest radiography

\- Pulmonary function testing

\- Additional risk factors, including any of:

\- Connective tissue disease

\- Predisposing drugs

\- Known telomerase mutation

\- Familial ILD/IPF with at least two affected first-degree relatives

\#### Management when any are present

\- Worsening pulmonary signs or symptoms

\- Progression on chest radiography or pulmonary function testing

\- Need to direct biopsy when initial imaging does not show definite UIP
pattern

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- HRCT preferred

\## I. Perioperative or Periprocedural Evaluation

\### Lung volume reduction procedures

Medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation before planned lung volume reduction procedures

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Navigational bronchoscopy planning for pulmonary mass or nodule

Medically necessary when used for either:

\- Planning a biopsy when neither percutaneous biopsy nor traditional

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

Medically necessary for:

\- Single evaluation before lung, liver, kidney, or hematopoietic stem
cell transplantation

\- Evaluation for complications after lung, liver, kidney, or
hematopoietic stem cell transplantation

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\## J. Signs and Symptoms

\### Cough (chronic or persistent)

Cough must be present for at least 8 weeks and either:

\- Not responding to appropriate treatment and unexplained by:

\- Clinical evaluation

\- Chest radiography

\- Pulmonary function testing or spirometry

\- Cough in immunosuppressed individuals

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Dyspnea

Both must apply:

\- Dyspnea is not explained by cardiac evaluation

\- Dyspnea is not explained by chest radiography

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Fever of unknown origin

Medically necessary in either:

\- Fever lasting more than 3 weeks and unexplained after standard
diagnostic evaluation

\- Unexplained fever in an immunocompromised patient

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Hemoptysis

Medically necessary for:

\- Evaluation after nondiagnostic chest radiographs

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest for suspected vascular anomaly in pediatric patients

\### Hoarseness / dysphonia / vocal cord weakness or paralysis

Medically necessary for initial evaluation in either:

\- After laryngoscopy, when findings suggest recurrent laryngeal nerve
dysfunction or a suspicious lesion

\- Symptoms persisting longer than 1 month and unexplained by
laryngoscopy

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Horner\'s syndrome

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest

\### Paraneoplastic syndrome

\- Medically necessary for diagnosis and management

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Weight loss

Unintentional weight loss must exceed 5% of body weight within 12 months
and either:

\- Persist after a negative comprehensive clinical evaluation and
observation period

\- Have abnormal findings suggestive of malignancy on history, physical
exam, imaging, or laboratory evaluation

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\## K. Abnormal Test Findings

\### Imaging abnormalities

Medically necessary for follow-up of any of the following seen on chest
X-ray or other thoracic imaging:

\- Pulmonary mass, structural abnormality, or parenchymal abnormality

\- Hilar enlargement or mediastinal widening

\- Hyperlucent lung in pediatric patients

\- Unexplained diaphragmatic elevation or immobility

\- Findings suggesting tracheal or bronchial pathology

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Positive sputum cytology

\- Medically necessary for follow-up

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\### Tracheal or bronchial lesion or other findings on bronchoscopy

\- Medically necessary for follow-up

\- Imaging study:

\- CT chest

\# 3. Required Documentation

\## Core Documentation for Most Requests

\- Current history and physical examination

\- Clear clinical indication for the requested study

\- Relevant signs and symptoms

\- Duration of symptoms when relevant

\- Relevant laboratory results when applicable

\- Prior imaging results

\- Prior non-advanced imaging, especially chest radiographs

\- Results of prior diagnostic testing relevant to the indication

\- Prior treatment and response to treatment

\- Explanation of how imaging will change management

\- Ordering provider\'s clinical intent

\- Documentation must accurately reflect the patient\'s status at the
time of request

\## Typical Prerequisite Documentation by Scenario

\### General thoracic imaging

\- Chest radiograph within 30 days in most clinical situations

\- Reason radiographs are insufficient to guide management

\### Pneumonia

\- Prior radiographs

\- Duration of treatment

\- Lack of radiographic improvement after at least 4 weeks, recurrence,
complications, or immunosuppression status

\### Chronic cough

\- Symptom duration of at least 8 weeks

\- Prior clinical evaluation

\- Chest radiography result

\- Pulmonary function testing or spirometry result

\- Prior treatment and failure of response

\- Immunosuppression status if applicable

\### Dyspnea

\- Cardiac evaluation results

\- Chest radiography result

\- Confirmation that both were nondiagnostic

\### Fever of unknown origin

\- Duration of fever

\- Standard diagnostic workup showing no source

\- Immunocompromised status if applicable

\### Hemoptysis

\- Nondiagnostic chest radiographs

\- Bronchoscopy results if performed

\- Pediatric concern for vascular anomaly if MRI requested

\### Hoarseness/dysphonia/vocal cord weakness

\- Laryngoscopy findings

\- Duration of symptoms

\- Why laryngoscopy was nondiagnostic or suspicious

\### Weight loss

\- Amount of unintentional weight loss

\- Time interval over which loss occurred

\- Comprehensive clinical evaluation results

\- Age-appropriate cancer screening results

\- Chest radiography

\- Initial laboratory evaluation

\- Observation period details

\- Suspicious findings if present

\### ILD/IPF

\- Symptoms and physical findings

\- Chest radiography and/or pulmonary function testing

\- Relevant risk factors

\- Need for biopsy targeting or reassessment of progression

\### Pulmonary nodule

\- Size

\- Morphology

\- Solid vs subsolid status

\- Calcification pattern

\- Patient age

\- Risk factors

\- Prior imaging for stability comparison

\### Lymphadenopathy

\- Node size

\- Location

\- Imaging features

\- Suspicious characteristics

\- Prior imaging interval

\- Why percutaneous biopsy is not feasible if applicable

\### Breast implant rupture

\- Symptoms

\- Implant type (silicone vs saline)

\### Pectoralis tear

\- Concern for full-thickness tear

\- Need for preoperative planning

\### Transplant imaging

\- Type of transplant

\- Whether pre-transplant evaluation or post-transplant complication
assessment is requested

\### Navigational bronchoscopy planning

\- Pulmonary mass or nodule details

\- Why traditional bronchoscopy or percutaneous biopsy cannot be
performed

\- Whether purpose is biopsy planning or fiducial marker placement

\### Repeat imaging

\- Change in clinical status, post-intervention need, or reason prior
study requires clarification

\- If repeat study is at a different facility, reason for change

\# 4. Limitations and Exclusions

\## General Exclusions / Limits

\- Indications, diagnoses, or imaging modalities not specifically
addressed are considered not medically necessary

\- Chest imaging is not appropriate for:

\- Cardiac imaging

\- Coronary artery imaging

\- Thoracic vascular imaging is addressed separately

\- In most cases, chest radiographs should be performed first and should
be recent

\- Repeated imaging without clinical change, treatment, or intervention
may be subject to additional review

\- Simultaneous ordering of multiple interventions may require added
review or peer discussion

\## Indication-Specific Limitations

\### Pulmonary nodules

\- Benign calcification patterns such as granulomas and popcorn
calcifications do not require routine follow-up

\- Low-risk solid nodules smaller than 6 mm generally do not require
routine follow-up

\- Stable nodules with 24 months documented stability do not require
follow-up

\- Guidance for age 35 and older applies to asymptomatic patients who:

\- Are not immunocompromised

\- Do not have cancer

\- Are not in a lung cancer screening program

\### Lymphadenopathy

\- Enlarged palpable chest wall nodes can often be evaluated with
ultrasound and biopsy instead of advanced imaging

\### Pleural fluid collection

\- Ultrasound should be considered first and before pleural tap

\### Breast implant rupture

\- MRI medical necessity is limited to symptomatic silicone breast
implants

\- MRI is not recommended for saline implant rupture evaluation

\- Routine screening of asymptomatic implants is not established here as
a medical necessity standard

\### Hoarseness/dysphonia

\- CT or MRI should not be obtained before laryngeal examination for a
primary complaint of hoarseness

\### Chronic cough

\- Advanced imaging should not be first-line for common causes of
chronic cough

\- Common causes should be evaluated first

\### Weight loss

\- CT is not intended as broad screening in unexplained weight loss
without proper preliminary evaluation

\- Diagnostic testing should be directed by clinical findings

\### Pulmonary embolism

\- Not addressed in this document; see vascular imaging criteria

\### Breast mass / gynecomastia / documented malignancy

\- These are handled under separate oncology-related guidance

\### Thoracic outlet syndrome

\- Vascular thoracic outlet syndrome requires vascular imaging pathways

\# 5. Practical Summary

Advanced chest imaging is most often medically necessary when:

\- Initial evaluation or basic imaging is nondiagnostic

\- Symptoms persist despite appropriate workup/treatment

\- There is concern for malignancy, structural abnormality, infection
complication, trauma, interstitial disease, pleural disease, or
procedure planning

\- The requested study is likely to alter diagnosis, treatment, biopsy
planning, or follow-up

The most commonly supported modalities are:

\- CT chest

\- MRI chest

\- MRI breast in selected implant cases

\- FDG-PET/CT in selected pulmonary nodule or lymph node scenarios
